“Как нам победить коррупцию?”

Ukraine continues to be one of the countries with the highest level of corruption on the planet. On one hand, it’s good when you can steal and bribe. On the other hand, it’s sad to realize that Ukrainians still remain one of the most foolish nations on the planet, unable to organize the work of their officials and police in a way that prevents them from stealing. How can we become smarter? Are there any proven methods?

According to Robert Klitgaard, known for his research on the nature of this phenomenon, “corruption is greed plus opportunity minus control”. This formula suggests that in order to eradicate corruption, it is necessary to a) reduce officials’ opportunities and b) strengthen control over them. However, it is not possible to reduce greed simply due to human nature - we always want more.

So, the first thing is the reduction of the officials’ powers, and there are many ways to do it. For example, in Georgia during Saakashvili’s time, the “one-stop shop principle” was introduced (also used in other countries) to reduce the number of procedures for obtaining state documents, and consequently reduce the number of bribes they receive.

The second part of the formula requires increased control over officials, which primarily involves tougher punishments and direct punitive actions. For example, Hong Kong is known for its idea of “presumption of guilt” for officials, where each of them is considered a thief until proven otherwise. The experience of Singapore, under the leadership of Lee Kuan Yew, is also illustrative, where anti-corruption legislation was tightened to the extent that even the promise of a bribe was considered a bribe. And China is ahead of everyone else - over the past ten years, more than 10,000 officials have been executed there.

Analyzing the situation in Ukraine, several years ago Saakashvili stated that the current state system is one that “does not allow for not stealing.” The formulation remarkably accurately combines all three components of the formula described above: 1) officials’ salaries are meager, 2) there are ample opportunities to steal, and 3) there is little oversight. Only the lazy and foolish would not steal.

Everything seems logical in this formula, and the solution is obvious both for Ukraine and any other country: 1) increase the salaries of officials, 2) reduce their number and simplify the procedures they control, and finally 3) regularly shoot violators. However, there is another important aspect that many people pay attention to - people’s attitude towards corruption. Those people who are the source of existence for officials and from whose pockets they steal.

Kakha Bendukidze, drawing analogies between cargo cults of Papua New Guinea and the consciousness of modern residents of post-Soviet territories, said that “if we want to be mature societies, we need not to receive our rules of life as manna from heaven, but to achieve them ourselves,” and then added that “to fight corruption, we need to want it strongly.” The main idea is that only we can achieve new rules of life without corruption if we want and strive for it.

Vitaliy Portnykov, in his analysis of the issue of combating corruption in Ukraine, writes that the success of this fight “may not be connected to government measures, repression, or punishments, but rather to public morality”, referring to the urgent need for changes in our consciousness before any anti-corruption measures can have an effect.

In my deep conviction, the first step in this fight should be a change in our attitude towards the wealth of officials. More often than not, it arouses envy in us, when it should arouse anger. We should understand that their expensive cars are primarily a sign of our own foolishness. They were stolen from us, and they are driving on our own streets, and we just watch and do nothing.

Moreover, we need to change our attitude towards officials in general. It is important to understand that they are our service staff, living off our money. We are forced to keep them around to organize our lives and, bluntly speaking, take out the trash and clean the roads. The head of the regional tax office or the deputy head of customs should be much less significant to us than, for example, a chef or a supermarket director.

The new rules, which Kaha Bendukidze spoke about, will consist precisely in such a revolution of creation: the reverent and timid attitude towards officials will be replaced by a calm and utilitarian one. After this, all other changes will occur naturally, and corruption will transform from a universal disaster into a focal problem, as in developed countries.
