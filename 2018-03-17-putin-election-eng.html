“Что сделал Путин, чтобы завтра победить”

Tomorrow in Russia, there will be presidential elections, and Vladimir Putin will win. This will be his fourth presidential term. In fact, he has been in power for 18 years, since the moment of his seizure of power in January 2000. Almost 150 million people in the geographically largest country in the world will once again entrust this person with the mandate to govern their destiny. It is hard to disagree that this is a respectable result. How does he do it?

The main factor of Putin’s success in politics is his sober and adequate attitude towards the human material he has to deal with. When Feuchtwanger made a remark about the tasteless and exaggerated reverence for Stalin’s personality during their conversation, Stalin, according to him, “apologized for his peasants and workers, saying that they were too busy with other matters and couldn’t develop good taste in themselves.” Apparently, a similar sober cynicism is inherent in Putin, which is manifested in his style of governing his people.

Here are the main methods that bring such results:

Autocracy. Russia is Putin, and Putin is Russia. It is difficult to say exactly how decisions are made behind the scenes of power, who among the advisors holds what weight, and how power is truly distributed among them. For an ordinary citizen, it creates the feeling that the country is governed by a single person. It is not so important what people’s opinion of this person is, whether it is love or hatred. What is important is that the opinion is focused. This allows for maintaining control over the situation.

Cult of Personality. It is difficult to create a cult of personality for a ruler in six years, but it can and should be done in almost twenty years. Having a cult makes governance easier because it allows for reliance on authority. The average citizen is generally not inclined to think deeply or critically. Complex logical constructions are difficult and tiresome for them. They need clear answers to pressing questions from those in power. The logical conclusion - “because it’s Putin” - easily and confidently puts an end to most of these questions.

Openness. Annual press conferences with journalists, open lessons with schoolchildren, visits to factories, interviews with foreign journalists - all of this creates an image of a person who is open and accessible. What he specifically says - and he speaks quite well - is not so important. It is important to see him openly interacting with people. This creates a sense of “familiarity” and “kinship”. When we meet a famous actor on the street, we smile and nod, not receiving any reaction in return, which surprises and confuses us - we are used to considering this person as our close acquaintance. The same effect occurs with the publicity of a politician - Putin has long become familiar to millions of Russians.

Aggressiveness. Even before becoming president, in 1999 Putin promised to “wipe out terrorists in the outhouse,” which, despite the rudeness of the wording, is pleasant to hear for the average voter. In such a formulation, there is a clear formula: we are against them. Who exactly are these “they” that we will “wipe out” doesn’t really matter. What matters is that there is a “we” who will not allow themselves to be offended. Throughout Putin’s years in power, Russia had plenty of enemies, and if there weren’t enough, they were created by blowing up buildings (read). It is impossible to rally millions of people around the idea of peace. Hatred and war are needed for unity. Whether it’s Chechnya, Georgia, Transnistria, Syria, Donbass, or all of NATO as a whole. The population wants to know where the enemy is. The leader’s task is to find or create this enemy. Putin successfully copes with this.

Religiosity. It is hard to believe that a former member of the CPSU and a KGB lieutenant colonel became a believer. However, understanding the darkness of his people, he is doing the right thing by associating himself with the Russian Orthodox Church and its top figures. The idea of a tsar as God’s anointed one is close to many Russians, who form the majority of the actively voting electorate. Religion, as Marx believed, was the opium of the people, and it will always remain so. It is difficult and tiring to believe in oneself, in logic, in common sense, and in cause-and-effect relationships. Simple formulas are needed that allow one to shift responsibility for their mistakes onto someone else. Christ is just as suitable for this as Putin.

Power. Machiavelli also believed that the only way to control the masses is through fear. Neither love, nor respect, nor common sense, nor even money can stop the masses when they get out of control. By granting almost unlimited authority to the security structures, particularly the FSB, Putin simplifies and crystallizes the image of power for his electorate - everyone understands who exactly to fear, who holds the power, where it comes from, and whom to worship. The negative consequences for the country are outweighed by the positive ones, considering the kind of human material Putin is dealing with.

Ruthlessness. Regular arrests and cruel punishments of people from their immediate surroundings create an image of a just ruler. It doesn’t matter what exactly the next Ulyukaev is guilty of, what matters is how strict and inevitable the punishment is. The people fear the oprichniki, and the oprichniki fear the tsar - this formula has been working for hundreds of years. The people believe that the tsar knows nothing about lawlessness and corruption. The people want to see the tsar punishing the arrogant murderers. The people write letters to the tsar and rely on his justice. This format of communication with the authorities is convenient and understandable to the people.

Hypocrisy. The theatrical opposition, led by the unwavering Zhirinovsky, vividly demonstrates that Putin knows how to play a double game and negotiate with his opponents. He doesn’t “dispose of everyone in the toilets” like many dictators do. He pays some, like Kadyrov, shares the stage and glory with others, like Sobchak and Navalny, and simply kills some—each case requires its own individual decision. Questions of honor, conscience, and human dignity are completely ignored, as a competent leader of the masses should. People don’t care about the character of their leader; they care about their personal comfort. A leader who cares about the comfort of the people, rather than the purity of his conscience, will be respected and re-elected.

Confidence. Refusing to acknowledge one’s mistakes is a repelling quality in an ordinary person. However, in a prominent leader, it becomes a virtue. The people are unable to understand the reasons behind the mistakes and the conclusions drawn. A mistake is simply a mistake. According to Machiavelli, a leader who makes mistakes and even admits to them is a weak leader. Putin never criticizes himself or admits to his mistakes, while continuing to make promises without fulfilling them and even lying. From a purely human perspective, this outrages us, but on a scale of millions, it is a quality of an effective leader.

Patriotism. Wisdom, determination, energy, and decisiveness are important qualities of a leader. However, patriotism is more important. The people, while they may sometimes forgive mistakes, will never forgive disloyalty. Constantly strained, and often openly hostile, relations with other countries and their leaders are a clear message from Putin to his citizens: “I am with you, not with them.” International sanctions and Russia’s isolation on the world stage may have little practical impact on the average citizen, but they vividly illustrate the president’s patriotism through the media.

The conclusion is that Putin is doing everything right, considering the circumstances he is in and the people who choose him. With different people in another country and under different conditions, it would probably be possible to do something differently.
