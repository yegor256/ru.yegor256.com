For the past approximately 400 years, since the time of Locke, we have been living under the banner of freedom, equality, and fraternity. It is now difficult to imagine a reasonable person who seriously denies the importance of personal freedom, the value of human life, the virtue of social equality, the preciousness of peace, friendship, and love. These concepts seem to be the foundation of our morality. They have always been with us and will remain forever. However, I propose to look deeper and understand what freedom really is and whether it is beneficial to us.

In his Two Treatises on Government, the English educator and philosopher John Locke formulated: “freedom is to follow my own will in all cases where the law does not prohibit it.” Armed with this obviously attractive formula and inspired by early humanists, liberal revolutionaries of all kinds have been changing the social order of European countries for years, along with the consciousness of their inhabitants. As a result, according to Valery Kuvakin, a professor at Moscow State University, “the Constitution of the Russian Federation is an expression of victorious liberalism-capitalism in terms of its source and content”, and we are liberals too!

Thus, freedom is possible as long as two “free” individuals do not conflict with each other. “Is internet censorship permissible?” - school students asked the president. “Of course not,” the president replied… and added that “if it is incitement to terrorism, then of course it is permissible.” This was followed by a quote from Bakunin. In other words, as long as your actions do not interfere with others, we are willing to grant you freedom, but as soon as they contradict our expectations, your freedom will be restricted.

The key words here are “conflict” and “contradiction”. Where they are absent, there is freedom, and where they exist, freedom cannot be.

Who will control and punish those who encroach on the freedom of each of us? Roskomnadzor? Moral police? Public court? “Conscience!” - says a prepared libertarian. Each of us must have a moral principle, as Immanuel Kant bequeathed, which will not allow us to encroach on the freedom of others. Initially, of course, as long as we build the society of the future, not everyone will have it. Therefore, we will still need a court and police, acting on the motives of John Stuart Mill’s essay, recommending the use of violence against a person “only when he harms others.” Then, of course, sooner or later, people will learn to live without conflicts and contradictions, and will stop harming each other.

Is it really good when society consists of those who avoid conflicts by not contradicting the interests of others? Can we progress while constantly looking back at those who are hindered by our progress? And in general, can progress, as per Voltaire’s liberal opinion, be possible without conflict - the “basic law of nature”? Can a good teacher be tolerant of the mistakes of underachievers? Can a scientist create something new while fearing to offend colleagues? Can a new product be released while fearing to harm competitors? It sounds absurd.

However, it is precisely to this that liberals lead us under the slogans of freedom. It is precisely to a society without conflicts - national, domestic, economic, cultural, and political - to a society of freedom!

Certainly, none of this works in the end. In a society that has been raised for centuries with the idea of “everyone does what they want as long as they don’t interfere with their neighbor,” people are inherently repulsed by the idea of violence against them. They are born feeling free, and therefore despise both the state and the authorities, who inevitably bring violence into their lives. As a result, such citizens require three times as much police presence as they would if violence had been an integral part of their existence since childhood, along with its derivatives: discipline, obedience, order, and control.

Violence in liberal societies is not only excessive but also hypocritical. Bright slogans about freedom and equality cover up “exploitation and alienation”, as noted by Professor Boris Kashnikov. The cowardly “do no harm to your neighbor” raises weak, indifferent, tolerant, and agreeable people - who are easily exploited.

Everything would be different if a person did not fear causing harm to their neighbor, disturbing their “freedom”, and instead considered conflict as the norm. They would then calmly accept disciplined order, respect subordination, consider any kind of work as the highest virtue, and of course, would not allow alienation and exploitation. This would be a person of a completely different quality: firm, uncompromising, unyielding, and consistent. They would consider obedience as the greatest good. Of course, not for anyone but for “the proletariat - the armed vanguard of all exploited and working people,” as Lenin wrote.

It would be a different society and different people.

Those who for four centuries in a row instill in us the ideas of freedom are our enemies. We should not strive for freedom, but for order and discipline, as natural forms of natural and rational violence. We should not fear violence, but respect and understand it. It is a part of our nature, and we need to face this fact directly. Otherwise, others will do it for us, and we will remain slaves to our tolerance.
