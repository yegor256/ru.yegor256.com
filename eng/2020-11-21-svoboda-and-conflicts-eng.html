“Четыре века якобы свободы”

For approximately the last 400 years, since the time of Locke, we have been living under the banner of freedom, equality, and brotherhood. It is now difficult to imagine a reasonable person who seriously denies the importance of personal freedom, the value of human life, the virtue of social equality, and the preciousness of peace, friendship, and love. These concepts seemed to be the foundation of our morality. They have always been with us and will remain forever. However, I propose to look deeper and understand what freedom really is and whether it is truly beneficial to us.

In his Two Treatises on Government, the English educator and philosopher John Locke formulated: “freedom is to follow my own will in all cases where the law does not prohibit it.” Armed with this obviously attractive formula and inspired by early humanists, liberal revolutionaries of all kinds have been changing the social fabric of European countries for all these years, along with the consciousness of their inhabitants. As a result, according to Professor Valery Kuvakin from Moscow State University, “the Constitution of the Russian Federation is an expression of victorious liberalism-capitalism in terms of its source and content,” and we are liberals too!

Thus, freedom is possible as long as two “free” individuals do not conflict with each other. “Is internet censorship permissible?” - asked the students to the president. “Certainly not,” - the president replied… and added that - “if it involves calls for terrorism, then of course it is permissible.” This was followed by a quote from Bakunin. In simpler terms, as long as your actions do not hinder others, we are willing to grant you freedom, but once they contradict our expectations, your freedom will be restricted.

The key words here are “conflict” and “contradiction”. Where they are absent, there is freedom, and where they exist, freedom cannot be.

Who will control and punish those who encroach on the freedom of each of us? Roskomnadzor? Moral police? Public court? “Conscience!” - says a prepared libertarian. Each of us must have a moral principle, as Immanuel Kant bequeathed, which will not allow us to encroach on the freedom of others. At first, of course, while we are building the society of the future, not everyone will have it. Therefore, we will still need both a court and a police force, acting based on the motives of John Stuart Mill’s essay, which recommends the use of violence against a person “only when he harms others.” Then, of course, sooner or later, people will learn to live without conflicts and contradictions, and will stop harming each other.

Is it really good when society consists of those who avoid conflicts by trying not to contradict the interests of others? Can we develop while constantly looking back at those who hinder our progress? And overall, is progress possible - the “basic law of nature” according to the liberal Voltaire - without conflict? Can a good teacher be tolerant of the mistakes of underachievers? Can a scientist create something new while fearing to offend colleagues? Can a new product be released while fearing to harm competitors? It sounds absurd.

However, it is precisely to this that liberals lead us under the slogans of freedom. It is to a society without conflicts - national, domestic, economic, cultural, and political - to a society of freedom!

Certainly, none of this works in the end. In a society that has been raised for centuries in the spirit of “everyone does whatever they want, as long as it doesn’t bother their neighbor,” people are inherently opposed to the idea of violence against them. They are born thinking they are free and therefore despise both the state and the authorities, which inevitably bring violence into their lives. As a result, three times as many police officers are needed for such citizens compared to if violence had been an integral part of their existence from childhood, along with its derivatives: discipline, obedience, order, and control.

Violence in liberal societies is not only excessive but also hypocritical. Bright slogans about freedom and equality hide “exploitation and alienation,” as correctly noted by Professor Boris Kashnikov. The cowardly “do no harm to your neighbor” fosters weak, indifferent, tolerant, and acquiescent individuals - those who are easily exploited.

Everything would be different if a person did not fear causing harm to their neighbor, disturbing their “freedom”, and considered conflict as the norm - they would then calmly accept disciplined social order, respect subordination, consider any kind of work as the highest virtue, and of course, would not allow alienation and exploitation. This would be a person of a completely different quality: firm, uncompromising, unforgiving, and consistent. They would consider obedience as the highest good. Of course, not for anyone, but for the “proletariat - the armed vanguard of all exploited and working people,” as Lenin wrote.

It would be a different society and different people.

Those who have been imposing ideas of freedom on us for four centuries in a row are our enemies. We should not strive for freedom, but for order and discipline, as natural forms of natural and reasonable violence. We should not be afraid of violence, but respect and understand it. It is a part of our nature, and we need to face this fact directly. Otherwise, others will do it for us, and we will remain slaves to our tolerance.
