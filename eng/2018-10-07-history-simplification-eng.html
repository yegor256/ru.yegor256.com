This is how he envisioned the future from the difficult year of 1943. However, everything turned out to be the opposite: we ended up with much more dirt than he expected from his closest followers, and the winds of history are yet to be heard.

After studying our history, apparently through books and blogs, he told me that Stalin killed 30 million Ukrainians, and Putin took Crimea away from Ukraine. In connection with this, he was wondering how it is possible that we tolerate all of this? Why don’t we take up arms and fight for our freedom? As an American with an innate love for freedom, he finds this incomprehensible.

I asked him how Stalin managed to kill so many people if he had never been to Ukraine. I even joked if he did it with the help of some super-accurate long-range rifle. After that, the conversation took a completely different turn, and I was accused of not sharing the grief and sadness for the innocently killed, and therefore there was nothing to talk to me about.

However, I ask myself, is it really necessary for us to categorically simplify negative historical experiences in order to draw correct conclusions from them? Is it necessary for one of its leaders to be declared a messenger of Satan in order for us to avoid repeating genocide, and for all the deceased to be hailed as great martyrs? And won’t such simplification lead to the exact opposite result, namely to new senseless victims and new unjustified violence?

Did Stalin kill Ukrainians? Did Putin annex Crimea? Did Hitler burn Jews? And did Bin Laden blow up the towers in New York?

I don’t know. There are different opinions on this matter.

However, I know for sure that the more convinced we are that we know for certain who the villain is and who the savior is, the higher our chances of finding a new anti-hero. And very soon. The more actively we search for the guilty and the more eager we are to blame them for what happened, the less responsibility we leave on ourselves.

Remaining unable to take responsibility, we inevitably become hostages to someone else’s will. And most likely, this will is directed against us. New genocides, famines, repressions, and wars await us if we do not stop simplifying history and demonizing its individual figures.

In other words, we did this together. And our ancestors too.

For a better future, one should not be afraid to soberly look into the past and acknowledge their personal guilt in what has happened. The goal should not be to simplify what has occurred, but rather to see all aspects of past tragedies. We need to strive to become the very wind of history that the commander-in-chief relied on.
