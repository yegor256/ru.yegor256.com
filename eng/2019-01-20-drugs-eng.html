Yesterday I finished watching the TV series El Chapo (2017-2018), which tells the story of the ongoing life of one of the most successful drug lords of our time, Joaquin Guzman. The first season turned out to be the strongest, the second was weaker, and I was only able to endure the third season thanks to the storyline involving Conrado. But right now, we’re not talking about cinematography, but rather about drugs.

Throughout the entire movie illustrating the last 20 years of modern history, the corrupt government of Mexico fights against the number one enemy of the state. In the showdown with Joaquin, the less corrupt but equally treacherous and two-faced DEA (Drug Enforcement Administration) also takes an active role. In the end, our hero, with whom we have become friends during the course of the movie, is caught, extradited to the USA, and handed over to the hands of a fair American court. Justice prevails, and… American teenagers stop using methamphetamine!

The creators of the film do not tell us anything about this, even in the final credits, where it is solemnly announced that Joaquin has killed between two to three thousand people throughout his professional career. They do not mention it, probably because teenagers are not concerned about the arrest of El Chapo, and finding where to buy methamphetamine does not hinder them at all. Statistics show that despite the active fight against drug trafficking, drug consumption is increasing, and the mortality rate from drug overdose is twice as high in the United States compared to any other developed country, such as Europe.

I do not have precise statistics on the situation in Ukraine, but based on the graffiti on the walls of houses in any major city, one can judge the situation in the market: drugs are in high demand. Regular arrests of drug dealers do not at all hinder buyers from finding the goods they need… from new drug dealers.

Want to know my opinion? I consider punishing producers to be hypocrisy. After all, many of those who caught El Chapo, extradited him to New York, and will soon be judging and lynching him themselves are his clients. They, as representatives of the whole country, openly express only half of the public interests. However, in reality, society wants two things simultaneously: 1) narcotic euphoria and 2) to punish those who sell it.

The second interest is expressed openly and verbally, while the first is secret and involves money: the volume of consumption of illicit drugs in the United States alone exceeds $100 billion per year. We vote with our dollars and hryvnias for drug production, while openly persecuting those who produce them. The number of people who use drugs at least once a year is approximately 275 million, which is roughly 5.6% of the world population aged 15 to 64.

Парадокс.

If we truly want El Chapo to grow cotton instead of cocaine and synthesize gouache instead of fentanyl, let’s all together… stop using drugs. There’s no need to shoot, kill, arrest, prosecute, and spend three billion dollars a year on it (DEA budget). It’s enough to simply stop buying what this brave Mexican man produces and sells. I’m sure he would quickly redirect his energy towards a socially beneficial business.

On the contrary, by actively fighting against sellers like him, we only expose our own weakness and foolishness: nothing can stop us from indulging in sweet treats at night. So, should we imprison all confectioners? But what do confectioners have to do with it? Are they to blame for our being overweight, lazy, and lacking willpower? They merely exploit our shortcomings, but they are not the root cause. Drug dealers are the confectioners, and we, you and I, are the lazy and overweight ones.

What to do if the problem is not with the manufacturers, but with us?

How to reduce the demand for drugs? How to convince the population that addiction is evil? Maybe intimidation is necessary? Or maybe lifting the prohibitions, and the interest will decrease on its own?

On the first path of intimidation, countries such as Iran, Saudi Arabia, and China impose the death penalty, including for drug possession. In many other countries, punishments are less severe but still harsh, including in Ukraine and Russia. Statistics undoubtedly show that police measures have a positive effect.

Along the second path are the countries of Western Europe. For example, in the Netherlands or Portugal, possession of small quantities of drugs is not considered a criminal offense, although it does entail certain measures of social intervention. And once again, statistics show that legalization brings positive results.

I don’t believe in either the first or the second way.

It’s not about intimidation or permissions, but about our needs. Drugs are needed where the needs for joy, happiness, success, self-realization, and everything else that Maslow described in his famous pyramid are not sufficiently satisfied. In one of his recent novels, Vladimir Sorokin vividly depicts a future world where Telluria becomes the richest country thanks to the invention of a unique drug, the effect of which surpasses all others. The whole world depends on Telluria because it knows how to make people happy and sells “nails made of tellurium” at a very high price.

The movie Traffic (2000) effectively illustrates the problem: the fight against drug trafficking turns into a battle with one’s own family for one of its leaders. The true enemy turns out to be not the heroin dealer, but his consumer - the own daughter of one of the main characters, who realizes that even if he defeats the producer, he will not be able to defeat the main buyer - his daughter will always find a way to buy a new dose of “happiness”.

Olga Bogomolets believes that “today we need not to legalize drugs, but to expand the list of banned substances and ensure effective control over their circulation and [to] carry out a systematic fight against drug traffickers.” No, Olga, it’s not about legalization or fighting drug trafficking — that won’t help the cause.

The matter is about improving the standard of living of the Ukrainian population and satisfying their needs in education, leisure, art, and everything that can replace drugs. It is necessary to provide people with something that will excite them as much as cocaine. Obviously, it is difficult to do so, but it is necessary. This is where state funds and our joint efforts should be invested.

Fighting against drug trafficking and drug addicts is like fighting against confectioners. In the end, we will still be overweight, and Telluria will be in control of us.

Или, может быть, уже управляет?
