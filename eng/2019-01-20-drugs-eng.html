“Кого бы нам еще наказать за наркотики?”

I finished watching the TV series El Chapo (2017-2018) yesterday, which tells the story of the ongoing life of one of the most successful drug lords of our time named Joaquin Guzman. The first season turned out to be the strongest, the second weaker, and I was only able to digest the third season thanks to the storyline of Conrado. But right now, we are not talking about cinema, but about drugs.

Throughout the entire film illustrating the past 20 years of modern history, the corrupt government of Mexico fights against the number one enemy of the state. In the confrontation with Joaquin, the less corrupt but equally treacherous and two-faced DEA (Drug Enforcement Administration) also actively participates. In the end, our hero, whom we become friends with during the course of the film, is caught, extradited to the United States, and handed over to the hands of a fair American court. Justice prevails, and… American teenagers stop using methamphetamine!

The creators of the film do not tell us anything about it, not even in the final credits, where it is solemnly announced that Joaquin has killed between two and three thousand people throughout his professional career. They probably don’t mention it because teenagers are not particularly concerned about the arrest of El Chapo, and finding where to buy methamphetamine doesn’t seem to bother them at all. Statistics show that despite the active fight against drug trafficking, drug consumption is increasing, and the mortality rate from drug overdoses is twice as high in the United States as in any other developed country, such as Europe.

I don’t have precise statistics on the situation in Ukraine, but based on the graffiti on the walls of houses in any major city, one can judge the state of the market: drugs are in high demand. Regular arrests of drug dealers do not at all hinder buyers from finding the goods they need… from new drug dealers.

Do you want to know my opinion? I consider punishing the producers to be hypocritical. After all, many of those who caught El Chapo, extradited him to New York, and will soon judge and lynch him, are his clients themselves. They, as representatives of the whole country, openly express only half of the public interests. However, in reality, society wants two things at the same time: 1) drug-induced euphoria and 2) to punish those who sell it.

The second interest is openly and verbally expressed, while the first one is secretive and involves money: the consumption of illicit drugs in the United States alone exceeds 100 billion dollars per year. We vote with our dollars and hryvnias for drug production, while openly persecuting those who produce them. The number of people who use drugs at least once a year is approximately 275 million people, which is roughly 5.6% of the world population aged 15 to 64 years old.

Парадокс.

If we truly want El Chapo to grow cotton instead of cocaine and synthesize gouache instead of fentanyl, let’s all together… stop using drugs. There’s no need to shoot, kill, arrest, prosecute, and spend three billion dollars a year on it (DEA budget). It’s enough to simply stop buying what this brave Mexican man produces and sells. I’m sure he would quickly redirect his energy towards a socially beneficial business.

On the contrary, by actively fighting against such sellers, we acknowledge our own weakness and foolishness: nothing can stop us from indulging in sweet treats at night. So, should we imprison all the confectioners? What do confectioners have to do with it? Are they to blame for our being overweight, lazy, and weak-willed? They merely exploit our shortcomings but are not the root cause. Drug dealers are the confectioners, and we are the lazy and overweight ones.

What to do if the problem is not with the manufacturers, but with us?

How to reduce drug demand? How to convince the population that addiction is evil? Maybe it is necessary to intimidate? Or maybe lift the bans, and the interest will decrease on its own?

Countries like Iran, Saudi Arabia, and China follow the path of intimidation, where the death penalty is imposed, including for drug possession. In many other countries, punishments are less severe but still harsh, including Ukraine and Russia. Statistics undoubtedly show that law enforcement measures have a positive effect.

The second path is taken by the countries of Western Europe. For example, in the Netherlands or Portugal, possession of small amounts of drugs is not considered a criminal offense, although it does entail certain measures of social impact. And again, statistics show that legalization brings positive results.

I don’t believe in either the first or the second way.

It’s not about intimidation or permissions, but about our needs. Drugs are needed where the needs for joy, happiness, success, self-realization, and everything else that Maslow described in his famous pyramid are not sufficiently satisfied. In one of his recent novels, Vladimir Sorokin vividly depicts a future world where Telluria becomes the wealthiest country thanks to the invention of a unique drug whose effect far surpasses all others. The whole world depends on Telluria because it knows how to make people happy and sells “nails made of tellurium” at a very high price.

The film Traffic (2000) effectively highlights the issue: the fight against drug trafficking becomes a battle with one’s own family for one of its leaders. The true enemy turns out to be not the heroin dealer, but his consumer - the daughter of one of the main characters, who realizes that even if he defeats the producer, he won’t be able to defeat the main buyer - his daughter will always find a way to buy a new dose of “happiness”.

The case is about improving the standard of living of the Ukrainian population and meeting their needs in education, leisure, art, and everything that can replace drugs. It is necessary to give people something that will excite them as much as cocaine. Obviously, it is difficult to do so, but it is necessary. This is where state funds and our joint efforts should be invested.

Fighting against drug trafficking and drug addicts is like fighting against confectioners. In the end, we will still be overweight and controlled by Telluria.
