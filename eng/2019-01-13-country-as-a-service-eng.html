Historically, states were needed, among other things, for more efficient warfare. The larger the group, the easier it was for them to defeat smaller aggressors, and the easier it was to conquer adjacent territories. In order to conduct warfare more effectively, technologies, science, education, culture, medicine, and much more were needed. However, all of this could be done without establishing states and imposing rigid borders between them. Countries, flags, anthems, borders, and national interests are necessary where there is war. And war exists everywhere and always.

However, the world is changing and technologies are advancing. First and foremost, communication and labor automation technologies. Thanks to the internet and robots, physical presence in the place of creating value-added products is becoming less important for an increasing number of professions. Programmers, of course, are at the forefront. Just twenty years ago, it was crucial for them to be close to the product they were creating because the product was located on expensive servers, and mere phone or email communication was not enough for efficient work. Now servers have moved to the “cloud,” and communication has become no less, and often even more efficient through video conferences or online chats.

Architects, designers, planners, musicians, bloggers, writers, journalists, artists, managers, marketers, copywriters, producers, investors, analysts, and many other professions that are not tied to a specific location and do not require manual labor, now often have the freedom to choose their place of residence and even country. Why live where you were born if you can live somewhere warmer? Why pay high income tax in France when you can pay low or zero tax in Dubai? Why rent an apartment in San Francisco for $5,000 when you can write the same articles for the California Weekly while renting a bungalow in Bali for $500?

One can broaden the question further, as Pavel Cherkashin did in his recent article “The homeland-as-a-service model: How blockchain will disrupt the world order” (Russian version here): why not turn the state into a service and its citizens into clients? Each individual would be free to decide in which state they wish to reside, choosing from all the available options in the market.

Suppose Belgium offers quality healthcare but has high taxes. In Brazil, taxes are lower but the crime situation is tense. In the USA, taxes are acceptable and the crime rate is not as high, but public transportation and roads are of low quality. And so on. Each country will have something to offer its customers. It’s like offices for rent or trendy coworking spaces - each has its own conditions, price, pros, and cons.

Pavel Cherkashin predicts in his article that very soon “countries will have to compete for the most qualified citizens by providing the best services and advantages such as a strong economy, low taxes, a fair legal system, and reliable social security programs”.

Sounds beautiful! Isn’t it a utopia?

The authors of such ideas about free migration, carried away, forget about the primary purpose of states. As mentioned above, they are needed for conducting aggressive and liberating wars among people. Despite the fact that a small percentage of the inhabitants of this planet enjoy studying new technologies and writing sensible articles about them (including myself), the overwhelming majority struggle for survival in a semi-military or military regime.

The dynamics of social inequality, according to a recent report by the World Inequality Lab, are disheartening. The gap between the poor and the rich is widening: “the share of national income held by the top 10% of earners is 37% in Europe, 41% in China, 46% in Russia, 47% in the United States and Canada, and around 55% in sub-Saharan Africa, Brazil, and India, and 61% in the Middle East.”

Will all these people who live on a few dollars a month be offered a choice of the country to live in? They have already chosen. The United States. And now, specifically for them, Donald Trump is building a wall. So that they cannot fulfill their choice.

The United States, like most Western European countries, is in a state of escalating semi-military conflict with countries where there is less money but no shortage of desire to live well. The flow of immigration from the Middle East to Europe is rapidly increasing. This is not yet a third world war, but it is also not a time when one can talk about visa cancellation, passport abolition, border opening, and immigration control abolition.

States are still necessary and still serve military purposes.

Wars have only changed their form but have not disappeared.

Perhaps one can talk about the “state as a service,” but with one important caveat: it will be a very expensive service, and only for a select few. However, such a service already exists in the market, without any blockchains. Want to live and work in the United States? The EB-5 visa for $1,000,000 is always available. Want to live in Zurich? 300,000 francs a year, and welcome. And so on. The borders have long been open. But only for the wealthy.

Will they ever be open to everyone? I don’t think so. Will a child born in the slums of Bombay ever be able to turn 18, decide that it’s better to be a citizen of the United States, live in Miami, work as a programmer there, and pay taxes there - press a few buttons and get an American passport?
