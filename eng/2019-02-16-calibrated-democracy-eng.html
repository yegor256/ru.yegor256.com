Democracy is the worst form of government. Power belongs to the crowd, which is incapable of strategic thinking and is mostly composed of ignorant individuals making hasty decisions. This was believed by Aristotle in the 2nd century BCE. He considered a more effective system of government to be one in which power belongs to those who are inherently more authoritative, and therefore capable of making more balanced and knowledgeable decisions.

Contrary to the thoughts of the Greek philosopher, currently democracy is the prevailing form of government in most countries around the world. Even in countries where monarchy is preserved, such as the United Kingdom or Spain, the actual governance of the country is carried out by the cabinet of ministers, and laws are passed by an elected parliament. On paper, it is the people who govern all countries in the world, including Cuba, North Korea, and China, through their representatives.

For example, Article 71 of the Constitution of Ukraine states that “elections to state authorities and local self-government bodies are free and conducted on the basis of universal, equal, and direct suffrage through secret ballot.”

Aristotle was specifically against this equality, as were much later Vilfredo Pareto, Gaetano Mosca, and many other supporters of elitism who believed that people are inherently unequal, both physically and intellectually. And, more importantly, weak and foolish individuals outnumber strong and intelligent ones in any society. Thus, by endowing everyone with equal rights and giving a “green light” to decisions made by the simple majority, we inevitably create conditions for the degradation of the entire community. The most rational and strategically balanced decisions will always be in the minority along with their authors, simply due to the inherent inequality of human abilities. In other words, fools will always shout down the wise.

To solve the problem, it is logical to either a) eliminate fools completely, b) deprive them of the right to vote, or c) make them think they have the right to vote when in fact they don’t. The Nazis attempted to go down the first path but were stopped in time. The second path leads to massive revolutions, as seen in the year 1917. Currently, all developed countries, led by the United States, are following the third path - creating an illusion of democracy where every citizen has equal voting rights, but the actual governance of the country is carried out regardless of the majority’s choice.

In other words, modern democracy is a qualitative spectacle enacted by the minority for the majority in order to calm them down and instill in them a sense of universal equality. Notice once again, all of this is primarily done in the interests of the majority.

Может быть есть четвертый путь?

Есть.

What if equal voting rights were replaced with unequal ones? What if each citizen didn’t have just one vote, which they “cast” into the ballot box every few years, but several votes, depending on their value to society, contribution to its development, and authority? Let’s say an economics professor would have 50 votes, while a hair stylist would only have two.

Of course, the first immediate question that arises is: who and how will decide how many votes each person has, and why does a professor have more votes than a hairdresser? In this way, citizens’ voices will be calibrated based on their authority in matters of state governance. It is even possible to consider the option of regular qualification testing for every citizen who wishes to increase their rating. The higher the expected rating, the more professional and complex the questions will be, specifically in the fields of economy, law, or state history. By using decentralized data storage and processing technologies, it is possible to completely eliminate bias in assessment and any form of corruption.

Such a “calibrated” democracy will solve two problems at once. Firstly, it will bring to power those who deserve it and whose opinions we all truly should rely on. Secondly, it will explain to everyone else what exactly they lack in order to also become those whose opinions the whole country listens to. Moreover, this will be done openly, objectively, and fairly, minimizing the eternal conflict between the rich and the poor, the intelligent and the foolish, the lower and the upper classes of any society.

Most likely, this model will not lead to a change of people in power. However, it will make the electoral formula significantly more understandable and fair. After all, these qualities are exactly what modern democracy lacks.
