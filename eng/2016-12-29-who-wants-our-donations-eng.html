Yesterday, I spoke at a small programming conference and witnessed the organizers collecting 50 hryvnias to help children with cancer. It’s a good cause, so I donated 100 hryvnias. Afterwards, I asked why we are forced to raise money. Why doesn’t the government, to which we pay taxes specifically for this purpose, cover the cost of children’s treatment? The answer was obvious - everyone there is stealing, and the children receive nothing. Our only hope lies with kind-hearted people. Let’s examine whether our actions truly reflect genuine kindness.

Healthcare in Ukraine is indeed free. In the recently adopted budget for 2017, the Ministry of Health will receive 62 billion hryvnias (8.6% of the budget and 2.7% of GDP). At the current exchange rate of the dollar to the hryvnia, this is $2.4 billion. Ukraine has a population of 42 million people. This means that $57 will be spent per person in 2017.

Apparently, with this money, Ukraine will continue to successfully maintain its first place in the world in terms of population natural decline and still not enter the top 50 countries in the healthcare efficiency rating.

Let’s compare with Singapore, which ranks second in this rating. Singapore spends 4.9% of its GDP on healthcare, which amounts to $2752 per Singaporean. This is 48 times more than our government plans to spend in 2017 on one Ukrainian.

Сколько в других странах?

In this table, it can be seen that different countries spend different shares of their GDP on healthcare for their citizens: from “negligible” 2% in Qatar to 13% in the Netherlands and a whopping 17% in the United States. However, healthcare is functioning well in these three countries. This means that the issue lies not in the percentage, but apparently in the absolute dollar amount per citizen: in Ukraine, it is $57, while in Qatar it is $572, in Singapore it is $2572, in South Korea it is $2060, in Japan it is $3703, in Israel it is $2910, and in Switzerland it is over $6100.

However, these are all developed countries. Let’s look at the numbers for developing countries: $23 in Pakistan, $34 in Kenya, $36 in Cambodia, $40 in India, $58 in Vietnam, and so on. It is evident that Ukraine currently occupies an honorable place among the poorest countries in the world, surpassing them all in terms of population decline. In other words, things are going very, very poorly.

Let’s now return to the issue of charity. It is obvious that in such a situation in the country, created consistently over the course of twenty years by the presidents and governments we elected, there is no hope for any adequate treatment for children with cancer.

Certainly, they want to help. However, think about the fact that by helping them, we are indirectly helping criminals maintain their power. Instead of highlighting the problem for its ultimate resolution, which would lead to transformative changes in society and Ukraine’s removal from the list of poorest countries, we cowardly and hypocritically conceal the facts by helping children from our own pockets.

Children receive treatment thanks to our donations, while the degradation of the state apparatus continues.

Therefore, if you truly care about the fate of sick children, don’t just donate money for their treatment, but do something more radical that leads to real changes. By donating, you are only appeasing your conscience while helping corrupt officials, the main enemies of sick children, to remain in power.
