I remember when I was four years old. There was a New Year’s performance in kindergarten. Of course, with the magical Santa Claus and his equally magical Snow Maiden. The story was a story, but after the celebration, I tried to explain to my parents that Santa Claus was actually our janitor Ivan Ilyich. Unfortunately, I didn’t have direct evidence at that time, but there were several indirect clues: the voice, eyes, and shoes of the fictional character were very similar to Uncle Vanya’s attributes. I was very upset by my parents’ unwillingness to listen to my arguments. They convinced me that Santa Claus was real and that the janitor, who for some reason was not at the performance, simply fell ill that day.

Many years passed, and I learned that my childhood desire to find an alternative explanation to what was happening, contrary to the widely accepted version, is called a conspiracy theory. Like adult conspiracy theorists, I didn’t have indisputable evidence at that time, and I couldn’t uncover the conspiracy. Santa Claus was exposed later when I became a full member of the closed group “adults,” where children are not allowed for their own good. In the end, I learned the most important thing - being a conspiracy theorist is dangerous and not beneficial. No one will believe you anyway, and you will waste a lot of nerves and energy.

Later, much more serious stories replaced the harmless Grandfather, and it was even harder to believe in them than in the fairytale character, despite all the official sources insisting on it. For example, there is the story of how two American astronauts were stranded on the Moon in 1969, spent twenty-one hours there, then pressed the “Home” button, took off, docked with the spacecraft waiting for them in lunar orbit, turned it around, and sped off towards the frozen Earth, anxiously awaiting them. Do you doubt that it happened exactly like that? You are a conspiracy theorist and a supporter of the moon landing hoax.

Or take, for example, the unfinished story of how the planet was struck by a previously unknown coronavirus, spreading at the speed of the medieval plague and threatening us all with inevitable and agonizing death… from a common cold. As evidence, there are charts showing the virus’s spread, interviews with virologist doctors, and photographs of hospitals being built. Is that not enough for you? Do you believe that the world media is deceiving you, that the numbers are falsified, and that COVID-19 patients do not exist? You are a crazy conspiracy theorist, and it is a waste of time to take your doubts seriously and discuss them.

To be fair, it should be acknowledged that not all conspiracy theories are capable of discovering the janitor’s mustache disguised as Santa Claus. Many of them are unable to even elicit a smile, for example, seriously claiming that the Earth is flat, all watchmakers conspired, and Australia doesn’t exist. However, it is not about them now, nor about who is right: Holocaust deniers or builders of its memorials. I am sure that each of them has their own truth.

In any legend, whether it’s about Santa Claus, Americans on the Moon, or the Chinese virus epidemic, there are interested parties who benefit from the majority of us continuing to believe the legend as truth, while the minority is left with the demeaning status of conspiracy theorists. If we consider our civilization as a closed system, it is logical to assume that as long as the beneficiary is not overthrown and the legend is alive, this situation is beneficial to everyone. In other words, it is better for all of us if the Americans’ journey to the Moon is considered a factual event and the coronavirus epidemic is seen as a real threat.

For example, once we were certain that the Sun revolved around the Earth, and all other versions were considered heresy and conspiracy theories. We lived happily until the time came and the legend and truth switched places. Another example, in the 19th century, Hungarian doctor Ignaz Philipp Semmelweis suggested that the mortality rate among women giving birth could be reduced if hospital staff washed their hands before surgery, for which he was ridiculed and fired from his job (antiseptics and bacteria were discovered much later). Conspiracy theorists and crazy authors of crazy ideas disrupt our ecosystem, and it, in turn, resists. At some point, it gives in, and yesterday’s heresy is recognized as truth, and yesterday’s truth is innocent misunderstanding. Until such a revolution occurs, the system is in a state of relative rest, and the majority of its members (including us) are interested in this.

The viral hysteria unfolding before our eyes, if not authored, involves a number of interested parties. However, its main beneficiary is us, obediently wearing masks and washing our hands for 20 seconds. What is our benefit? There are many, but I like this version. The mass antivirus psychosis will lead to increased hygiene, which in turn will weaken immunity, leading to higher mortality and a reduction in the global population, the importance of which I have already written about. Thus, this relatively inexpensive campaign successfully contributes to solving the global problem of overpopulation. Is it beneficial for us individually? Certainly not. Is it beneficial for civilizations as a whole? Yes.

However, humanity still benefits from having a legend that hundreds of millions of children believe in. Does each of us need a viral epidemic that kills small businesses and slows down the economy? No. However, apparently we all need it together.

What other benefits, in your opinion, can an epidemic bring to civilization as a whole?
