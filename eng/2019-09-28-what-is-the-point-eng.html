Recently, one of the readers of my political writings passionately asked me, why am I doing this? What am I trying to achieve and why do I write? What am I calling for? Whom am I for and with whom? What actions do I expect from my readers? He was also very interested in the question of where my homeland really is: in Moscow or in Kiev? The answer seemed to surprise and disappoint him.

Recently, I read a new book by Eduard Limonov called “Azbuka” - a collection of thoughts on various topics, almost unrelated to each other - about politics, Donbass, Putin, old age, violence, and of course, women. The text is juicy and frank, and the ideas are radical and sound, for example:

However, while I agree with the author on almost everything, I was surprised by their naive confusion:

You, Eduard Veniaminovich, with prison and war behind you, should know how the mass consciousness works. Our consciousness. You provoke thoughts and reasoning, while we wait for conclusions. You question basic truths, which makes us uncomfortable. You offer analysis, while we wait for synthesis. For us, the squareheads, it is very important to know: Do you actually support Putin or not?!

“Мы не можем предоставить вам выводы, директивы или точные алгоритмы. Мы не можем синтезировать однозначность для вас. Мы не можем разделить мир на две части и сказать, под чьи знамена вам встать. Нам жаль, но мы не можем выполнить вашу просьбу.”

You refuse to be dull with us, and we refuse to support you. However, it wasn’t always like this.

Previous generations witnessed an open war between two ideologies. The newspaper “Pravda” told the truth, while the magazine Time lied. However, it depended on who was reading them. It doesn’t matter who was actually right. What matters is that objectively the bipolar agenda gathered the intellectual elite around it. Where else could they gather if the world was polarized? It is certainly interesting to talk and think about it! Limonov’s first books were written while he was in emigration after a conflict with the KGB. According to his own words, it was formulated as, “We will either imprison you or you can leave.”

The vector of social conflict (capitalism versus communism) coincided with the vector of the most progressive thought (freedom or slavery). The best people were either in the academy of sciences or in exile. And in both places there were geniuses, heated debates, and the possibility to find the truth.

However, the ideology of Lenin and Stalin was defeated. Limonov returned to Russia, to a place where the war had already ended. There were no longer two opposing camps, no dissidents, and no KGB – the fire of global revolution had died down. The intellectual elite returned from exile and celebrated victory, while the economy surrendered to the mercy of the victors – we were brought McDonald’s and Casio calculators, in exchange for oil and a hungry, illiterate market of 150 million people. We emerged from the grim interim of the 1990s without a new political agenda. The Soviet Union, once an “evil empire,” had transformed into a handful of underdeveloped colonies that no longer aspired to fight against the yoke of capitalism, and people became weaker and more cowardly. There was no longer a need to search for truth – just work for Uncle Sam and be happy.

However, intellectuals with their eternal protest needed to be directed somewhere. We needed that very bipolarity that we lost when we dismantled Iron Felix. As a result, intentionally or accidentally, a liberal movement emerged, for all that is good and against all that is bad. The “concerned intelligentsia” joined it, as there was nothing else to join (certainly not the bloody regime of VVP!).

However, despite the simplified formula of “down with Putin!” the world is no longer as simple as it was in Limonov’s youth. It is no longer divided into Uncle Sam and the proletariat, and therefore, simply removing Putin and Co. from power is unlikely to solve all the problems as it did in the past, by giving up all the achievements of October at once. I am convinced that liberals, unlike their thousands of followers, understand this, but they cannot do anything about it. In order for others to follow them, they need to shout loudly and clearly: “Down with…” and then a specific name.

It sounds really loud, but there is little common sense in this cry. Therefore, only those who are not used to seeking meaning and … bored passers-by gather under the banners. As a result, three camps are formed instead of two, as before: 1) the authorities, 2) liberals, and 3) the thinking minority. There are no banners over the third camp, and no one is arrested there, and its numbers are constantly decreasing. It is precisely there that you can find people like Limonov - the dying veterans of the great war.

Once, in one of the restaurants in Moscow, I was asked to fill out a form in order to receive a permanent visitor card. The last item on the simple questionnaire was a sarcastic question, “Whose Crimea?” with a blank space for filling in. It took me a few minutes to come up with a concise response, different from the primitive “ours” or “not ours.”

The world has become more complex, and simple formulas like “ours vs. yours” or “freedom vs. slavery” are no longer capable of describing the reality surrounding us. Anyone who wants to present a situation in terms of “for” or “against” is not just a fool, but a harmful individual. Whether they are liberals or Kremlin propagandists.

Why am I writing this blog? To instill in you, my readers, a love for forms with free fields. If we can fill in the free fields, then we will truly have freedom.
