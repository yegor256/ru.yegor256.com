In any penitentiary institution - prison or correctional facility - there is a category of inmates who collaborate with the administration. In the early 20th century, in Soviet labor camps of the Gulag system, they were called “activists”. They helped the administration maintain order and discipline among the prisoners. Needless to say, throughout history, prisoners have regarded such “activists” at the very least with caution, and at most, with hostility.

The reason for such an attitude is obvious. People are in places of confinement against their will, and in Stalinist camps, many of them ended up there completely unjustifiably, solely by the will of a cruel fate. Naturally, the prisoners perceive the camp guards, as well as the entire corrective labor system of the state, as evil that breaks their lives.

Everyone who serves evil, including informants and convoy, is associated with this evil. Activists, on the other hand, who voluntarily switch to the side of the state machine, evoke a special dislike. They not only endure a difficult life behind bars, like their unfortunate comrades, but also assist the “oppressors and tormentors”.

The contribution of these voluntary agents of evil to the “stable” life of camps and prisons is difficult to overestimate. Thanks to the camp activists in Stalin’s Gulag, escape attempts and riots were thwarted, resistance was demoralized, and dissidents were identified and eliminated. With their active participation, the largest machine of enslavement in modern history existed for several decades. And not only that. In Nazi concentration camps, there was also a category of prisoners known as “kapos” who assisted the administration in exchange for improved conditions of confinement.

Such people have existed and will always exist, regardless of the regime of violence.

Interestingly, in most cases, they have good intentions. In essence, their goals are discipline, order, cleanliness, organization, the absence of fights and property damage, which seem to be highly commendable.

When Ukrainian and Belarusian activists became police officers during the Nazi occupation of the Soviet Union, they engaged in punitive actions against partisans among the civilian population, driven by a desire for order. They assisted the existing authorities, under the flag of the swastika, in maintaining order and preventing violations of the law.

The goals of activists and public figures are truly noble, as long as the regime they serve is not exploitative, such as the Soviet and Nazi regimes were, and as the Putin and Poroshenko regimes are now.

Both Russia and Ukraine - both countries and peoples - are on the verge of ruin. The main reason is the illiterate greed of the regimes and rulers they have chosen for the past 25 years. Topping the corruption rankings, poor healthcare, the collapse of culture and education, drug addiction and alcoholism - these are the results of the occupation and looting of the population in these territories over the past two and a half decades.

Like any occupation regime, various “activists” emerge and actively manifest themselves, today in the form of public movements and organizations. These include campaigners against traffic rule violators, environmentalists, charitable children’s homes, funds for the sick and disabled, as well as simply compassionate and conscientious citizens who fervently fight for European values and clean roadsides.

They all have the same noble intentions as the Gulag activists over half a century ago. They strive to bring order to the “concentration camp,” whereas the true goal and task should be the destruction of the “camp” and achieving freedom.

Life in Kyiv is not so drastically different from life in Amsterdam that people drive cars without following the rules, throw garbage outside trash bins, or steal from supermarkets. It is different because corrupt individuals are in power, whose rightful place is in prison.

Not the enemies who park on the sidewalk, refuse to treat a sick child, or leave trash on the roadside. These people act this way because they are “ordinary people”. By dealing with their upbringing or punishment on public principles, we become “camp activists” helping the regime.

We need to change the system, not the people oppressed by it.

A typical slogan of activists is “if you want to change the life in the country, start with yourself.” This is the mentality of a camp follower, ready to embark on the path of correction and collaborate with the administration. This is how those who are afraid of security think, afraid to admit that their rights are being trampled on, afraid to raise their heads and face the truth, afraid to start a fight with those who are living off them, afraid to truly change their lives and find freedom. And it’s not just passive fear, but actively calling on others to follow their example: to focus on “themselves” and not complain.

These people, accomplices of the regime, are our main enemies.

Those who are truly concerned about the future of our countries and their inhabitants are the ones who fight against the real problem - the power of corrupt officials. They fight with the goal of overthrowing and destroying it, rather than serving it by maintaining order on the streets and garbage dumps.
