According to Vakhushti Parcvenia, the formation and existence of different nations is an objective consequence of the process of socialization. In other words, for a person, as a “social being,” it is necessary to feel a sense of belonging to a group, which the person will create even if it doesn’t exist.

Thus, the process of finding in oneself and in one’s environment signs of distinction from “others” and then elevating these signs to the level of national identity is constructive and contributes to the survival of the individual.

However, a natural consequence of the formation of a people and a nation is nationalism - “the measles of mankind,” in the words of Albert Einstein. The meaning of this characterization is that it is a childhood disease that humanity, like a child, must overcome in the early stages of its development, when the key to the success of a tribe, city, or state is the timely physical destruction or enslavement of neighboring tribes, cities, and states. In these conditions, nationalism, or even open chauvinism and xenophobia, will unite the population and help it survive.

Nationalism is necessary and very useful in the presence of an external threat. For example, Ivan Ilyin equated nationalism with the “instinct of national self-preservation” and believed that this instinct is “a correct and justified state.” It is a kind of immunity for a social group, protecting it from destruction or absorption by another larger and more stable group. In simpler terms, it is important for us to instill in ourselves and our children a hatred of foreigners if we want our own children to survive.

There are other opinions, for example, Andrei Konchalovsky believes that “nationalism in its constructive forms is, above all, serving one’s own people, not destroying other nations in the name of one’s own.” This is a rose-colored view. As shown above, the very concept of dividing the population into nations and nationalities was born with exclusively military purposes: to identify one’s own and foster a sense of dislike towards others. Everywhere rhetoric of “us versus them” is used, war is at stake. When the second part of this equation is removed and only a call to “serving one’s own people” is made, as Konchalovsky did, it smells of nothing but hypocrisy.

Now let’s draw attention to an interesting situation with estimating the total population of peoples and nations on the planet. According to various opinions, there are currently between 800 and 2000 of them in the world. Wikipedia lists only a few hundred. In other sources, I found even more uncertain figures: from several hundred to several thousand. Why is there no exact list of them, considering that we are talking about groups consisting of tens of thousands of people or more? Where does this inaccuracy come from?

It’s simple, mostly thanks to technological progress, the borders between nations are blurring and disappearing: we no longer know who is who.

The defining trends on the ethnic map of our planet at the moment are consolidation and assimilation. In simpler terms, it is becoming increasingly difficult to determine who among us is Russian, Ukrainian, Jewish, Tatar, or Black. Surprisingly, many scientists even consider the term “race” in relation to humans to have lost its scientific relevance. In other words, it is becoming more complex to objectively determine the nationality of a specific inhabitant of our planet, and there is less and less real need for it.

We were born in the USSR to a Ukrainian mother and a Belarusian father; we live in Berlin; we speak English at the office, Russian with our wife, German with our children, and Turkish at the supermarket; we believe in Jesus Christ, do not celebrate Easter, but celebrate Soviet Army Day; we wear Chinese jeans; we love Italian cuisine and French comedies. What nationality are we? What nationality will our children be, who will speak Russian with a strong German accent only when asked about it? None.

It is absolutely obvious that such signs of division by nations as place of birth, language, cultural traditions, religious beliefs, as well as skull shape and eye shape, are losing their relevance. Moreover, according to Parcavania, the use of these outdated signs can only indicate the “narrow-mindedness and backwardness” of the people who use them.

However, the desire to gather in groups and identify oneself through belonging to them has not disappeared. As we have seen above, it is innate and vital. In other words, nationalism, chauvinism, and xenophobia are ingrained and cannot be eradicated because they are natural. The question is only how those who are not “backward and narrow-minded” will identify the “other”.

The future nationalism, if civilization continues to develop rather than degrade, will divide people into groups and communities based on such characteristics as professional interests (for example, artists, programmers, writers, fashion designers), life beliefs (for example, vegetarians, child-free individuals, downsizers), sports (for example, bodybuilders, snowboarders, cyclists), music, and others. These groups and communities are precisely the “nations of the future.”

These new nations are already forming if you observe the development of communities, for example on Facebook. Alongside politically charged groups dedicated to hating “vatniks” or “Ukies,” groups of vegetarians or fans of good cinema are equally, if not more, popular.

The more developed a person is, the more educated and oriented towards self-development, the less significant for them are outdated categories of national division. However, their desire to identify themselves through belonging to a group and contrasting themselves with “others” does not disappear. It is realized through the formation of new nations and peoples that have nothing in common with Ukrainians, Russians, Chinese, or Jews. Now they are called “coffee lovers,” “theater actors,” “travel blogger,” and “rebirth enthusiasts.”

Sooner or later, the Ukrainian nation will cease to exist, just like the Georgian, Tatar, Spanish, or Russian nations. The Ukrainian language, literature, music, cuisine, rituals, and traditions will remain, but there will be no “Ukrainian people”. This concept will simply outlive itself.
