I recently expressed my opposition to the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from Ukraine, which sparked a lot of negative comments from readers, most of which boiled down to “if you don’t like it, leave.” Only a few were interested in what exactly I was calling for, what my constructive proposal was. I’m glad that there were still those who were able to see an unspoken call for real actions behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and still is the dire situation of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire, and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, as an empire exists to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is entirely natural that the development of Ukrainian culture, as well as its hindrance, has been greatly influenced by its administrative subordination to Russia and the Soviet Union, although not always negatively.

There is significantly less good literature written in Ukrainian than in Russian. There have been few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the homeland. For many years, Moscow has attracted talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors. And they gladly moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol did at the age of 19, leaving the Poltava province for St. Petersburg. Would “The Government Inspector” have been written if he had stayed in his hometown of Nezhin?

Culture consists of talents. And talent, first of all, seeks a soil on which it will be most comfortable to grow and where it will be appreciated. Rich and developed St. Petersburg was much more attractive for a young and talented writer at that time than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive for young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kiev, Berlin, or Delhi. People from all over the world aspire to go there if they have ambitions and a desire to fulfill them. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving away its talents to Moscow. And this is undoubtedly offensive. It would probably be great if the novel “The Master and Margarita” had been written in Ukrainian, as Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv, where he moved to Moscow at the age of 30; or if the famous “Black Square” had been created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth before moving to Moscow at the age of 25; or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky sang “Lilac Negro” in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kyiv and moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

However, none of this happened.

Ukrainian culture has suffered many losses and fallen far behind, just like many other cultures that contributed to the creation of Russian culture.

Do we want to fix this situation? Definitely. But doing it by police methods, which the current Ukrainian government is confidently moving towards, by banning the Russian language, books, and movies - this only sets up against the Ukrainian language and culture those very talents that should bring them to the level of Russia.

Police bans are disgusting, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and I am not alone, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here’s what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not only from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there will be no such rejection as the Ukrainianization is causing right now.

I really hope that Ukraine follows the path described above.
