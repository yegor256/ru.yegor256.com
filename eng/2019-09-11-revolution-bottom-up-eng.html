Russian liberalism is taking on increasingly aggressive forms and taking to the streets, Instagram, and YouTube. “We will definitely win” is the main slogan of those fighting against injustice. For now, their main weapon is peaceful demonstrations, thousands of likes, and professionally edited video channels. Many of them are even likable to us. However, their directors deserve genuine applause. In the relentless cyber war, they manage to defeat armies of “Kremlin bots,” turning mediocrity into popular heroes. As Russia approaches its denouement, a Maidan-like scenario seems to await, with a transfer of power, redistribution of property, amnesties, arrests, and… the ultimate victory of good over evil. It is expected that “we will win,” and corruption along with bureaucratic lawlessness will disappear forever. Perhaps we will “win,” but will they disappear?

Who are these enemies of the Russian people who take bribes and hinder our way of life? They are citizens of Russia who have been fortunate enough to hold positions in the government. There are approximately 140 of them for every ten thousand people. But what do the other 9860 people in Russia do? Who do they work for and what do they produce? Let’s listen to the statistics and do the math.

In 2018, Russia exported goods and services worth $450 billion. Of this, only $125 billion (about a quarter) accounted for the so-called non-raw non-energy exports, which includes everything other than oil and gas. This means that three-quarters of the country’s exports are related to the extraction of natural resources and selling them abroad, or, as the father of the nation aptly called it, “trading with the Motherland.” It is entirely logical that with a corrupt state apparatus and the state as the main shareholder of the largest oil companies, the main beneficiaries of this trade with the Motherland are officials and law enforcement agencies.

The rest of the population is mostly engaged in either extracting these minerals (only 2.2% of the total working-age population) or serving the mentioned officials (over 70%, according to the Federal State Statistics Service, see page 49). They need to be groomed, dressed, provided with footwear, fed, resettled in new apartments, and entertained.

In order for an official to be able to steal, they need help. They must be well-dressed and well-equipped. They should have electricity and hot water in their apartment. They need good roads, quality food, and medical services. The majority of the working population in Russia is responsible for providing all of this. Officials extract oil and sell it abroad, and the population helps them in this endeavor, dreaming of being as close to the state as possible, and if lucky, even occupying a position near valuable natural resources.

This is what modern Russian neo-feudalism looks like.

In the current situation, someone blames the Soviet past and post-Soviet devastation from which we will never recover; someone believes that the rich nature is a curse for modern Russia, corrupting the economy; and for someone, it is obvious that behind all this are American Masons, seeing Russia as a resource supplement to their American economy and throwing us their geopolitical challenges in the form of new packages of sanctions. The majority agrees on one thing: there is no way out.

However, Russia is a country with immense resources and a centuries-old corruption problem, involving the majority of its population. I dare to assume that attempts to combat corruption in Russia have always been made, but never with the intention of completely eliminating it. For instance, five centuries ago, Tsar Ivan the Terrible introduced the death penalty as a punishment for “excessive (!) bribes.” Note that it was not for bribes themselves, but specifically for their excessiveness.

Thus, producing little of value, we primarily serve officials who “trade the homeland.”

And in these conditions, we hear the slogan “we will win.” The question is, who exactly do you want to defeat, gentlemen liberals? And what will we do after we imprison those who are currently pumping oil? In their place, we will have to seat new officials and assign them to pump the same oil, in the same volumes. Simply because we don’t know how to do anything else, and we don’t really want to.

We either steal or serve.

The European or American model could potentially work for us if implemented gradually, over the course of about a hundred years, gradually replacing oil and gas extraction with furniture, automobile, phone, construction material, and meat production. Gradually replacing thieves and their associates with builders, programmers, farmers, and engineers. Gradually replacing respect for uniform with respect for calloused hands. Gradually replacing dreams of the greatest country with dreams of the wealthiest. Gradually!

Today’s “our” temporary victory at Sakharov Avenue will only lead to one thing: the replacement of one officials with others. The essence and structure of the Russian economy and social order will not change. They cannot be changed in one day. And not in one year either. And certainly not by one person.

It is absolutely obvious to the authors of the slogan “we will win” that they are talking about their personal victory, not ours. They will win, and we will remain who we were - servants of the people.
