First of all, according to statistics, police officers in the United States kill approximately three people every day, with around 70% of them being African Americans. Thirdly, according to statistics, racism in the United States is decreasing every year: in 1950, only 5% of white people approved of marriages with people of color, while in 2013, over 85% had no objections. Against this backdrop, it seems quite strange, for example, the statement made by Russian Foreign Ministry representative Maria Zakharova that “the problem of racism in the United States is growing.” Obviously, someone wants it to “appear,” while it is steadily disappearing.

Thus, it is evident that the problem of racism-based violence does not have a systemic and nationwide character in American society. However, it is presented as a tragedy of global coronavirus proportions, rallying tens of thousands of both black and white individuals under its black and white banners. Why is this being done and who benefits from it?

Without being afraid to repeat myself once again, I suggest considering a conflict much more fundamental and profound than a racial one: a class conflict. Capitalism, despite the high standard of living in most European and North American countries, has never lost its slaveholding essence inherited from earlier forms of social organization. The tension arises not only from the notorious growing gap between the rich and the poor but also from the gap between the philosophies of the owners and the slaves: the majority toils to survive while the minority owns and enjoys life without having to work at all.

There is no peaceful solution to this conflict, and there cannot be. Over the course of many centuries, from Spartacus to Mao Zedong, humanity has only found one solution: revolution, which inevitably leads to repression, civil war, dictatorship, and… a return to previous positions.

The owners of factories, newspapers, and steamships are concerned about preventing a new uprising of the “hungry and enslaved.” They are more concerned about this issue than overpopulation, climate change, hunger, and epidemics. And it’s not just about the People’s Republic of China, which, with its modified but still communist ideology, is becoming a real threat to the global capitalist order as it gains economic power. The main issue is the contradiction of capitalism according to Marx, which does not disappear no matter how many slaves are fed with new iPhones and low-interest mortgages.

Creating distracting conflicts is an excellent way to mask the main conflict: from now on, it is not capitalism that is the cause of the oppressive and growing hopelessness of life for the middle class in the West, but rather racism, sexism, anti-Semitism, masculinism, feminism, environmental pollution, domestic violence (important to emphasize). All efforts are thrown into fighting these social diseases, but defeating them is, of course, impossible. The fight can and should continue indefinitely, creating a sense of revolutionary tension in society, showcasing broken windows, and emotionally satisfying those who would not mind, given leaders, rising against oppressors. The opportunity to rise is given, but not against oppressors, but against mythical racists whom no one has seen, but everyone considers it their duty to hate them.

The riots, if they erupted spontaneously after the death of the unfortunate drug addict Floyd, spread throughout America and Europe not on their own. And they do not harm Trump, as some may think. On the contrary, they are beneficial to both Trump, Hillary, conservatives, Democrats, and all the other lackeys of global capital who sponsor these riots.
