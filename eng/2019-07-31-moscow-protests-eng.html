Revolts, protests, and revolutions can have various natures, but essentially, they can probably be divided into two categories. The first one is rebellion. Bright examples of this include Krasnodar (1961), Aleksandrov (1961), Tambov (1920-1921), and finally, the Spartacus uprising. The cause of rebellion is mass dissatisfaction with living conditions, specifically hunger and/or violence. As life becomes increasingly worse, people can no longer bear it, their patience snaps, and they take to the streets, unafraid of punishment because they have no strength left.

The second category, which includes the majority of mass protest movements, is the change of power. One ruling group fights against another for access to resources and uses mass disorder as a tool of pressure. The most vivid examples in recent times are the Euromaidan in Kiev in 2014.

Certainly, it is quite difficult to draw a clear line between the revolt of an oppressed people and the organized transfer of power disguised as a people’s struggle against tyranny. Of course, at the rally last Sunday on Tverskaya in Moscow, there were many who sincerely believed that by replacing Putin with Navalny, they would be able to improve their personal well-being.
