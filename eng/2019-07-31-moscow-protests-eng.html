Last Sunday in Moscow, according to Lyubov Sobol, a candidate who was not allowed to participate in the Moscow City Duma elections, the “largest unauthorized rally in recent history” took place. The protesters demanded, in her words, “simple and understandable things”: elections, freedom, and respect. Putin, Sobyanin, and other “scoundrels” in power stand in the way of the oppressed people. We just need to push a little harder, and evil will retreat, prisons will crumble, and freedom will joyfully welcome us at the entrance. We also thought the same way in 2004, adorning ourselves with orange ribbons in Kyiv. And then again in 2014 during the Euromaidan. And we were wrong.

Have you ever tried to organize a rally with thousands of people? In theory, it should be easy. There is an obvious agenda: Putin is a scoundrel and a thief. This is currently a trend and it seems like everyone would support you in it. Create a YouTube channel for yourself, record a few videos, tell the truth to everyone, present the facts, and call for resistance and fighting. Then set a date for the rally and come there with signs, megaphones, and flyers. How many people do you think will show up?

A few of your friends, no more.

Of course, Navalny has almost three million subscribers on YouTube, you might say! First, you need to build an audience, and then a rally will be organized, right? Not quite. Sometimes I watch the channel called “Enough Silence!” which is hosted by a guy who is not from Moscow (I don’t know his name). He has eight times fewer subscribers than Navalny. Not a thousand, but eight. However, despite this seemingly large audience of 330 thousand people, even a plane ticket to Moscow to participate in the rally, according to his own words, turned out to be “an expensive pleasure” for him.

Do you know how much it costs to set up a stage for a rally and rent the necessary audio equipment? Or to pay for the work of the campaign headquarters? And the funds for motivating “volunteers,” meeting moderators, and of course, the “independent” press? Some time ago, the late Boris Berezovsky announced the amount of financial assistance he provided to the forces behind the Orange Revolution of 2004: $45,000,000.

It is impossible to organize a mass rally with just one call to fight for “freedom and respect”.

Uprisings, protests, and revolutions can have various natures, but essentially, they can probably be divided into two categories. The first one is rebellion. Bright examples include Krasnodar (1961), Aleksandrov (1961), Tambov (1920-1921), and finally, the Spartacus uprising. The cause of rebellion is mass dissatisfaction with living conditions, specifically hunger and/or violence. Life becomes increasingly worse, people can no longer endure it, their patience wears thin, and they take to the streets, no longer fearing punishment because they have no strength left.

The second category, which most mass protest movements can be classified under, is the change of power. One ruling group fights against another for access to resources and uses mass unrest as a tool of pressure. The most notable recent example is the Euromaidan in Kiev in 2014.

Undoubtedly, it is quite difficult to draw a clear line between the revolt of an oppressed people and the organized transfer of power under the guise of the people’s struggle against tyranny. Certainly, during the protests on Maidan in Kiev in 2014, there were many who sincerely expressed their dissatisfaction with their lives and hoped to bring about change by overthrowing Yanukovych. Of course, at the recent rally on Tverskaya Street in Moscow last Sunday, there were many who sincerely believed that by replacing Putin with Navalny, they would be able to improve their personal well-being. And naturally, every revolt inevitably mobilizes all the processes of power transfer that have matured by its beginning. And, of course, any revolt mostly brings to power those who have already been fighting for it in one way or another before the mass actions began.

However, sometimes the ears of power seekers stick out too much.

It is evident that the protest movements in Russia in recent years should be attributed to the second category - power transfer. There are no objective reasons for dissatisfaction with life among Russians. Undoubtedly, Russia is not the most comfortable country to live in, and I have written about this more than once. However, thanks to powerful propaganda and a slowly growing economy, there cannot be natural mass protest sentiments among the population. There are excesses, there are shortcomings, there is corruption, but there are not enough conditions for a mass revolt against the existing system.

I did not attend the unauthorized rally last Sunday, but I went to the sanctioned one two weeks ago. Overall impression: it’s not a protest, it’s a Sunday stroll. People, for the most part, are satisfied. They are stylishly dressed, calm, and on Monday they have well-paid jobs waiting for them. They came to take a walk in the beautiful center of Moscow, to see people and show themselves. They have no aggression and are very afraid of getting into any conflicts with the police. They are polite, friendly, and their behavior is devoid of any signs of rebellion.

Russia is not rebelling on its own. More precisely, it is rebelling within the framework of a typical rebellion in a Central European country, like Italy, Germany, or England regularly rebels. Someone wants Russia to rebel more intensively. Intensely enough to bring about a change in power. Who wants this? What forces stand behind Navalny and others? Where will these forces lead the country after gaining power? Will this direction be more democratic than it is now? I don’t know.

Do those who attend rallies ask themselves these questions?
