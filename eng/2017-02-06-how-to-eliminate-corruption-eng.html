Ukraine remains one of the countries with the highest level of corruption on the planet. On one hand, it is convenient when one can steal and bribe their way out. On the other hand, it is sad to realize that Ukrainians still remain one of the most ignorant nations on the planet, unable to organize the work of their public servants and police officers in a way that prevents them from stealing. How can we become smarter? Are there any proven methods?

According to Robert Klitgaard, known for his research on the nature of this phenomenon, “corruption is greed plus opportunity minus control.” From this formula, it follows that in order to eradicate corruption, a) opportunities for officials need to be reduced and b) control over them needs to be strengthened. However, reducing greed does not seem possible simply due to human nature - we always want more.

So, the first thing is the reduction of the powers of officials, and there are many ways to do it. For example, in Georgia during Saakashvili’s time, the “one-stop shop principle” was introduced (also applied in other countries) to reduce the number of procedures for obtaining government documents and, consequently, reduce the number of signatures they put in exchange for bribes.

The second part of the formula requires increased control over officials, which primarily involves stricter punishments and direct punitive actions. For example, Hong Kong is known for its idea of “presumption of guilt” for officials, where each of them is considered a thief until proven otherwise. The experience of Singapore is also indicative, where under the leadership of Lee Kuan Yew, anti-corruption legislation was tightened to such an extent that, for example, even the promise of a bribe is considered a bribe. And China is ahead of everyone - in the last ten years, over 10,000 officials have been executed there.

Everything seems logical in this formula, and the solution is obvious both for Ukraine and any other country: 1) increase the salaries of officials, 2) reduce their number and simplify the procedures they control, and finally 3) regularly shoot violators. However, there is another important aspect that many people pay attention to - people’s attitude towards corruption. Those people who are the source of existence for officials and from whose pockets they steal.

Vitaliy Portnikov, in his analysis of the issues of fighting corruption in Ukraine, writes that the success of this fight “may be linked not to government measures, not to repression, not to punishments, but to public morality,” referring to the urgent need for changes in our consciousness before any anti-corruption measures can have an effect.

In my strong belief, the first step in this fight should be a change in our attitude towards the wealth of officials. More often than not, it arouses envy in us, when it should arouse anger. We should understand that their expensive cars are, first and foremost, a sign of our own foolishness. They were stolen from us, and they drive around our own streets, while we watch and do nothing.

Moreover, we must change our attitude towards officials in general. It is important to understand that they are our service staff, living off our money. We are forced to keep them around to organize our lives and, bluntly speaking, to take out the trash and clean the roads. The head of the regional tax office or the deputy head of customs should be much less significant to us than, for example, the head chef or the supermarket director.

The new rules, which Kaha Bendukidze talked about, will involve exactly such a transformation of the system: the reverent and fearful attitude towards officials will be replaced by a calm and utilitarian one. After that, all other changes will occur naturally, and corruption will turn from a widespread disaster into a localized problem, as in developed countries.
