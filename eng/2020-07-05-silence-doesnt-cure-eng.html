Let’s assume that scientists are right and it is imperative to do something about intolerance so that it does not hinder progress and threaten humanity (even though it has coexisted with progress for thousands of years, and progress has somehow managed to deal with it; but let’s leave this logical inconsistency on the conscience of scientists). Do campaigns against, for example, racism, help to overcome it or do they have the opposite effect?

In 2020, the United Nations held the “World Conference against Racism, Racial Discrimination, Xenophobia and Related Intolerance” in South Africa, during which it was stated that (I quote) “any doctrine of racial superiority is scientifically invalid, morally condemnable, socially unjust and dangerous, and should be rejected along with theories attempting to establish the existence of separate human races.” How can it be that doctrines and theories don’t exist yet, but we a priori deem them scientifically invalid?

It is very difficult to make Borat stop hating Jews, whom he believes can transform into insects. The only way to deal with such radical anti-Semitism is to explain or scientifically prove that this version is a fiction.

It is worth noting that not every xenophobia is as comical as Borat’s fear of Jews. In the overwhelming majority of cases, each hatred has a perfectly logical explanation that underlies the offensive generalization.

For example, according to statistics, more than half of the homicides in the United States are committed by African American citizens, while they make up only 13% of the total population. Should a police officer, when apprehending an African American criminal, take into account this statistic and take stricter safety measures than if they were apprehending, let’s say, an Indian? Wouldn’t the use of this statistic be offensive towards the specific offender who ended up being African American through no fault of their own?

Another example: currently, the overwhelming majority of civilian pilots are men. Should an airline conduct additional training and checks, such as stress resilience assessments, when hiring female pilots, as women are believed to handle stress worse? Should airlines notify passengers that a woman will be piloting their flight? Would such information be offensive to a specific female pilot who may be more stress-resistant than the average representative of the “women” social group?

And finally, another example: you are walking with your child and find yourself near a stadium where a football match has just ended with a defeat. Excited people with masks on their faces and cold weapons in their hands surround you. They have not yet started breaking windows or flipping cars, but it is highly likely that it is heading towards that. Would you explain to your child that these are aggressive football fans and they should not expect anything good from their company - it is better to stay away? It is quite possible that many of them are wonderful people. Moreover, not all football fans break windows. Your generalization will undoubtedly offend many of them, but it will save your child’s life. What will you choose, lengthy discussions on political correctness and tolerance, or immediate stereotyping and evacuation to a safe place?

I mean that not every xenophobia and even hatred is unfounded and is a prejudice. It is necessary to be able to distinguish between the two and to fight against the latter, getting rid of senseless violence and aggression, which, as we know from Fromm, is the main obstacle on the path of human progress. The only remedy against prejudices can be one thing: enlightenment.

However, the opposite is happening. The UN, as we can see, condemns any theories about the existence of races, and instead of identifying contradictions, emphasizing differences, and analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of different social groups, everyone is declared equal. Instead of enlightenment, which is always based on doubts and internal conflicts, we are offered empty slogans like “black lives matter” that are emotionally charged but lack substance. Education is being blocked. Sharp social issues are being tabooed. Scientists are being stripped of their honors. Any discussion of inequality that does not include radical polarizations and unequivocally condemning everything negative is considered racism and leads to a boycott of anyone who dares to engage in such a discussion.

A few years ago, I participated in a computer conference in a small Western European city. In the corridors, I struck up conversations with several fellow speakers. We touched on the topic of racism, and I allowed myself to mention what I wrote in this article above: not everything is so straightforward, and it’s important to understand that if people of color commit crimes several times more often, it’s not enough to demand unbiased treatment from them, we also need to address the crimes and do something to reduce their level. Within a few minutes, the table where we were sitting became empty. The discussion came to a halt before it even began, as the participants simply fled from the subject.

Supposedly moving towards a noble goal - reducing xenophobia - we achieve the opposite effect by using repressive methods. Tabooing the subject and intimidating scientists will not eliminate social, racial, sexual, or national conflicts. Only through education and enlightenment, which require open discussions and debates, can we truly address these issues. However, discussions are becoming fewer, debates are prohibited, and dissenting voices are silenced.

Perhaps someone benefits from the opposite task being solved under the guise of fighting hatred worldwide: igniting the fire of xenophobia with the fuels of foolishness, narrow-mindedness, and fanaticism.
