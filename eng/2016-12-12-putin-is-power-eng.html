Recently, Vladimir Putin held a meeting with the Council for Development of Civil Society and Human Rights, during which he “strongly criticized some of the existing gross violations of law and human rights in Russia.” In particular, the president highlighted an absurd court decision: “In the judge’s ruling, it is written that a person committed a crime by submitting a statement to the Lipetsk regional prosecutor’s office. Such things make my hair stand on end. What is this? Have they completely lost their minds?” And indeed, how can such a thing be acceptable?

This is unacceptable. But I’m not talking about the Lipetsk regional prosecutor. I’m talking about the president, who protects us from himself, emotionally disapproving of it.

We see in the power that starts with the president and ends with the Lipetsk regional prosecutor, the main enemy and the main force threatening our rights. They govern, we obey. They decide, we execute. They permit, we rejoice. They prohibit, we grieve and endure.

These emotions of ours are the foundation and guarantee of stability in power.

We forget that these people, from Vladimir Vladimirovich to the prosecutor in Lipetsk, were chosen by us to serve us and at our expense. We like to see them as our superiors, although in fact, we are the ones who are the bosses, the people who elected them and provide for them.

Our fear of presidents and prosecutors is equivalent to the fear of a warehouse guard: it’s our warehouse, our goods are stored there, and we pay the salary. And then, after some time, this guard starts dictating rules for visiting our own warehouse, not allowing us in on weekends and taking half of our products for himself. Will this behavior evoke respect and fear from us? Not at first. But over time, we will start considering the guard an important figure and will create a Council to protect our civil rights from this scoundrel in a cap.

If our attitude towards our government was sober and adequate, then President Putin should have apologized to this Council for the behavior of the prosecutor, his subordinate. Ideally, he should have requested his resignation, due to the inability to fully perform his duties and serve us with faith and truth.

Instead, by his “completely lost his mind” remark, Putin emphasized who is the guardian of power here and to whom one should turn for justice. Not to dismiss the guardian as incompetent, but to seek justice from them, hoping for mercy. He emphasized whose hands hold the power and who exactly will decide when, where, and how justice will be served.

Are we not ashamed to create these Human Rights Guidelines and to ask for justice from those whom we should demand it from?
