“Кнут и пряник для украинского языка”

I recently spoke out against the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture in Ukraine, which has sparked a lot of negative comments from readers, most of which can be summarized as “if you don’t like it, leave.” Only a few have actually asked what exactly I am advocating for, what my constructive suggestions are. I’m glad that there are still those who were able to see an implied call to real actions beyond criticism. Now, I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and still is the dire situation of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, as an empire’s purpose is to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is natural that the administrative subordination to Russia and the Soviet Union had a significant influence on the development of Ukrainian culture, as well as its hindrance, although not always negative.

Культура России принадлежит десяткам народов Запада и Востока. Именно на этой основе, на многонациональной почве, она выросла во всем своем своеобразии. Дмитрий Лихачев

There is significantly less good literature written in Ukrainian than in Russian. There have been few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of a great love for the homeland. Moscow has been attracting talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors for many years. They gladly moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol did at the age of 19, leaving the Poltava province for St. Petersburg. Would “The Inspector General” have been written if he had stayed in his native Nezhin?

Culture consists of talents. And talent, first of all, seeks a ground on which it will be most comfortable to grow and where it will be appreciated. Rich and developed St. Petersburg was much more attractive for a young and talented writer at that time than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive to young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kiev, Berlin, or Delhi. People from all over the world aspire to go there if they have ambitions and a desire to fulfill them. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities are left behind at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

For many years, Ukraine was losing by giving its talents to Moscow. And this, undoubtedly, is offensive. It would probably be great if the novel “Master and Margarita” was written in Ukrainian since Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv before moving to Moscow at the age of 30. Or if the famous “Black Square” was created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth before moving to Moscow at the age of 25. Or, for example, if Alexander Vertinsky sang “Lilac Negro” in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kyiv and moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

However, none of this happened.

Ukrainian culture has suffered significant losses and fallen far behind, just like many other cultures that contributed to the creation of Russian culture.

Do we want to fix this situation? Absolutely. But doing it by police methods, which the current Ukrainian government is confidently adopting, by banning the Russian language, books, and movies, is only alienating those very talents that should bring them up to the Russian level.

Где много политики, там нет культуры. Иван Бунин

Police bans are disgusting, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and I am not alone, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here is what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not only from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

Books. In Russia, there are many awards for writers, including the Russian Booker Prize with a grand prize of $20,000 (a small amount for such a large country, but still). In Ukraine, the Lesya Ukrainka Prize, worth 10,000 hryvnias, ceased to exist ten years ago, and there is practically nothing else. If, for example, an annual prize for the best book in the Ukrainian language with a prize fund of $200,000 is announced, I think even Vladimir Sorokin will switch to Ukrainian. There is no need to spend money fighting the “Russian chimera,” give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved - we will start reading them.

Schools. Instead of prohibiting teaching in Russian, we should encourage teaching in Ukrainian. Let’s pay teachers twice as much if they conduct classes and give lectures in Ukrainian. Let’s provide scholarships to students who defend their diplomas and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not demanding or forcing, but motivating and incentivizing. Some may ignore this call, but for others, it will prove effective, and we will gain more genuine desire to write in the national language.

Advertising. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but rather encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, tax incentives can be introduced for enterprises that advertise themselves in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the business – lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian or pay higher taxes and advertise in the native language of many, Russian. The business and its customers will decide what is more advantageous for them. Meanwhile, the country will experience organic growth in Ukrainian language speakers.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke in Ukrainian, we could celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But this will not happen until he himself wants it. It is impossible to force him. He, as it seems to me, is a journalist who respects himself enough not to stoop to Ukrainian populism, as Saakashvili does. So how can we motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a language that is not native to them, without fearing a loss in quality? I think there is nothing we can do. Language is their main tool and commodity, and their popularity directly depends on its quality. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for this, we must guarantee freedom of speech to this new young generation. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and will write in Ukrainian from childhood. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

Documents. In Ukraine today, there is one official language, and there is no point in changing this situation. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to independently decide in which language it is most convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolaev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhhorod, it may be Hungarian. Of course, some documents of national importance, such as passports, must be in Ukrainian. But such “global” documents are extremely few. This freedom of language choice will only add respect for their country and its official language to the citizens of Ukraine, as the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

Banks. Forcing banks to conduct documentation with clients in Ukrainian in regions such as Odessa, Nikolaev, or Dnipro literally means hindering their communication with those who owe them money or to whom they owe money. Instead, if we want the population to switch to the Ukrainian language in business communication, let’s provide banks that fully document only in Ukrainian, for example, with state loans at preferential rates. The choice will be up to the client: go to a bank where all the paperwork is in Ukrainian and get better conditions there, or be served where the documentation is in their native language but more expensive. I will choose the latter, but only for now. If the cost difference is significant, I will consider and sincerely become interested in learning Ukrainian. I will understand why I need it. Not because there is no other way, but because I will see a real financial motive.

Cinema. Ukrainian cinema is still limited in quantity and remains of low quality. Implementing measures similar to those for books, such as establishing serious state awards for good Ukrainian films, is the first obvious solution. The second is the organization of film festivals. Russia has plenty of them, while Ukraine has significantly fewer, and the quality is significantly lower. The third is the provision of state funds for film producers. For the country, this would be a minor expense, but for the Ukrainian film industry, it would be a tremendous support.

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there won’t be such rejection as the Ukrainization is causing now.

I really hope that Ukraine will follow the path described above.
