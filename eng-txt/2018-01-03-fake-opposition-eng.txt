The main goal of any state regime is survival. The main potential threat to this is always the people who are oppressed by this regime. The most active and determined representatives of the people, united around a common goal, are called the opposition. The opposition's transition to decisive actions results in rallies, demonstrations, strikes, riots, and terrorist acts. Unsuccessful attempts are qualified as a coup d'etat and are harshly punished by the regime. Successful ones are given the proud name of Revolution and are celebrated. A new regime emerges.

In order to avoid watching the anniversary of the revolution being celebrated on the television cameras of the investigative isolation facility, the regime needs to keep the opposition under control. It is necessary to prevent them from taking decisive actions. To achieve this, they can be pursued, punished, frightened, and destroyed. However, the martyr role of opposition revolutionaries only stimulates their enthusiasm and attracts new supporters. Only regimes of strong dictatorships (like Lukashenko, Kim Jong Un, and Berdimuhamedov) can afford open repressive measures against the opposition.

A weak regime, taking its first steps towards true authoritarianism (Russia, Ukraine), finds it more advantageous to use the method of fictitious opposition, which consists of the following. The most prominent, vocal, and audacious figures are selected from the opposition movement, whose slogans are filled with hatred towards the regime, but completely exclude any constructive ideas ("Putin is a thief" or "Poroshenko is the supreme profiteer"). These figures become the spokespersons of the opposition (Navalny, Saakashvili). The regime minimally obstructs them, only enough to not deprive them of their status as hero-revolutionaries (regularly arresting and releasing them).

Soon, a majority of those dissatisfied with the regime gather around these leaders. Over time, the percentage of those capable of meaningful and constructive revolutionary actions among the gathered starts to decline. This is mainly due to the intensive influx of enthusiastic supporters who often chant catchy slogans, sometimes containing profanity. As a result, the quality of the opposition mass descends to the level of an ordinary citizen who is completely incapable of causing any significant harm to the regime.

Opposition leaders, being public figures, are entirely dependent on the opinions and desires of their audience. Being hostages of those they have gathered around themselves, they are forced to generate the informational product that their opposition electorate demands, namely, widespread criticism towards those in power. However crude and offensive this opposition may sound, it is completely harmless to the regime because those who are truly capable of carrying out a coup will not rally behind its slogans. By the time the main opposition leaders triumph, such people simply do not exist among their supporters.

The development of a fictional opposition does not hinder the regime, but on the contrary, actively contributes to it. The louder and cruder their slogans sound, the more incapable population they gather around themselves, and the fewer truly dangerous fighters against the regime remain there. At the same time, the real revolution loses its chances of capturing minds because its voice sounds quieter and less convincing against the backdrop of categorical, vivid, bold, and primitive slogans.

Russia has been following this path for a long time. Currently, Ukraine is also adopting this method.
