I recently expressed my opposition to the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from Ukrainian territory, which resulted in a lot of negative comments from readers, most of which boiled down to one phrase: "if you don't like it, leave." Only a few people were interested in what exactly I am calling for, what my constructive solution is. I am glad that there were still those who could see an unspoken call for real actions behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and continues to be the disastrous situation of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire, and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, as an empire is designed to oppress its subordinate territories. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is only natural that the development of Ukrainian culture, as well as its inhibition, has been greatly influenced by the administrative subordination to Russia and the Soviet Union, although not always negatively.

The culture of Russia belongs to dozens of Western and Eastern peoples. It is on this basis, on a multinational soil, that it has grown in all its uniqueness. - Dmitry Likhachev.

There is significantly less good literature written in Ukrainian than in Russian. There have been few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the homeland. For many years, Moscow attracted talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors. And they happily moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol did at the age of 19, leaving Poltava Governorate for St. Petersburg. Would "The Inspector General" have been written if he had stayed in his hometown of Nezhin?

Culture is made up of talents. And talent primarily seeks a soil on which it will be most comfortable to grow and where it will be appreciated. The wealthy and developed St. Petersburg was much more attractive for a young and talented writer at that time than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive to young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kiev, Berlin, or Delhi. Those with ambitions and a desire to fulfill them flock there from all over the world. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving away its talent to Moscow. And it is undoubtedly offensive. It would probably be great if the novel "The Master and Margarita" were written in Ukrainian, as Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 30; or if the famous "Black Square" was created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth before moving to Moscow at the age of 25; or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky sang "Lilovy Negro" in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

However, none of this happened.

Ukrainian culture has suffered significant losses and fallen far behind, just like many other cultures that have contributed to the creation of Russian culture.

Do we want to fix this situation? Absolutely. But doing it by police methods, which the current Ukrainian government is confidently adopting, by banning the Russian language, books, and movies — is only setting up those very talents against the Ukrainian language and culture, talents that should bring them up to the Russian level.

Police bans are disgusting, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and I'm not alone, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here's what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not just from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

**Books**. In Russia, there are [many awards](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9B%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B0%D1%82%D1%83%D1%80%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B5_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D0%BC%D0%B8%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%81%D0%B8%D0%B8) for writers, including the [Russian Booker](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A0%D1%83%D1%81%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%91%D1%83%D0%BA%D0%B5%D1%80) with a grand prize of $20,000 (a small sum for such a large country, but still). And in Ukraine, the [Lesya Ukrainka Prize](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9F%D1%80%D0%B5%D0%BC%D0%B8%D1%8F_%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8_%D0%9B%D0%B5%D1%81%D0%B8_%D0%A3%D0%BA%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%BA%D0%B8) with a prize of 10,000 hryvnias ceased to exist ten years ago, and practically [nothing else](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9A%D0%B0%D1%82%D0%B5%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D1%8F:%D0%9B%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B0%D1%82%D1%83%D1%80%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B5_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D0%BC%D0%B8%D0%B8_%D0%A3%D0%BA%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%8B) exists. If, for example, an annual prize for the best book in Ukrainian with a prize fund of $200,000 is announced, I think even [Vladimir Sorokin](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A1%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%BD,_%D0%92%D0%BB%D0%B0%D0%B4%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B8%D1%80_%D0%93%D0%B5%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B3%D0%B8%D0%B5%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87) will switch to Ukrainian. There is no need to spend money [fighting](http://rian.com.ua/analytics/20161209/1019506143.html) against the "Russian chimera," give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved - we will start reading them.

**Schools**. It should not be about prohibiting teaching in Russian, but about encouraging teaching in Ukrainian. Let's pay teachers twice as much if they conduct classes and give lectures in Ukrainian. Let's provide scholarships to students who defend their diplomas and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not demanding and forcing, but motivating and encouraging. Some may ignore this call, but for others, it will prove effective, and we will gain more sincere desire to write in the state language.

**Advertising**. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but instead encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, we could introduce tax incentives for businesses that advertise in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the business - lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian, or pay higher taxes and advertise in the native language of many, Russian. The business and its customers will decide what is more advantageous for them. The country, on the other hand, will benefit from organic growth in Ukrainian language speakers.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke Ukrainian, we could celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But this will not happen until he himself wants it. It is impossible to force him. He, as I think, is a journalist who respects himself enough not to stoop to Ukrainian populism, as Saakashvili does. So how can we motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a language that is not native to them, without fearing a loss in quality? I think there is nothing we can do. Language is their main tool and commodity, and their popularity depends directly on its quality. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for this, we must guarantee freedom of speech to this new young generation. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and will write in Ukrainian from an early age. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

**Documents**. In Ukraine today, there is one official language, and there is no point in changing this situation. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to independently decide in which language it is more convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolaev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhgorod, it could be even [Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians_in_Ukraine). Undoubtedly, some documents of national importance, such as passports, should be in Ukrainian. But there are very few of such "global" documents. Such language choice will only add respect for their country and its official language to the citizens of Ukraine, as the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

**Banks**. Forcing banks to conduct documentation in Ukrainian in regions such as Odessa, Nikolaev, or Dnipro, literally means hindering their communication with those who owe them money or whom they owe. Instead, if we want the population to switch to Ukrainian in business communication, let's provide banks that fully document only in Ukrainian, for example, state loans at preferential rates. The choice will be up to the client: go to a bank where all the paperwork is in Ukrainian and get better conditions there, or be serviced where the documentation is in their native language, but more expensive. I will choose the latter, but only for now. If the cost difference is significant, I will consider and with genuine interest, start learning Ukrainian. I will understand why I need it. Not because there is no way out, but because I will see a real financial motive.

**Cinema**. Ukrainian cinema is still scarce, and it is still of very low quality. The first obvious solution is to follow the same path as with books and establish serious [state awards](https://www.ukrinform.net/rubric-culture/2191986-stepan-koval-rezisseranimator-laureat-sevcenkovskoj-premii2017.html) for good Ukrainian films. The second solution is, of course, the [organization of film festivals](https://telegraf.com.ua/kultura/kino/amp-3160604-v-ukraine-planiruyut-sozdat-analog-oskara.html). Russia has plenty of them [here](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9A%D0%B0%D1%82%D0%B5%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D1%8F:%D0%9A%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%BE%D1%84%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B8%D0%B2%D0%B0%D0%BB%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%81%D0%B8%D0%B8), while Ukraine has significantly fewer [here](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9A%D0%B0%D1%82%D0%B5%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%B8%D1%8F:%D0%9A%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%BE%D1%84%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B8%D0%B2%D0%B0%D0%BB%D0%B8_%D0%A3%D0%BA%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%8B), and the quality is significantly lower. The third solution is [state funding](https://delo.ua/lifestyle/desjat-glavnyh-momentov-v-novom-zakone-o-gospodderzhke-kinematog-322966/) for film producers. For the country, this is insignificant expenditure, but for the Ukrainian film industry, it is [enormous support](http://cutinsight.com/prodyuser-mezhi-andrej-ermak-dazhe-art-hausnoe-kino-eto-biznes/).

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there will be no such rejection as the Ukrainianization currently causes.

I really hope that Ukraine will follow the path described above.
