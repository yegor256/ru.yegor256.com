Tomorrow in Russia, there will be presidential elections, in which Vladimir Putin will win. This will be his fourth term as president. In fact, he has been in power for 18 years, since taking office in January 2000. Almost 150 million people in the geographically largest country in the world will once again entrust this person with the mandate to govern their destiny. It is hard not to agree that this is a result deserving of respect. How does he do it?

The main factor of Putin's success in politics is his sober and adequate attitude towards the human material he has to deal with. When Feuchtwanger made a comment to Stalin about the tasteless and exaggerated worship of his personality, Stalin, according to him, "apologized for his peasants and workers, saying that they were too busy with other matters and couldn't develop good taste in themselves." Apparently, a similar sober cynicism is inherent in Putin, which is manifested in his style of governing his people.

Here are the main methods that produce such results:

Autocracy. Russia is Putin, and Putin is Russia. It is difficult to say exactly how decisions are made behind the scenes of power, who among the advisers carries more weight, and how authority is truly distributed among them. For an ordinary citizen, it creates the feeling that the country is ruled by one person. It doesn't matter what people's opinion of this person is, whether it is love or hatred. What matters is that the opinion is focused. This allows for keeping the situation under control.

Cultism. It is difficult to create a cult of personality for a ruler in six years, but it can and should be done in almost twenty years. The presence of a cult makes governance easier because it allows for reliance on authority. The average citizen is generally not inclined to think deeply and critically. Complex logical structures are difficult and tiring for them. Clear answers to pressing questions are what they need from those in power. The logical conclusion - "because it's Putin" - easily and confidently puts an end to most of them.

**Openness**. Annual [press conferences](http://kremlin.ru/events/president/news/56378) with journalists, [open lessons](https://ria.ru/society/20170901/1501532269.html) with schoolchildren, visits to [factories](https://ria.ru/society/20180201/1513799897.html), interviews with [foreign](https://www.1tv.ru/projects/putin/serii/putin-dokumentalnyy-film-olivera-stouna-chast-pervaya-smotret-onlayn) journalists - all of this creates an image of a person who is open and accessible. What exactly he says - and he speaks rather well - is not so important. It is important to see him openly communicating with people. This creates a sense of "familiarity" and "kinship". When we meet a famous actor on the street, we smile and nod, not receiving any reaction in return, which surprises and confuses us - we are used to considering this person as our close acquaintance. The same effect arises from the publicity of a politician - Putin has long become familiar to millions of Russians.

**Aggressiveness**. Even before becoming president, in 1999 Putin [promised](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9C%D0%BE%D1%87%D0%B8%D1%82%D1%8C_%D0%B2_%D1%81%D0%BE%D1%80%D1%82%D0%B8%D1%80%D0%B5) to "wipe out terrorists in the toilets," which, despite the crude wording, is pleasing to the average voter. In such a formulation, there is a clear formula: we are against them. Who exactly are these "they" that we will "wipe out" does not matter much. What matters is that there is a "we" who will not allow themselves to be offended. Throughout Putin's years in power, Russia has had enough enemies, and if there weren't enough, they were created by [blowing up buildings](https://www.svoboda.org/a/28718520.html) ([read](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A4%D0%A1%D0%91_%D0%B2%D0%B7%D1%80%D1%8B%D0%B2%D0%B0%D0%B5%D1%82_%D0%A0%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%81%D0%B8%D1%8E)). It is impossible to unite millions of people around the idea of peace. Hatred and war are needed for unity. Whether it is Chechnya, Georgia, Transnistria, Syria, Donbass, or all of NATO. The population wants to know where the enemy is. The leader's task is to find or create this enemy. Putin successfully copes with this.

**Religiosity**. It is hard to believe that a former member of the CPSU and a KGB lieutenant colonel became a believer. However, understanding the darkness of his people, he acts absolutely right by associating himself with the Russian Orthodox Church and its top officials. The idea of a tsar as God's anointed one is close to many Russians, who constitute the majority of the actively voting electorate. Religion, as Marx believed, remains the opium of the people. Believing in oneself, in logic, in common sense, and in cause-and-effect relationships is difficult and exhausting. People need simpler formulas that allow them to shift responsibility for their mistakes onto someone else. Christ serves this purpose just as well as Putin.

Power. Even [Machiavelli](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli) believed that the only way to control the masses is through fear. Neither love, nor respect, nor common sense, nor even money can stop the masses when they get out of control. By granting almost unlimited powers to the security forces, particularly the [FSB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Security_Service_of_the_Russian_Federation), Putin simplifies and crystallizes the image of power for his electorate - it is clear to everyone who to fear, who holds the power, where it comes from, and who to worship. The negative consequences for the country are outweighed by the positive, considering the type of people Putin has to deal with.

**Ruthlessness**. Regular arrests and cruel punishments of people from their closest circle create the image of a just ruler. It doesn't matter what exactly the next [Ulyukayev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleksey_Ulyukayev) is guilty of, what matters is how strict and inevitable the punishment is. The people fear the oprichniki, and the oprichniki fear the tsar - this formula has been working for hundreds of years. The people believe that the tsar knows nothing about lawlessness and corruption. The people want to see the tsar punish those who have become too arrogant. The people write letters to the tsar and rely on his justice. This format of communication with the authorities is convenient and understandable to the people.

**Hypocrisy**. The theatrical opposition led by the perennial Zhirinovsky vividly demonstrates that Putin knows how to play a double game and negotiate with opponents. He doesn't "eliminate" everyone in the toilets, like many dictators do. He pays someone, like [Kadyrov](https://www.segodnya.ua/world/nevzorov-putin-platit-dan-chechne-za-mir-dlya-rf-1022080.html), shares the stage and fame with someone, like Sobchak and Navalny, and [simply kills](https://republic.ru/russia/politicheskie_ubiystva_v_sovremennoy_rossii-1220581.xhtml) someone else — in each case, an individual decision is made. Questions of honor, conscience, and human dignity are completely ignored, as a competent leader of the masses should. People don't care what their leader is like, they care about their personal comfort. A leader who cares about the comfort of the people, rather than the purity of their conscience, will be respected and re-elected.

**Self-confidence**. Not acknowledging one's mistakes is a repelling quality in an ordinary person. However, in a prominent leader, it becomes a virtue. The people are incapable of understanding the reasons behind mistakes and the conclusions drawn from them. A mistake is just a mistake. According to Machiavelli, a leader who makes mistakes and even admits them is a weak leader. Putin [never](https://thequestion.ru/questions/61697/putin-kogda-nibud-publichno-izvinyalsya-kogda-nibud-priznaval-oshibki) criticizes himself or admits his mistakes, while continuing to make promises and not fulfill them, and even [lying](https://meduza.io/feature/2015/12/17/inostrantsy-ne-rvalis-usynovlyat-bolnyh-detey-na-samom-dele-net). From a purely human perspective, this upsets us, but on a scale of millions, it is a quality of an effective leader.

**Patriotism**. Wisdom, determination, energy, and decisiveness are important qualities of a leader. However, patriotism is even more important. The people, although they may occasionally forgive mistakes, will never forgive disloyalty. Constantly strained, and often openly hostile, relationships with other countries and their leaders are a clear message from Putin to his citizens: "I am with you, not with them." International sanctions and the isolation of Russia on the world stage have little practical impact on the average citizen, but through the media, they vividly illustrate the patriotism of the president.

The conclusion is that Putin is doing everything right, considering the circumstances he is in and the people who elect him. With different people in another country and under different conditions, it seems that something else could be done.
