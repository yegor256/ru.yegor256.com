Rebellions, protests, and revolutions can have various natures, but essentially, they can probably be divided into two categories. The first one is a revolt. Bright examples include Krasnodar (1961), Aleksandrov (1961), Tambov (1920-1921), and finally, the Spartacus uprising. The cause of a revolt is mass dissatisfaction with living conditions, namely hunger and/or violence. Life becomes increasingly worse, people can't bear it anymore, their patience snaps, and they take to the streets without fearing punishment, because they have no strength left.

The second category, which includes most mass protest movements, is a change of power. One power group fights against another for access to resources and uses mass disorder as a pressure tool. The most vivid examples in recent times are the Euromaidan in Kiev in 2014.

Undoubtedly, it is quite difficult to draw a clear line between the rebellion of an oppressed people and the organized transfer of power under the guise of popular struggle against tyranny. Of course, at the protest on Tverskaya Street in Moscow last Sunday, there were many who sincerely believed that by replacing Putin with Navalny, they would be able to improve their personal well-being. And, of course, every rebellion inevitably mobilizes all the processes of power transfer that have matured by the time it begins. Naturally, any rebellion primarily brings to power those who have already been fighting for it in one way or another before the mass actions began.
