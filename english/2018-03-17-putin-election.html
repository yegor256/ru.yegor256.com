“Что сделал Путин, чтобы завтра победить”

Tomorrow in Russia there will be presidential elections, in which Vladimir Putin will win. This will be his fourth term as president. In fact, he has been in power for 18 years, since the moment of its capture in January 2000. Almost 150 million people in the territorially largest country in the world will once again give this person a mandate to manage their destiny. It is hard not to agree that this is a result deserving of respect. How does he do it?

The main factor of Putin’s success is a sober and adequate attitude towards the human material he has to deal with. When in a conversation with Stalin, Feuchtwanger made a remark about the tasteless and exaggerated admiration for his personality, Stalin, according to his words, “apologized to his peasants and workers for being too busy with other matters and not being able to develop good taste in themselves.” Apparently, similar sober cynicism is inherent in Putin, which is manifested in his style of governing his people.

Here are the main methods that bring such results:

Autocracy. Russia is Putin, Putin is Russia. It is difficult to say exactly how decisions are made behind the scenes of power, who among the advisors has what influence, and how power is truly distributed among them. For an ordinary citizen, it creates the feeling that the country is controlled by one person. It is not so important what people’s opinion of this person is, whether it is love or hatred. What matters is that the opinion is focused. This allows for keeping the situation under control.

Cult of Personality. It is difficult to create a cult of personality for a ruler in six years of power, however, it can and should be done in almost twenty years. The presence of a cult facilitates governance because it allows for reliance on authority. The average citizen is generally not inclined to think deeply and critically. Complex logical constructions are difficult and tiresome for them. They need clear answers to pressing questions from those in power. The logical conclusion - “because it is Putin” - easily and confidently puts an end to most of them.

Openness. Annual press conferences with journalists, open lessons with schoolchildren, visits to factories, interviews with foreign journalists - all this creates the image of a person who is open and accessible. What exactly he says - and he speaks well - is not so important. It is important to see him openly communicating with people. This creates a sense of “familiarity” and “kinship”. When we meet a famous actor on the street, we smile and nod, not receiving any reaction in return, which surprises and dismays us - we are used to considering this person as our close acquaintance. The same effect occurs with the publicity of a politician - Putin has long become familiar to millions of Russians.

Aggressiveness. Even before becoming president, in 1999 Putin promised to “wipe out terrorists in the toilets,” which, despite the rudeness of the wording, is pleasing to the average voter. In this formulation, there is a clear formula: we are against them. The exact identity of these “they” whom we will “wipe out” does not play a major role. What is important is that there is “us” who will not allow themselves to be offended. Throughout Putin’s years in power, Russia has had enough enemies, and if there weren’t enough, they were created by blowing up buildings (read). It is impossible to unite millions of people around the idea of peace. Hatred and war are needed for unity. Whether it is Chechnya, Georgia, Transnistria, Syria, Donbass, or all of NATO as a whole. The population wants to know where the enemy is. The task of a leader is to find or create this enemy. Putin is successfully coping with this.

Religiosity. It is hard to believe that the former member of the CPSU and lieutenant colonel of the KGB became a believer. However, understanding the darkness of his people, he acts absolutely correctly by associating himself with the Russian Orthodox Church and its top officials. The idea of a tsar as the anointed one of God is close to many Russians, who make up the majority of the actively voting electorate. Religion, as Marx believed, was the opium of the people, and it will always remain so. It is difficult and tiresome to believe in oneself, in logic, in common sense, and in cause-and-effect relationships. Simple formulas are needed to shift the responsibility for one’s mistakes onto someone else. Christ is as suitable for this purpose as Putin.

Power. Machiavelli also believed that the only way to control the masses is through fear. Love, respect, common sense, and even money are unable to stop the masses when they get out of control. By granting practically unlimited authority to the security structures, particularly the FSB, Putin simplifies and crystallizes the image of power for his electorate - it is clear to everyone who exactly to fear, who holds the power, where it comes from, and whom to bow down to. Considering the kind of human material Putin deals with, the negative impact on the country is less than the positive.

Mercilessness. Regular arrests and cruel punishments of people from their closest circle create an image of a just ruler. It doesn’t matter what exactly another Ulyukayev is guilty of, what matters is how strict and inevitable the punishment is. The people are afraid of the oprichniki, and the oprichniki are afraid of the tsar - this formula has been working for hundreds of years. The people believe that the tsar knows nothing about lawlessness and corruption. The people want to see the tsar punishing the arrogant murderers. The people write letters to the tsar and rely on his fairness. This format of communication with the authorities is convenient and understandable to the people.

Hypocrisy. The theatrical opposition, led by the unchanging Zhirinovsky, clearly demonstrates that Putin knows how to play a double game and negotiate with opponents. He doesn’t “kill everyone in the toilet” like many dictators do. He pays someone, like Kadyrov, he shares the stage and fame with someone, like Sobchak and Navalny, and he simply kills someone - in each case, his own individual decision is made. Questions of honor, conscience, and human dignity are completely ignored, as a competent leader of the masses should act. People don’t care about their leader’s character, they care about their personal comfort. They will respect and re-elect a leader who cares about the comfort of the people, rather than the cleanliness of their conscience.

Self-confidence. Not recognizing one’s own mistakes is a repelling quality in an ordinary person. However, in a prominent leader, it turns into a virtue. The people are unable to understand the reasons for mistakes and the conclusions drawn. A mistake is a mistake. According to Machiavelli, a leader who makes mistakes and admits to them is a weak leader. Putin never criticizes himself or admits to mistakes, while continuing to make promises and not fulfill them, and even lying. From a purely human point of view, this infuriates us, but on a scale of millions, it is the quality of an effective leader.

Patriotism. Wisdom, determination, energy, and decisiveness are important qualities of a leader. However, patriotism is more important. The people, although they may sometimes forgive mistakes, will never forgive disloyalty. Constantly tense, and often openly hostile, relations with other countries and their leaders are a clear message from Putin to his citizens: “I am with you, not with them.” International sanctions and the isolation of Russia on the world stage have little practical impact on the average citizen, but they vividly illustrate the patriotism of the president through the media.

The conclusion is that Putin is doing everything right, taking into account the circumstances in which he finds himself and the people who choose him. With other people, in another country, under different conditions, it seems that something else could be done.
