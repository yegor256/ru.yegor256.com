“План "Фиктивная Оппозиция"”

The main task of any state regime is survival. The main potential threat to this is always the people oppressed by this regime. The most active and determined representatives of the people, united around a common goal, are called the opposition. The opposition’s transition to decisive actions results in rallies, demonstrations, strikes, riots, and acts of terrorism. Unsuccessful attempts are classified as a state coup and harshly punished by the regime. Successful ones are celebrated with pride under the name Revolution and a new regime is established.

In order to avoid watching the anniversary of the revolution celebrations on television from the cameras of the detention center, the regime needs to keep the opposition under control. It is necessary to prevent them from taking decisive actions. For this purpose, they can be pursued, punished, frightened, and destroyed. However, the martyr role of opposition-revolutionaries only fuels their enthusiasm and attracts new supporters. Only a regime of strong dictatorship (Lukashenko, Kim Jong-un, Berdimuhamedov) can afford open repressive measures against the opposition.

To a weak regime that is taking its first steps towards true authoritarianism (Russia, Ukraine), it is more beneficial to use the method of fictitious opposition, which consists of the following. The most prominent, loud, and audacious figures are chosen from the opposition movement, whose slogans are filled with hatred towards the regime, but completely exclude any constructive criticism (“Putin is a thief” or “Poroshenko is the supreme profiteer”). These figures become the spokespersons of the opposition (Navalny, Saakashvili). The regime minimally hinders them, only enough to not deprive them of the status of heroes-revolutionaries (regularly arrests and releases them).

Soon, around these leaders, the majority of those dissatisfied with the regime gather. Over time, the percentage of those capable of meaningful and constructive revolutionary actions among the gathered starts to decline. This is mainly due to the intensive influx of enthusiastic admirers of catchy and often vulgar slogans. In terms of quality, the composition of the opposition mass descends to the level of an ordinary citizen, completely incapable of causing any significant harm to the regime.

Opposition leaders, being public figures, are entirely dependent on the opinions and desires of their audience. Being hostages of those they have gathered around themselves, they are forced to generate the informational product that their opposition electorate demands, namely widespread criticism towards those in power. However rude and offensive this opposition may sound, it is completely harmless to the regime because those who are truly capable of carrying out a coup will not follow its slogans. By the time the main opposition leaders triumph, such people simply do not exist among their supporters.

The regime is not hindered by the development of a fictitious opposition, on the contrary, it actively contributes to it. The louder and more crude their slogans sound, the more incapable population they will gather around themselves, and the fewer truly dangerous fighters against the regime will remain there. At the same time, the real revolution loses its chances of capturing minds because its voice sounds quieter and less convincing against the backdrop of categorical, vivid, audacious, and primitive slogans.

Russia has been following this path for a long time. Now Ukraine is adopting this method.
