“Калиброванная демократия”

Democracy is the worst form of government. Power belongs to the crowd, which is incapable of thinking strategically and consists mostly of ignorant people who make impulsive decisions. This was believed by Aristotle in the second century BC. He considered a more effective system of government to be one in which power belongs to those who have more authority and are therefore able to make more balanced and informed decisions.

Contrary to the thoughts of the Greek philosopher, democracy is currently the prevailing form of government in most countries of the world. Even in countries where monarchy is preserved, such as Great Britain or Spain, the actual governance of the country is carried out by the cabinet of ministers, and laws are passed by an elected parliament. On paper, it is the people, through their representatives, who govern all countries in the world, including Cuba, North Korea, and China.

For example, Article 71 of the Constitution of Ukraine asserts that “elections to the bodies of state power and local self-government are free and conducted on the basis of universal, equal, and direct suffrage by secret ballot.”

Aristotle was precisely against this equality, and much later Wilfredo Pareto, Gaetano Mosca, and many other supporters of elitism, who believed that people are inherently unequal in many ways, both physically and intellectually. And, more importantly, there are significantly more weak and foolish individuals in any society than strong and intelligent ones. Thus, by endowing everyone with equal rights and giving “green light” to decisions made by the simple majority, we inevitably create conditions for the degradation of the entire community. The most rational and strategically balanced decisions will be in the minority along with their authors - simply due to the inherent inequality of human abilities. Roughly speaking, fools will always drown out the wise.

To solve the problem, it is logical to do either a) eliminate fools completely, b) deprive them of the right to vote, or c) make them think they have the right to vote, but in fact they do not. The Nazis tried to go down the first path, but they were stopped in time. The second path leads to massive revolutions, as seen in the year 1917. Currently, all developed countries, led by the United States, are following the third path - creating the illusion of democracy, where every citizen has equal voting rights, but the actual governance of the country is carried out regardless of the majority’s choice.

In other words, modern democracy is a qualitative spectacle performed by a minority for the majority in order to calm the latter and instill in them confidence in universal equality. Notice once again, all of this is done primarily in the interests of the majority.

Может быть есть четвертый путь?

Есть.

And what if equal voting rights are replaced with unequal ones? What if every citizen will not have just one vote, which they “cast” into the ballot box once in a while, but several votes, depending on their value to society, contribution to its development, and authority. Let’s say an economics professor will have 50 votes, while a hairdresser-stylist will only have two.

Of course, the first question that immediately arises is: who and how will decide how many votes each person has and why does the professor have more than the hairdresser? In order to answer this question, it would be possible to develop a transparent and relatively objective system for evaluating the contribution of each citizen to the societal and economic life of the country. For example, the following indicators could be used to calculate the electoral rating:

Вылаченные налоги

Опубликованные научные труды

Дети и внуки

Судимости и правонарушения

Возраст и состояние здоровья

Followers on Instagram (don’t laugh!)

And so on. The list can go on. Each indicator can be easily verified and published in a decentralized data storage system, for example, based on Blockchain. Thus, citizens’ votes will be calibrated based on their authority in matters of state governance. It is even possible to assume the possibility of regular qualification testing for every citizen who wishes to increase their rating. The higher the expected rating, the more professional and complex the questions will be, specifically in the field of economics, law, or the history of the state. By using decentralized technologies for data storage and processing, it is possible to completely eliminate bias in assessment and any form of corruption.

Such “calibrated” democracy will solve two problems at once. Firstly, it will bring to power those who truly deserve it and whose opinion we all should really rely on. Secondly, it will explain to everyone else what exactly they lack in order to also become those whose opinion the whole country listens to. At the same time, this will be done openly, objectively, and fairly, minimizing the eternal conflict between the rich and the poor, the smart and the foolish, the bottom and the top of any society.

Most likely, this model will not lead to a change in the people in power. However, it will make the electoral formula significantly more understandable and fair. After all, it is these qualities that modern democracy lacks.
