“Кнут и пряник для украинского языка”

I recently expressed my opposition to the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from the territory of Ukraine, which sparked a multitude of negative comments from readers, the majority of which boiled down to one phrase: “if you don’t like it, leave.” Only a few inquired about what exactly I am advocating for, what my constructive approach is. I am glad that there were still those who managed to see an unspoken call for real actions behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and continues to be the dire situation of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire, and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, as an empire exists precisely to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is natural that the development of Ukrainian culture, as well as its hindrance, were greatly influenced by Russia and the Soviet Union’s administrative subordination, although not always negatively.

The culture of Russia belongs to dozens of peoples of the West and East. It is on this basis, on a multinational soil, that it has grown in all its uniqueness. Dmitry Likhachev

Significantly less good literature has been written in the Ukrainian language than in Russian. There have been few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the homeland. Moscow has been taking talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors to itself for many years. They happily moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol moved from the Poltava Province to St. Petersburg at the age of 19. But would “The Inspector General” have been written if he had stayed in his hometown of Nezhin?

Culture consists of talents. And talent, first of all, seeks a ground on which it will be most comfortable to grow and where it will be appreciated. Rich and developed St. Petersburg was much more attractive at that time for a young and talented writer than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive to young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kyiv, Berlin, or Delhi. People from all over the world strive to go there if they have ambitions and a desire to fulfill them. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

So Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving away its talents to Moscow. And this, undoubtedly, is offensive. It would probably be great if the novel “The Master and Margarita” was written in Ukrainian, as Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kiev, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 30; or if the famous “Black Square” was created in Kiev, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth until he moved to Moscow at the age of 25; or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky sang “Lilac Negro” in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kiev, where he moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

However, none of this happened.

Ukrainian culture has suffered many losses and fallen significantly behind, just like many other cultures, on account of which Russian culture was created.

Do we want to fix this situation? Definitely. But doing it by police methods, which the current Ukrainian authorities confidently adopt, by forbidding the Russian language, books, and movies, only sets up those very talents against the Ukrainian language and culture, talents that should help bring them up to the Russian level.

Where there is a lot of politics, there is no culture. Ivan Bunin

Police bans are disgusting, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and not only me, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here’s what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not just from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

Books. In Russia, there are many awards for writers, including the Russian Booker with a main prize of $20,000 (a small amount for such a big country, but still). And in Ukraine, the Lesya Ukrainka Prize of 10,000 hryvnias ceased to exist ten years ago, and practically there is nothing else. If, for example, an annual prize for the best book in Ukrainian language with a prize fund of $200,000 is announced, I think even Vladimir Sorokin will switch to Ukrainian. There is no need to spend money on fighting the “Russian chimera”, give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved - we will start reading them.

Schools. Instead of prohibiting teaching in Russian, we should encourage teaching in Ukrainian. Let’s pay teachers twice as much if they conduct classes and lectures in Ukrainian. Let’s provide scholarships to students who defend their diplomas and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not demanding and forcing, but motivating and encouraging. Some may ignore this call, but for others, it will be effective, and we will gain more genuine desire to write in the national language.

Advertising. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but rather encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, tax incentives can be introduced for enterprises that advertise themselves in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the businesses - to lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian or to pay more taxes and advertise in their native language, which is Russian for many. Businesses and their customers will decide what is more beneficial for them. The country, on the other hand, will gain organic growth in speakers of the Ukrainian language.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke in Ukrainian, it would be possible to celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But this will not happen until he himself wants to. It is impossible to make him do it. As I think, he is a journalist who respects himself enough not to stoop to Ukrainian populism, as Saakashvili does. So how can we motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a language that is not native to them, without fearing a loss in quality? I think, nothing. Language is their main tool and commodity, and their popularity directly depends on its quality. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for that, we must guarantee freedom of speech to this new young generation. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and from childhood, they will write in Ukrainian. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

Documents. In Ukraine today, there is only one official language, and it doesn’t make sense to change this situation. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to independently decide in which language it is more convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolaev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhgorod, it may be Hungarian. Undoubtedly, some documents of national importance, such as passports, should be in Ukrainian. But such “global” documents are extremely few. Such freedom of language choice will only add respect for their country and its official language to the citizens of Ukraine, as the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

Banks. Forcing banks to conduct documentation with clients in Ukrainian in regions such as Odessa, Nikolaev, or Dnipro, literally means hindering them from communicating with those who owe them money or to whom they owe money. Instead, if we want the population to transition to the Ukrainian language in business communication, let’s provide banks that exclusively document in Ukrainian, for example, with state-subsidized loans at preferential rates. The choice will be up to the client: go to a bank where all the paperwork is in Ukrainian and receive better terms there, or be served where the documentation is in their native language but more expensive. I will choose the latter, but only for now. If the cost difference is significant, I will consider it and with genuine interest, I will start learning Ukrainian. I will understand why I need to do it. Not because there is no other way, but because I will see a real financial motive.

Cinema. There is still a shortage of Ukrainian cinema, and it is still of very low quality. To act in the same way as with books, by establishing serious state awards for good Ukrainian cinema, is the first obvious solution. The second is, of course, the organization of film festivals. In Russia, there are plenty of them, while in Ukraine, they are significantly fewer and the quality is considerably lower. The third is the state funds for film producers. For the country, these are insignificant expenses, but for the Ukrainian film industry, it is a huge support.

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there will be no such rejection as the one caused by Ukrainization now.

I really hope that Ukraine will follow the path described above.
