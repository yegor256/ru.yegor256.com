I am writing this article based on the results of my trip to small Russian cities, namely: Penza, Samara, and Novosibirsk. It left a depressing impression, especially after Moscow. Poverty and dirt. However, there is absolutely no feeling that these cities are inhabited by stupid and lazy people who deserve such a life. Rather, it seems like “they are not idle and could live differently.” What is the matter? Why do people in the third Russian city barely make ends meet while working two shifts, and why are there dirt and potholes on their streets? How are they different from Muscovites or, let’s say, residents of Zurich?

The answer is obvious: they steal! However, it’s not that simple.

And in Penza and in Zurich, very similar people live, most of them have eight working hours a day to create goods and services. Each Swiss produces approximately $65,000 worth of these goods and services per year, while the average Russian is slightly less, only $29,000. Twice as less. Have you been to Zurich? How about Penza? The Swiss autobahn and the road from Penza airport to its center is not “twice as less,” it is two big differences.

Apparently, the issue is not laziness and negligence, but rather theft. But who? Under the cover of night, burglars persistently break into Penza’s storerooms, steal all the valuables created by hardworking people, and disappear in an unknown direction. Unlucky residents of Penza wake up in the morning, discover broken locks, sigh, return to lathes and sewing machines, and continue working. And so it repeats year after year.

No, they don’t steal in that way. The robbers of Penza do not need the night to take away a large part of the GDP produced by its residents. They act during the day, openly, without much restraint or fear. Because they are the residents of Penza. More precisely, its best representatives.

The reason for the dirt and poverty is not in theft, but in the unbalanced distribution of earned resources, despite such a high GDP. $65,000 earned by a Swiss person in a year will primarily be spent on themselves, their family, and road repairs in front of their house. Some of it will go to officials, capitalists, and other freeloaders who do not create the product themselves but only own the means of its creation, capital, and land. However, this portion will be significantly less than what the average Russian gives to their owners.

At the very beginning of perestroika, a film was made called “Jack Vosmerkin - the American”, whose hero tried to build a business in young Soviet Russia. On his plot of land, he planted tobacco, his family helped it grow, and he decided to start a advertising campaign by giving out free cigars to his fellow villagers as a “trial”. The whole village gladly smoked the free promotional goods, but refused to buy the products outright, mainly citing the reason that “neighbors don’t do this to each other”. They were categorically outraged by Jack’s desire to profit from his fellow unfortunate comrades - people in the same dire circumstances.

Then the most interesting thing happened. Jack, having no means of subsistence and being rejected by the entire market, gave his tobacco for a pittance to the local bully Skorokhodov, who ground it into snuff and began selling it to the same poor people who immediately lined up. No more protests were heard.

Why is that? What is the difference between Jack, who grew tobacco, and Skorokhodov, who only resold it? Why were people willing to receive only free gifts from Jack, while there was no doubt that Skorokhodov needed to be paid?

The difference between them is huge - they come from different social classes. Jack is just as poor as they are. Skorokhodov is their owner. In the minds of the characters in this comedy, despite the revolution and profound social changes of that time, the formula of social “justice” has forever remained: we work, the owner gets rich. And if any American tries to question this dogma, even if it is in our favor, we will all stand up to defend it. Skorokhodov didn’t have to do anything. His interests as a capitalist owner were protected by the entire village.

Something similar is happening in modern Russia, Ukraine, and many other countries, where people are accustomed to wealthy and all-powerful owners who “rightfully” own the majority of all the products and services that these people create. In other words, the whole city of Penza works for a small number of people who are called thieves in the opposition press, but whose interests the entire city will rush to defend if their right to own the city is ever questioned.

It’s not about stealing. No one steals anything, although technically the actions of officials can almost always be classified as such. The matter lies in the natural and historically inherited desire of the entire nation for social inequality and material injustice.

Can this formula be changed to bring our consciousness closer to the consciousness of the average Swiss, for whom it is not a misunderstanding or a threat that an enriching fellow villager is? This is an interesting question. However, a much more interesting question is - is it necessary?
