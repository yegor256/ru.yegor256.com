“Совесть в балаклавах”

During the inauguration of the 45th President of the United States, Donald Trump, protesters took to the streets of Washington, and 217 of them were detained. They came out with placards, but ended up overturning garbage bins, smashing shop windows, and setting cars on fire. Many of them were dressed in black and acted somewhat organized. Regardless of our opinions about Trump and his policies, explosions and fires, people in masks, and the wailing of police sirens scare us. We rely on the reliability of the police. However, there is also a downside to this phenomenon.

Since the time of the Spartacus Uprising, uprisings against authority have been occurring regularly on this planet. The goal is always the same - freedom. The motives are always different. For example, economic - a rise in taxes preceding the uprising led by Stepan Razin (although there are doubts); political, like the Romanian Revolution - the communist regime was replaced by pro-Western democracy; ethnic and national, like in Nagorno-Karabakh or Kosovo; and there are also purely forceful, like the October Revolution - the revolution was necessary for the Bolshevik party, and they carried it out.

According to Latynina’s concise expression, “there are much more revolutions in the world than democracies”. Indeed, the majority of uprisings do not lead to an improvement in the lives of those on behalf of whom they arise. Although new people come to power, the order remains absolutely or almost the same. The recent Ukrainian revolutions on Maidan, both the Orange Revolution and the Euromaidan, are a vivid illustration of this.

And it’s not the rebels, revolutionaries, and terrorists with armor and Molotov cocktails in their hands to blame. And not even the Robespierres who lead and inspire them. We are to blame, the very majority that cannot be changed by simply changing the flag and declaring freedom. Remember how Lancelot tried to kill the dragon in the famous film by Mark Zakharov, but it was born again because that’s what the “best people of the city” demanded.

Freedom cannot be given to a person if they are not ready to be free. And if they are ready, then it is impossible to enslave them. They don’t need freedom, they already have it. Such is the paradox. Nietzsche said it concisely: “Rebellion is the virtue of a slave.”

So, revolutions, uprisings, riots, burning tires, snipers on rooftops, drunken students on piles of rebar, broken shop windows, and burned buses - all of this doesn’t make any sense and won’t change anything anyway? Apparently, smart people should respect the authorities, stay at home, watch TV series, not go out to Maidan with pots on their heads, and express their political demands through democratic means, dropping a ballot in the voting urn every few years.

Yes, it is possible. However, think about what the world would be like without Pugacheva, Danton, Muravyov, Bakunin, Nechaev, Trotsky, Castro, Dudayev, Saakashvili, and many others who opposed the authorities, risking their lives above all else. Some were defeated by the authorities, some were exterminated, some managed to defeat and in turn become the authorities to fight new revolutionaries and revolutions. Individual practical achievements of each uprising are not important, although there are many of them, and without them we would not have pensions, weekends, paid vacations, freedom of speech, human rights, equality, or justice. Something else is important.

It is important that there is always a spirit of disobedience to authority and readiness for rebellion in society. As Jean Jaurès said, “a revolution is only possible where there is conscience.” These semi-drunk young people in [balaclavas](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%91%D0%B0%D0%BB%D0%B0%D0%BA%D0%BB%D0%B0%D0%B2%D0%B0%28%D0%B3%D0%BE%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%BD%D0%BE%D0%B9%D1%83%D0%B1%D0%BE%D1%80%29], who are smashing the windows of our beloved restaurants and painting graffiti on the walls of our homes, represent our conscience. Without them, we would be slaves.

Victor Hugo believed that “revolution is a return from the fake to the real,” apparently understanding “the real” as our ability and desire to be free.

Do not rush to condemn the rebels for broken windows and overturned trash bins. Perhaps, in a few hundred years, your great-grandchildren will be grateful to them for their freedom.
