“Совесть в балаклавах”

During the inauguration of the 45th President of the United States, Donald Trump, protesters took to the streets of Washington, with 217 of them being detained. They came out with placards, but ended up overturning trash bins, smashing shop windows, and setting cars on fire. Many of them were dressed in black and acted somewhat organized. Regardless of our opinion on Trump and his policies, we are frightened by the explosions and fires, people in masks, and the wailing sirens of the police. We rely on the reliability of the police. However, there is also a flip side to this phenomenon.

Since the time of the Spartacus uprising against the authorities, uprisings regularly occur on this planet. The goal is always the same - freedom. The motives are always different. For example, economic - a rise in taxes that preceded the uprising led by Stepan Razin (although there are doubts); political, like the Romanian Revolution - the communist regime was replaced by pro-Western democracy; ethnic and national, like in Nagorno-Karabakh or Kosovo; and there are also purely forceful ones, like the October Revolution - the revolution was necessary for the Bolshevik Party, and they carried it out.

According to Latynina’s accurate expression, “there are much more revolutions in the world than democracies”. Indeed, the majority of uprisings do not lead to an improvement in the lives of those for whom they are raised. Although new people come to power, the orders remain absolutely or almost the same. The recent Ukrainian revolutions on the Maidan, both the orange and dignity, are a vivid illustration of this.

And the blame is not on rebels, revolutionaries, and terrorists with armor and Molotov cocktails in their hands. And not even on the Robespierres leading and inspiring them. We are to blame, that very majority, which cannot be changed by simply changing the flag and declaring freedom. Remember how Lancelot tried to kill the dragon in the famous film by Mark Zakharov, but it was reborn because that is what the “best people of the city” demanded.

Freedom cannot be given to a person if they are not ready to be free. And if they are ready, then enslaving them is impossible. They don’t need freedom, they already have it. Such is the paradox. Nietzsche put it concisely: “Rebellion is the courage of a slave.”

Therefore, revolutions, uprisings, riots, burning tires, snipers on rooftops, drunk students on rebar mountains, broken shop windows, and burnt buses - all of this is meaningless and will not change anything anyway? Yes, it is possible. However, think about what the world would be like without Pugacheva, Danton, Muravyov, Bakunin, Nechaev, Trotsky, Castro, Dudayev, Saakashvili, and many others who stood against authority, risking their lives above all. Some were defeated by power, some were exterminated, and some managed to win and, in turn, become authorities to fight new revolutionaries and revolutions. Individual practical achievements of each uprising are not important, although there are many of them, and without them, we would not have pensions, weekends, paid vacations, freedom of speech, human rights, equality, or justice. Something else is important.

It is important that there is always a spirit of disobedience to authority and readiness for rebellion in society. As Jean Jaurès said, “a revolution is only possible where there is conscience.” It is precisely these semi-drunk young people wearing balaclavas, smashing the windows of our beloved restaurants and painting graffiti on the walls of our homes, who represent our conscience. Without them, we would be slaves.
