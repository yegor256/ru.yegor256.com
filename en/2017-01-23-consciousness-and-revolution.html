“Совесть в балаклавах”

During the inauguration of the 45th President of the United States, Donald Trump, protesters took to the streets of Washington, and 217 of them were detained. They came out with posters, but ended up overturning trash bins, breaking windows, and setting cars on fire. Many of them were dressed in black and acted relatively organized. Regardless of our opinion of Trump and his policies, we are frightened by explosions and fires, people in masks, and the wail of police sirens. We rely on the reliability of the police. However, there is also a downside to this phenomenon.

Since the times of the Spartacus uprising against the authorities regularly occur on this planet. The goal is always the same - freedom. The motives are always different. For example, economic - the increase in taxes that preceded the uprising of Stepan Razin (although there are doubts); political, as in the Romanian Revolution - the communist regime was replaced by pro-Western democracy; ethnic and national, as in Nagorno-Karabakh or Kosovo; and there are also purely power-driven ones, like the October Revolution - the revolution was necessary for the Bolshevik party, and they carried it out.

According to Latynina’s accurate expression, “there are much more revolutions in the world than democracies”. Indeed, the majority of uprisings do not lead to an improvement in the lives of those for whom they rise. Although new people come to power, the orders remain absolutely or almost the same. The recent Ukrainian revolutions on Maidan, both the Orange Revolution and EuroMaidan, are a vivid illustration of this.

And it’s not the rebels, revolutionaries, and terrorists with weapons and Molotov cocktails in their hands to blame. And not even the Robespierres who lead and inspire them. We are to blame, the majority, who cannot change anything with just a change of flag and a declaration of freedom. Remember how Lancelot tried to kill the dragon in the famous movie by Mark Zakharov, but it was reborn because that’s what the “best people of the city” demanded.

Freedom cannot be given to a person if they are not ready to be free. And if they are ready, it is impossible to enslave them. They do not need freedom, they already have it. Such is the paradox. Nietzsche put it more succinctly: “Rebellion is the virtue of a slave.”

Therefore, revolutions, uprisings, riots, burning tires, snipers on rooftops, drunken students on piles of rebar, broken windows, and burnt buses - all of this is meaningless and will not change anything anyway? Apparently, intelligent people should respect authority, stay at home, watch TV series, not go to Maidan Square with pots on their heads, and express their political demands through democratic means, dropping a ballot into the voting box every few years.

Yes, it’s possible. However, think about what the world would be like without Pugacheva, Danton, Muravyov, Bakunin, Nechaev, Trotsky, Castro, Dudayev, Saakashvili, and many others who stood up against power, risking their lives above all else. Some were defeated by power, some were annihilated, some managed to win and in turn become the power to fight against new revolutionaries and revolutions. Individual practical achievements of each uprising are not important, although there are many of them, and without them, we would not have pensions, weekends, paid vacations, freedom of speech, human rights, equality, or justice. Something else is important.

It is important that there is always a spirit of disobedience to authority and readiness for rebellion in society. As Jean Jaurès said, “a revolution is only possible where there is conscience”. It is precisely these semi-drunk young people in balaclavas who represent our conscience, smashing the windows of our beloved restaurants and graffitiing the walls of our homes. Without them, we would be slaves.

Victor Hugo believed that “revolution is a return from the fake to the real,” apparently understanding “the real” as our ability and desire to be free.

Do not rush to condemn the rebels for broken windows and overturned trash bins. Perhaps, in a few hundred years, your great-grandchildren will be grateful to them for their freedom.
