“Моя ли это страна?”

A few days ago, an American acquaintance of mine asked me if I’m worried about my country due to its difficult situation. He was referring to the war in Donbass, the current economic condition of Ukraine, and all of it combined as seen from across the ocean. I replied that I don’t consider this country as my own, so I don’t have much to worry about. He was surprised. Patriotism seems to be in the blood of Americans. Apparently, I don’t have it. But should I?

Culture. Many years ago, in school, during the times of the Soviet Union, I was taught to be proud of my homeland and its culture. And there was something to be proud of. In the ranking of the world’s best writers, poets, composers, artists, or directors, Russia always held leading positions, which the Soviet Union successfully inherited. Now I have a different homeland imposed on me, whose culture does not occupy any serious positions in any rankings. Instead of Dostoevsky, Bulgakov, and Pelevin, they “forced” me to accept third-rate Shevchenko, Karpenko-Karyi, and Zhadan. These are not “my” writers, I don’t want to read them and I can’t be proud of them.

В борьбе с культурой народ на нашей стороне. Аркадий Давидович

Language. The country increasingly speaks Ukrainian, a language foreign to me. Moreover, I am forbidden from using my native Russian language in official communication with government agencies, when concluding contracts at the bank, when watching movies at the cinema, when listening to news on central television channels, and even when consuming advertisements. In my own country, I feel like a national minority, whose interests, as accurately noted by Russian propaganda, are increasingly being violated. The country in which I was born and raised did not speak it. This language is being imposed on me. It is not “my” language.

Information. In my country, I am partially prohibited from using the Internet. Just like in Iran, access to Twitter is blocked, and in China, access to Facebook is blocked. In my country, access to Vk.com, Ok.ru, Auto.ru, and other websites is blocked. Just as the KGB used to jam “Radio Liberty” for my parents, now they “jam” Vkontakte for me and prohibit undesirable literature. I cannot consider this country mine if it treats my constitutional right to information like this. This is not my country.

Politics. Whether it’s the progressing degradation of the population or the skillful actions of the ruling elite in recent years have raised anti-Russian sentiments to the level of absurdity, and mass patriotism to the level of idiocy. As a result, in practical terms, this has resulted in the loss of Crimea and Donbass, and for me personally, the inability to fly directly from Ukraine to Russia. Now a train ticket from Odessa to Moscow costs 7130 hryvnias (~$285), and a plane ticket from Odessa to Minsk to Moscow costs $153. I cannot find any logic in this mathematics, just like I cannot find it in the fact that entering Crimea is now a criminal offense. I do not want to live by these new rules, and I cannot consider them “mine”. I can only endure them.

Power. The country is ruled by blatant criminals, chosen by an openly foolish people. My people regularly gather in squares to forcibly change the government and redistribute property or financial flows. I cannot respect such behavior and do not want to consider that people “mine” who are not truly fighting for their freedom. I also have no respect for a government that robs and humiliates its people. This is not my government and not my people.

And I’m not even talking about the fact that the population of my country is on the brink of extinction due to poverty primarily caused by corruption. I’m not talking about the fact that my people are one of the world leaders in drug consumption. I’m not talking about the fact that there are only a few countries in Africa with a more impoverished healthcare system than Ukraine. With all of this, perhaps it would be possible to fight and maybe even win. But only if it was “my” country.

I do not consider Ukraine as such, although formally I am still its citizen.

К сожалению.
