“Моя ли это страна?”

A few days ago, an American acquaintance of mine asked if I am concerned about my country due to its difficult situation. He meant the war in Donbass, the current economic state of Ukraine, and everything taken together as it appears from across the ocean. I replied that I don’t consider this country as my own, so there is not much to worry about. He was surprised. Patriotism seems to be in the blood of Americans. Apparently, I don’t have it. But should I?

Culture. Many years ago, in school, during the times of the Soviet Union, I was taught to be proud of my Motherland and its culture. And there was something to be proud of. In the ranking of the world’s best writers, poets, composers, artists, or directors, Russia always held leading positions, which the Soviet Union successfully inherited. Now I have a different, imposed on me, Motherland, whose culture does not occupy any significant positions in any rankings. Instead of Dostoevsky, Bulgakov, and Pelevin, they “offered” me third-rate Shevchenko, Karpenko-Karyi, and Zhadan. These are not “my” writers, I don’t want to read them and I can’t be proud of them.

В борьбе с культурой народ на нашей стороне. Аркадий Давидович

Language. The country is increasingly speaking Ukrainian, a language foreign to me. Moreover, I am prohibited from using my native Russian language in official communication with government agencies, when signing contracts at the bank, when watching movies at the cinema, when listening to news on central television channels, and even when consuming advertisements. In my own country, I feel like a national minority, whose interests, as accurately noted by Russian propaganda, are increasingly being violated. The country in which I was born and raised did not speak this language. This language is being imposed on me. It is not “my” language.

Information. In my country, I am prohibited from using the Internet, at least partially. Just like in Iran, access to Twitter is blocked, and in China, access to Facebook is blocked. In my country, access to Vk.com, Ok.ru, Auto.ru, and other websites is also blocked. Just as the KGB used to jam “Radio Liberty” for my parents, now they are “jamming” Vkontakte for me and prohibiting me from accessing dissident literature. I cannot consider this country mine if it treats my constitutional right to information in this way. This is not my country.

Politics. Whether it’s the progressing degradation of the population or the skillful actions of the ruling elite in recent years that have raised anti-Russian sentiments to absurd levels, and mass patriotism to the level of idiocy. As a result, in practical terms, this has resulted in the loss of Crimea and Donbass, and personally for me, the impossibility of flying from Ukraine to Russia on direct flights. Now, a train ticket from Odessa to Moscow costs 7130 hryvnias (~$285), and a plane ticket from Odessa to Minsk to Moscow costs $153. I can’t find any logic in this mathematics, just as I can’t find it in the fact that entry into Crimea is now a criminal offense. I don’t want to live according to these new rules, and I can only tolerate them.

Power. In the country, power is held by blatant criminals, chosen by an openly foolish people. My people regularly gather in squares to forcefully change the government and redistribute property or financial flows. I cannot respect such behavior and do not want to consider that people “mine” who do not truly fight for their freedom. I also do not respect the power that robs and humiliates its own people. This is not my power and not my people.

And I’m not even talking about the fact that the population of my country is on the verge of extinction due to poverty mainly caused by corruption. I’m not talking about the fact that my people are one of the leaders in the world in terms of drug consumption. I’m not talking about the fact that there are only a few countries in Africa with a more impoverished healthcare system than in Ukraine. With all this, perhaps it would be possible to fight and perhaps even win. But only under the condition that it would be “my” country.

I do not consider Ukraine as such, although formally I am still its citizen.

К сожалению.
