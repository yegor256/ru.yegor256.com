“Моя ли это страна?”

A few days ago, an American acquaintance of mine asked me if I am worried about my country due to its difficult situation. He was referring to the war in Donbass, the current economic state of Ukraine, and everything taken together, as it appears to them from across the ocean. I replied that I do not consider this country as my own, so there is not much to worry about. He was surprised. Patriotism seems to be in the blood of Americans. Apparently, I do not have it. Should I?

Culture. Many years ago, in school, during the times of the Soviet Union, I was taught to be proud of my homeland and its culture. And there was something to be proud of. In the rankings of the best global writers, poets, composers, artists, or directors, Russia always held leading positions, which the Soviet Union successfully inherited. Now I have a different, imposed homeland, whose culture does not hold any significant positions in any rankings. Instead of Dostoevsky, Bulgakov, and Pelevin, I was “given” third-rate Shevchenko, Karpenko-Karyi, and Zhadan. These are not “my” writers, I do not want to read them and cannot be proud of them.

Language. The country increasingly speaks Ukrainian, a language foreign to me. Moreover, I am prohibited from using my native Russian language in official communication with government agencies, when signing contracts at the bank, when watching movies at the cinema, when listening to news on central television channels, and even when consuming advertisements. In my own country, I feel like a national minority, whose interests, as Russian propaganda accurately notes, are increasingly being infringed upon. The country in which I was born and raised did not speak this language. This language is being imposed on me. It is not “my” language.

Information. In my country, I am partially prohibited from using the Internet. Just like in Iran, access to Twitter is blocked, and in China, access to Facebook is banned, in my country, access to Vk.com, Ok.ru, Auto.ru, and other websites is blocked. Just as the KGB used to jam “Radio Liberty” for my parents, now Vkontakte is being jammed for me and I am prohibited from accessing undesirable literature. I cannot consider this country mine if it treats my constitutional right to information this way. This is not my country.

Politics. Whether it is the progressing degradation of the population or the skillful actions of the ruling elite in recent years, anti-Russian sentiments have reached absurd levels, and mass patriotism has reached the level of idiocy. As a result, in practical terms, this has resulted in the loss of Crimea and Donbass, and personally for me, the impossibility of flying directly from Ukraine to Russia. Now a train ticket from Odessa to Moscow costs 7130 hryvnias (~$285), and a plane ticket from Odessa to Minsk to Moscow costs $153. I cannot find any logic in this mathematics, just as I cannot find it in the fact that entry into Crimea is now considered a criminal offense. I do not want to live by these new rules, and I cannot consider them “mine.” I can only tolerate them.

Power. The country is ruled by blatant criminals, chosen by an openly foolish people. My people regularly gather in squares to forcibly change power and redistribute property or cash flows. I cannot respect such behavior and do not want to consider that people “mine” who do not really fight for their freedom. I also do not respect the power that robs and humiliates its people. This is not my power and not my people.

And I’m not even talking about the fact that the population of my country is on the brink of extinction due to poverty, mainly because of corruption. I’m not talking about the fact that my people are among the leaders in the world in terms of drug consumption. I’m not talking about the fact that there are only a few countries in Africa with a poorer healthcare system than Ukraine. With all of this, perhaps it would be possible to fight and probably even win. But only on the condition that it would be “my” country.

I do not consider Ukraine as such, although formally I am still its citizen.
