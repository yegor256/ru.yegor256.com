“Кнут и пряник для украинского языка”

I recently spoke out against the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from the territory of Ukraine, which sparked a lot of negative comments from readers, most of which boiled down to one: “if you don’t like it, leave.” And only a few were interested in what exactly I was calling for, what my constructive suggestions were. I am glad that there were still those who were able to see an unspoken call for real action behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and still is the disastrous state of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire, and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, an empire exists precisely to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. Naturally, Russia’s and the Soviet Union’s administrative subordination exerted a huge influence on the development of Ukrainian culture, as well as its hindrance, although not always negative.

There is significantly less good literature written in Ukrainian than in Russian. There have been few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the homeland. Moscow has been attracting talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors for many years. And they gladly moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol did when he moved from the Poltava Province to St. Petersburg at the age of 19. And would “The Government Inspector” have been written if he had stayed in his hometown of Nezhin?

Culture consists of talents. And talent first and foremost seeks soil on which it can comfortably grow and be appreciated. Rich and developed St. Petersburg was much more attractive to a young and talented writer at that time than Poltava or Kyiv.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive to young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kyiv, Berlin, or Delhi. People from all over the world strive to go there, those who have ambitions and a desire to realize them. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. The United States undoubtedly benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses.

Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving its talents to Moscow. And it is undoubtedly offensive. It would have been great if the novel “Master and Margarita” had been written in Ukrainian, as Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv before moving to Moscow at the age of 30. Or if the famous “Black Square” had been created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth before moving to Moscow at the age of 25. Or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky had sung “The Lilac Negro” in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kyiv before moving to Moscow at the age of 24.

However, none of this happened.

Ukrainian culture has lost a lot and fallen far behind, just like many other cultures that were overshadowed by Russian culture.

Do we want to change this situation? Definitely. But doing it through “police methods,” which the current Ukrainian authorities are confidently adopting by banning the Russian language, books, and movies, is only antagonizing the very talents that should raise them to the Russian level.

Police bans are repulsive, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and I’m not the only one, that the Ukrainian government should act differently. Here’s what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not only from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

Books. In Russia, there are many literary awards for writers, including the Russian Booker with a main prize of $20,000 (a small amount for such a large country, but still). And in Ukraine, the Lesya Ukrainka Prize of 10,000 hryvnias ceased to exist ten years ago, and there is practically nothing else. If, for example, an annual prize for the best book in the Ukrainian language with a prize fund of $200,000 were announced, I think even Vladimir Sorokin would switch to Ukrainian. There’s no need to spend money on fighting the “Russian chimera,” give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved - we will start reading them.

Schools. Instead of banning teaching in Russian, we should encourage teaching in Ukrainian. Let’s pay teachers twice as much if they teach lessons and give lectures in Ukrainian. Let’s provide scholarships to students who defend their theses and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not demand and force, but motivate and encourage. Some may ignore this call, but for others, it will be effective, and we will get more genuine desire to write in the national language.

Advertising. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, we can introduce tax incentives for those enterprises that advertise themselves in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the business - to lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian or pay more taxes and advertise in the language that is native to many, Russian. Businesses and their customers will decide what is more profitable. The country will receive organic growth in Ukrainian language speakers.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke in Ukrainian, we could celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But this will not happen until he wants to. It is impossible to force him. He, as I see it, is a self-respecting journalist enough not to stoop to Ukrainian populism, as Saakashvili does. How can we motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a language that is not native to them, without fearing a loss in quality? I think there is nothing we can do. Language is their main tool and commodity; their popularity directly depends on its quality. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for that, we must guarantee this new young generation freedom of speech. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and will write in Ukrainian from childhood. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

Documents. In Ukraine today, there is one official language, and changing this situation would not make sense. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to decide in which language it is more convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolaev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhgorod, maybe Hungarian. Of course, some documents of national importance, such as passports, should be in Ukrainian. But such “global” documents are extremely few. This freedom of language choice will only increase the respect of Ukrainian citizens for their country and its official language because the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

Banks. Forcing banks to conduct documentation with clients in Ukrainian in regions like Odessa, Nikolaev, or Dnipro literally means preventing them from communicating with those who owe them money or to whom they owe money. Instead, if we want the population to switch to Ukrainian in business communication, let’s give preferential state loans to banks that fully document only in Ukrainian, for example. The choice will be up to the client: to go to a bank where all documents are in Ukrainian and get better conditions there, or to be served where documentation is in their native language, but everything is more expensive. I will choose the second option, but only for now. If the price difference is significant, I will think about it and with genuine interest start learning Ukrainian. I will understand why I need it, not because there is no way out, but because I will see a real financial motive.

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block the Russian, but to develop the Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there will be no such rejection as the Ukrainianization currently causes.

I really hope that Ukraine will follow the path described above.
