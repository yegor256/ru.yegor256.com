“Кнут и пряник для украинского языка”

I recently expressed my opposition to the forced ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from the territory of Ukraine, which triggered a lot of negative comments from readers, the majority of which boiled down to one: “if you don’t like it, leave.” Only a few were curious about what exactly I am calling for, what is my constructive suggestion? I am glad that there were still those who managed to see an unexpressed call for real action behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991 and up until now, has been and remains the dire situation of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire, and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, an empire exists precisely to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is only natural that Russia and the Soviet Union had a huge influence on the development and hindrance of Ukrainian culture due to their administrative subordination, although not always negative.

Культура России принадлежит десяткам народов Запада и Востока. Именно на этой основе, на многонациональной почве, она выросла во всем своем своеобразии. Дмитрий Лихачев

Significantly less good literature is written in Ukrainian than in Russian. There are few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the homeland. Moscow has been attracting talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors for many years. And they gladly moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol, who moved from the Poltava province to St. Petersburg at the age of 19. And would “The Inspector General” have been written if he had stayed in his hometown of Nizhyn?

Culture consists of talents. And talent, first of all, seeks the ground on which it will be most comfortable to grow and where it will be appreciated. At that time, the rich and developed St. Petersburg was significantly more attractive to a young and talented writer than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive to young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kiev, Berlin, or Delhi. From all over the world, those who have ambitions and a desire to fulfill them strive to go there. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

So Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving away its talents to Moscow. And it is undoubtedly offensive. It would probably be great if the novel “Master and Margarita” had been written in Ukrainian, as Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 30; or if the famous “Black Square” had been created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth before moving to Moscow at the age of 25; or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky had sung “The Lilac Negro” in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

Однако, всего этого не произошло.

Ukrainian culture has suffered a lot and fallen far behind, as many other cultures have fallen behind, thanks to which Russian culture was created.

Do we want to fix this situation? Definitely. But doing it “by police methods”, which the current Ukrainian government confidently adopts, banning the Russian language, books, and movies — only sets up those very talents that should bring them to the Russian level, against the Ukrainian language and culture.

Где много политики, там нет культуры. Иван Бунин

Police bans are disgusting, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and I am not the only one, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here’s what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not only from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

Books. In Russia, there are many awards for writers, including the Russian Booker with a main prize of $20,000 (a small amount for such a large country, but still). And in Ukraine, the Lesya Ukrainka Award with a prize of 10,000 hryvnias ceased to exist ten years ago, and practically nothing else remains. If, for example, an annual prize for the best book in Ukrainian with a prize fund of $200,000 is announced, I think even Vladimir Sorokin will switch to Ukrainian. There is no need to spend money fighting the “Russian chimera,” give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved - we will start reading them.

Schools. It would be appropriate not to prohibit teaching in Russian, but to encourage teaching in Ukrainian. Let’s pay teachers twice as much if they conduct lessons and give lectures in Ukrainian. Let’s provide scholarships to those students who defend their diplomas and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not to demand and force, but to motivate and encourage. Someone will ignore this call, but for someone it will prove effective, and we will gain more sincere desire to write in the state language.

Advertising. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but rather encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, tax incentives could be introduced for enterprises that advertise themselves in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the business - to lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian or to pay higher taxes and advertise in the native language for many, Russian. The business and its clients will decide for themselves what is more advantageous. The country, however, will benefit from the organic growth of Ukrainian language speakers.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke Ukrainian, we could celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But this won’t happen until he himself wants it. It won’t be possible to force him to do so. He, as it seems to me, is a respected journalist enough not to stoop to Ukrainian populism, like Saakashvili does. What can motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a non-native language without fearing a loss in quality? I think nothing. Language is their main tool and commodity, and their popularity directly depends on its quality. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for this, we must guarantee freedom of speech to this new young generation. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and will write in Ukrainian from childhood. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

Documents. In Ukraine today, there is one official language, and it doesn’t make sense to change this situation. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to decide in which language it is more convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolaev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhhorod, it could be Hungarian. Undoubtedly, some documents of national importance, such as passports, should be in Ukrainian. But there are very few of such “global” documents. Such freedom of language choice will only add respect for their country and its official language to the citizens of Ukraine, as the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

Banks. Forcing banks to conduct documentation with clients in Ukrainian in regions such as Odessa, Nikolaev, or Dnipro, literally means hindering their communication with those who owe them money or to whom they owe money. Instead, if we want the population to switch to Ukrainian language in business communication, let’s give banks that fully document only in Ukrainian, for example, state loans at preferential rates. The choice will be up to the client: go to a bank where all the papers are in Ukrainian and get the best conditions there, or be served where the documentation is in their native language but everything is more expensive. I will choose the latter, but only for now. If the difference in cost is significant, I will consider and sincerely become interested in studying Ukrainian. I will understand why I need it. Not because there is no other option, but because I will see a real financial incentive.

Cinema. Ukrainian cinema is still scarce and of very low quality. Taking the same approach as with books, establishing serious state awards for good Ukrainian films is the first obvious solution. The second is, of course, organizing film festivals. There are plenty of them in Russia (source), while in Ukraine there are significantly fewer (source), and the quality is significantly lower. The third is state funds for film producers. For the country, this is insignificant expenditure, but for the Ukrainian film industry, it is significant support.

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there will be no such rejection as the Ukrainization is causing now.

Я очень надеюсь, что по описанному выше пути пойдет Украина.
