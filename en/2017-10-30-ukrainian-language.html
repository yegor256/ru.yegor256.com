“Кнут и пряник для украинского языка”

I recently expressed my opposition to the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from the territory of Ukraine, which sparked a lot of negative comments from readers, most of which boiled down to “if you don’t like it, leave.” Only a few were interested in what exactly I was calling for, what my constructive suggestion was. I’m glad that there were still those who were able to see an unspoken call to real actions behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and remains the disastrous situation of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire and then the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, as an empire exists precisely to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is natural that the administrative subordination of Russia and the Soviet Union had a huge influence on the development of Ukrainian culture, as well as on its hindrance, although not always negative.

Культура России принадлежит десяткам народов Запада и Востока. Именно на этой основе, на многонациональной почве, она выросла во всем своем своеобразии. Дмитрий Лихачев

There is significantly less good literature written in the Ukrainian language than in Russian. There are few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the Motherland. Moscow has been attracting talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors for many years. And they gladly moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol moved from Poltava Governorate to St. Petersburg at the age of 19. And would “The Inspector General” have been written if he had stayed in his native Nizhyn?

Culture consists of talents. And talent first of all seeks the ground on which it will be most comfortable to grow and where it will be appreciated. The rich and developed St. Petersburg was much more attractive to a young and talented writer at that time than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive to young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kiev, Berlin, or Delhi. Ambitious individuals with a desire to fulfill their ambitions strive to go there from all over the world. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

So Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving its talents to Moscow. And this is undoubtedly offensive. It would probably be great if the novel “The Master and Margarita” had been written in Ukrainian, because Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 30; or if the famous “Black Square” had been created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth before moving to Moscow at the age of 25; or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky had sung “Purple Negro” in Ukrainian, because he was originally from Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

However, none of this happened.

Ukrainian culture has suffered a lot and has fallen behind, like many other cultures, due to which Russian culture has been created.

Do we want to fix this situation? Definitely. But doing it with “police methods,” which the current Ukrainian government confidently adopts, banning the Russian language, books, and cinema — it only sets up those very talents against the Ukrainian language and culture, talents that should bring them up to the Russian level.

Где много политики, там нет культуры. Иван Бунин

Police bans are repugnant, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and I’m not the only one, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here’s what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not just from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

Books. In Russia, there are many literary awards for writers, including Russian Booker with the main prize of $20,000 (a small amount for such a big country, but still). And in Ukraine, the Lesya Ukrainka Prize of 10,000 hryvnias ceased to exist ten years ago, and there is practically nothing else. If, for example, an annual prize for the best book in Ukrainian language with a prize fund of $200,000 is announced, I think Vladimir Sorokin will switch to Ukrainian. There is no need to spend money fighting the “Russian chimera,” give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved — we will start reading them.

Schools. It should not be prohibited to teach in Russian, but to encourage teaching in Ukrainian. Let’s pay teachers twice as much if they conduct lessons and give lectures in Ukrainian. Let’s pay a scholarship to those students who defend their diplomas and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not to demand and force, but to motivate and encourage. Someone will ignore this call, but for someone, it will prove effective, and we will get more sincere desire to write in the state language.

Advertisement. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but rather encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, tax incentives can be introduced for enterprises that advertise themselves in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the business - to lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian or to pay higher taxes and advertise in their native Russian language, which is familiar to many. The business and its customers will decide what is more beneficial for them. The country, on the other hand, will gain organic growth in Ukrainian language speakers.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke Ukrainian, we could celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But that won’t happen until he wants to do it himself. It won’t be possible to force him to do it. He, in my opinion, is a journalist who respects himself enough not to stoop to Ukrainian populism, like Saakashvili does. So, what can motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a language that is not native to them, without fearing a loss in quality? I think nothing. Language is their main tool and commodity, and the quality of it directly affects their popularity. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for this, we must guarantee freedom of speech to this new young generation. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and will write in Ukrainian from childhood. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

Documents. In Ukraine today, there is only one official language, and it doesn’t make sense to change this situation. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to independently decide in which language it is more convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolaev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhgorod, it can be Hungarian. Undoubtedly, some documents of nationwide importance, such as passports, should be in Ukrainian. But such “global” documents are extremely few. This freedom to choose the language will only add respect for their country and its official language to the citizens of Ukraine, as the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

Banks. Forcing banks to conduct documentation with clients in Ukrainian in regions such as Odessa, Nikolayev, or Dnipro, literally means preventing them from communicating with those who owe them money or to whom they owe money. Instead, if we want the population to switch to Ukrainian in business communication, let’s give banks that fully document only in Ukrainian, for example, state loans at preferential rates. The choice will be up to the client: go to a bank where all the papers are in Ukrainian and get the best conditions there, or be served where the documentation is in their native language but everything is more expensive. I will choose the second option, but only for now. If the cost difference is significant, I will consider and genuinely be interested in learning Ukrainian. I will understand why I need it. Not because there is no way out, but because I will see a real financial motive.

Cinema. Ukrainian cinema is still scarce and of very low quality. Taking the same approach as with books, establishing serious state awards for good Ukrainian films is the first obvious solution. The second is, of course, organizing film festivals. There are plenty of them in Russia, but significantly fewer in Ukraine, and the quality is significantly lower. The third is state funds for film producers. For the country, these are insignificant expenses, but for the Ukrainian film industry, it is huge support.

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there will be no such rejection as the Ukrainization is causing now.

I really hope that Ukraine will follow the path described above.
