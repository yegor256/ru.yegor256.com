“Кнут и пряник для украинского языка”

I recently spoke out against the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from the territory of Ukraine, which caused a lot of negative comments from readers, most of which boiled down to one: “if you don’t like it - leave.” Only a few were interested in what exactly I was calling for, what is my constructive proposal? I am glad that there were still those who were able to see an unspoken call to real actions behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and continues to be the disastrous situation of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire, and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, as an empire exists precisely to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is natural that the administrative subordination to Russia and the Soviet Union had a huge influence on the development and hindrance of Ukrainian culture, although not always negative.

Культура России принадлежит десяткам народов Запада и Востока. Именно на этой основе, на многонациональной почве, она выросла во всем своем своеобразии. Дмитрий Лихачев

There is significantly less good literature written in Ukrainian than in Russian. There are few films made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the homeland. For many years, Moscow has attracted talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors. And they happily moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol moved from Poltava Province to St. Petersburg at the age of 19. And would “The Government Inspector” have been written if he had stayed in his hometown of Nezhin?

Culture consists of talents. And talent first of all seeks a ground on which it will be most comfortable to grow, and where it will be appreciated. Rich and developed St. Petersburg was much more attractive to a young and talented writer at that time than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive for young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kiev, Berlin, or Delhi. People from all over the world strive to go there if they have ambitions and a desire to fulfill them. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

So Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving its talents to Moscow. And it is undoubtedly offensive. It would probably be great if the novel “Master and Margarita” was written in Ukrainian because Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 30; or if the famous “Black Square” was created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth until he moved to Moscow at the age of 25; or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky sang “Lilac Negro” in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kyiv and moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

However, none of this happened.

Ukrainian culture has suffered a lot and fallen behind, like many other cultures, due to which Russian culture was created.

Do we want to fix this situation? Absolutely. But doing it “by police methods”, which the current Ukrainian government confidently adopts, by banning the Russian language, books, and movies — this only antagonizes the very talents that should bring them up to the Russian level.

Где много политики, там нет культуры. Иван Бунин

Police restrictions are disgusting, especially in the field of culture. I suppose, and not just me, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here’s what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not only from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

Books. In Russia, there are many awards for writers, including the Russian Booker with a main prize of $20,000 (a small amount for such a large country, but still). And in Ukraine, the Lesya Ukrainka Prize with a value of 10,000 hryvnias ceased to exist ten years ago, and practically nothing else exists. If, for example, an annual prize for the best book in Ukrainian with a prize fund of $200,000 is announced, I think even Vladimir Sorokin will switch to Ukrainian. There is no need to spend money fighting the “Russian chimera,” give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved - we will start reading them.

Schools. It should not be prohibited to teach in Russian, but to encourage teaching in Ukrainian. Let’s pay teachers twice as much if they conduct classes and give lectures in Ukrainian. Let’s grant scholarships to those students who defend their theses and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not demanding and forcing, but motivating and encouraging. Someone will ignore this call, but for someone it will be effective, and we will get more sincere desire to write in the state language.

Advertising. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but rather encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, tax benefits can be introduced for enterprises that advertise themselves in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the business: to lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian or to pay higher taxes and advertise in the native language of many, Russian. The business and its customers will decide for themselves what is more advantageous. The country, on the other hand, will gain organic growth in Ukrainian language speakers.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke Ukrainian, we could celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But this won’t happen until he himself wants it. It is impossible to force him to do so. He, as it seems to me, is a journalist who respects himself enough not to stoop to Ukrainian populism, like Saakashvili does. So, how can we motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a non-native language without fearing a loss in quality? I think there’s nothing we can do. Language is their main tool and commodity, and the quality of it directly affects their popularity. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for that, we must guarantee freedom of speech to this new young generation. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and will write in Ukrainian from childhood. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

Documents. In Ukraine today, there is one official language, and it doesn’t make sense to change this situation. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to decide in which language it is more convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolaev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhgorod, it could be Hungarian. Of course, some documents of national importance, such as passports, should be in Ukrainian. But there are very few such “global” documents. Such freedom of language choice will only add respect for their country and its official language to the citizens of Ukraine, as the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

Banks. Forcing banks to conduct documentation with clients in Ukrainian in regions such as Odessa, Nikolaev, or Dnipro, literally means hindering them from communicating with those who owe them money or to whom they owe money. Instead, if we want the population to switch to Ukrainian language in business communication, let’s provide banks that fully document in Ukrainian, for example, state loans at preferential rates. The choice will be up to the customer: go to a bank where all the documents are in Ukrainian and get better conditions there, or be served where the documentation is in their native language but more expensive. I will choose the second option, but only for now. If the cost difference is significant, I will consider and sincerely become interested in learning Ukrainian. I will understand why I need it. Not because there is no way out, but because I will see a real financial incentive.

Film. Ukrainian cinema is still scarce, and it is still of very low quality. To take the same approach as with books, establishing serious state awards for good Ukrainian cinema is the first obvious solution. The second is, of course, organizing film festivals. There are plenty of them in Russia, but significantly fewer in Ukraine, and the quality is significantly lower. The third is state funds for filmmakers. For the country, it’s insignificant spending, but for the Ukrainian film industry, it’s huge support.

I think the idea is clear. Not to forbid, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there won’t be such rejection as the Ukrainization is causing now.

I really hope that Ukraine will follow the path described above.
