“Кнут и пряник для украинского языка”

I recently expressed my opposition to the forced Ukrainization and displacement of Russian culture from the territory of Ukraine, which provoked a lot of negative comments from readers, most of which boiled down to one thing: “if you don’t like it, leave.” Only a few were interested in what exactly I am calling for, what is my constructive proposal? I am glad that there were still those who were able to see an unspoken call for real actions behind the criticism. Now I will express it.

The trump card of any Ukrainian government, starting from 1991, has been and remains the disastrous state of Ukraine as a result of its oppression by the Russian Empire, and later the USSR. It is difficult to argue with this, as an empire is meant to oppress the territories under its control. Ukraine, just like Poland, Finland, and the Caucasus, was such a territory for many years. It is completely natural that the administrative subordination to Russia and the Soviet Union had a tremendous influence on the development of Ukrainian culture, as well as its hindrance, although not always negative.

Культура России принадлежит десяткам народов Запада и Востока. Именно на этой основе, на многонациональной почве, она выросла во всем своем своеобразии. Дмитрий Лихачев

There is significantly less good literature written in Ukrainian than in Russian. Few films are made in Ukrainian, and most of them can only be watched out of great love for the homeland. Moscow has been taking talented scientists, artists, musicians, writers, and directors to itself for many years. And they happily moved there, just like Nikolai Gogol moved from Poltava province to St. Petersburg at the age of 19. And would “The Inspector General” have been written if he had stayed in his native Nezhin?

Culture consists of talents. And talent, above all, seeks a soil on which it will be most comfortable to grow and where it will be appreciated. At that time, the rich and developed St. Petersburg was significantly more attractive to a young and talented writer than Poltava or Kiev.

Today, Silicon Valley is more attractive to young and talented programmers than Moscow, Kiev, Berlin, or Delhi. People from all over the world strive to go there if they have ambitions and a desire to realize them. Those who have fewer ambitions or opportunities stay at home. Undoubtedly, the United States benefits from this, while the rest of the world loses out.

So Ukraine has been losing for many years, giving away its talents to Moscow. And this is undoubtedly offensive. It would probably be great if the novel “Master and Margarita” was written in Ukrainian, as Mikhail Bulgakov was born and raised in Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 30; or if the famous “Black Square” was created in Kyiv, where Kazimir Malevich spent his youth before moving to Moscow at the age of 25; or if, for example, Alexander Vertinsky sang “Lilac Negro” in Ukrainian, as he was originally from Kyiv, from where he moved to Moscow at the age of 24.

Однако, всего этого не произошло.

Ukrainian culture has lost a lot and fallen behind, like many other cultures, due to which Russian culture was created.

Do we want to fix this situation? Definitely. But doing it with “police methods”, which the current Ukrainian government is confidently adopting, by prohibiting the Russian language, books, and cinema - is only setting up against the Ukrainian language and culture those very talents that should bring them up to the Russian level.

Где много политики, там нет культуры. Иван Бунин

Police bans are disgusting, especially in the field of culture. I believe, and I am not the only one, that the Ukrainian authorities should act differently. Here’s what could be done if we wanted to hear the Ukrainian language more often, not just from politicians, TV presenters, and actors in mayonnaise commercials.

Books. In Russia, there are many awards for writers, including the Russian Booker with a grand prize of $20,000 (a small amount for such a large country, but still). And in Ukraine, the Lesya Ukrainka Prize worth 10,000 hryvnias ceased to exist ten years ago, and practically nothing else exists. If, for example, an annual prize of $200,000 is announced for the best book in the Ukrainian language, I think even Vladimir Sorokin will switch to Ukrainian. There is no need to spend money fighting with the “Russian chimera”, give writers an incentive to write in Ukrainian, and the problem will be solved - we will start reading them.

Schools. It should not be prohibited to teach in Russian, but teaching in Ukrainian should be encouraged. Let’s pay teachers twice as much if they conduct classes and deliver their lectures in Ukrainian. Let’s provide scholarships to those students who defend their diplomas and write dissertations in Ukrainian. Not demanding and forcing, but motivating and encouraging. Some may ignore this call, but for others, it will prove effective, and we will gain more sincere desire to write in the national language.

Advertisement. We should not prohibit businesses from advertising in their preferred language, but rather encourage them to do so in Ukrainian. For example, tax incentives can be introduced for enterprises that advertise themselves in Ukrainian. The choice will be up to the businesses – to lose customers who do not understand Ukrainian or to pay higher taxes and advertise in the native language for many, Russian. Businesses and their clients themselves will decide what is more beneficial for them. The country, in turn, will gain organic growth of Ukrainian language speakers.

Television. If Savik Shuster spoke Ukrainian, we could celebrate the victory of the Ukrainian language on Ukrainian television. But this will not happen until he himself wants to. It is impossible to force him to do so. He, in my opinion, is a respectable journalist who does not stoop to Ukrainian populism, like Saakashvili. So, what can motivate Shuster, Gordon, and other serious journalists to switch to a language that is not native to them, without being afraid of losing quality? I think nothing. Language is their main tool and commodity, and their popularity depends directly on its quality. The only thing we can do is motivate them to teach their children Ukrainian. And for this, we must guarantee freedom of speech to this new young generation. If Ukraine becomes a country where any social and political issues can be openly discussed, young journalists will see it as a territory for their professional growth and will write in Ukrainian from childhood. In other words, if you want more professional Ukrainian language on television, give freedom of speech.

Documents. In Ukraine today, there is one official language, and it doesn’t make sense to change this situation. Let there be one language, but let each administrative unit have the right to decide in which language it is more convenient to communicate with its residents. In Odessa and Nikolayev, it will be Russian, in Lviv - Ukrainian, and in Uzhhorod, it may be Hungarian. Undoubtedly, some documents of national importance, such as passports, should be in Ukrainian. But such “global” documents are extremely rare. Such freedom of language choice will only add respect for their country and its official language to the citizens of Ukraine, as the element of coercion in relations with the state will disappear.

Banks. Forcing banks to conduct documentation with clients in Ukrainian in regions such as Odesa, Mykolaiv, or Dnipro, literally means hindering them from communicating with those who owe them money or to whom they owe money. Instead, if we want the population to switch to Ukrainian language in business communication, let’s give banks that fully document only in Ukrainian, for example, government loans at preferential rates. The choice will be up to the client: to go to a bank where all the paperwork is in Ukrainian and get better conditions there, or to be served where the documentation is in their native language but more expensive. I will choose the latter, but only for now. If the cost difference is significant, I will consider and genuinely become interested in studying Ukrainian. I will understand why I need it. Not because there is no other way, but because I will see a real financial motive.

Cinema. Ukrainian cinema is still scarce and of low quality. The first obvious solution is to treat it the same way as books, by establishing serious state awards for good Ukrainian films. The second is, of course, the organization of film festivals. Russia has plenty of them here, while in Ukraine, there are significantly fewer here, and the quality is significantly lower. The third is government funds for film producers. For the country, these are insignificant expenses, but for the Ukrainian film industry, it is enormous support.

I think the idea is clear. Not to prohibit, but to encourage. Not to block Russian, but to develop Ukrainian. Not to run away from the enemy, but to become stronger ourselves. Then there won’t be such rejection as the Ukrainianization is causing now.

Я очень надеюсь, что по описанному выше пути пойдет Украина.
