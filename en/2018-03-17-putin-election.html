“Что сделал Путин, чтобы завтра победить”

Tomorrow in Russia there will be presidential elections, in which Vladimir Putin will win. This will be his fourth presidential term. In fact, he has been in power for 18 years, since the moment of its transfer in January 2000. Almost 150 million people in the geographically largest country in the world will once again give this person a mandate to govern their destiny. It is hard not to agree that this is a respectable result. How does he do it?

The main factor of Putin’s political success is his sober and adequate attitude towards the human material he has to deal with. When Feuchtwanger, in a conversation with Stalin, made a remark about the tasteless and exaggerated reverence for his personality, Stalin, according to his words, “apologized to his peasants and workers for being too busy with other matters and unable to develop good taste in themselves.” Apparently, similar sober cynicism is inherent in Putin, which is manifested in his style of governing his people.

Here are the main methods that bring such results:

Authoritarianism. Russia is Putin, Putin is Russia. It is difficult to say exactly how decisions are made behind the scenes of power, who among the advisors has what weight, and how power is actually distributed among them. For an ordinary citizen, the feeling is created that the country is being governed by one person. It doesn’t matter what people’s opinion of this person is, whether it is love or hatred. What matters is that the opinion is focused. This allows for keeping the situation under control.

Cult of Personality. It is difficult to create a cult of personality for a ruler in just six years, but it can and should be done in almost twenty years. Having a cult makes governance easier because it allows relying on authority. An ordinary citizen is generally not inclined to think deeply and critically. Complex logical constructions are difficult and tiring for him. From the government, he needs clear answers to pressing questions. The logical conclusion - “because it’s Putin” - easily and confidently puts an end to most of them.

Openness. Annual press conferences with journalists, open lessons with schoolchildren, visits to factories, interviews with foreign journalists - all this creates the image of a person who is open and accessible. It is not so important what he says - and he speaks well - what is important is to see him openly communicating with people. This creates a sense of “familiarity” and “kinship”. When we meet a famous actor on the street, we smile and nod, not receiving any reaction in return, which surprises and confuses us - we are used to considering this person our close acquaintance. The same effect occurs with the publicity of a politician - Putin has long become familiar to millions of Russians.

Aggressiveness. Even before becoming president, in 1999 Putin promised to “wipe out terrorists in the outhouse”, which, despite the rudeness of the wording, is pleasant to hear for the average voter. In this formulation, there is a clear formula: we are against them. It doesn’t matter exactly who these “they” are, whom we will “wipe out”. What matters is that there is “we” who will not let themselves be offended. Throughout Putin’s years in power, Russia has had enough enemies, and if there were not enough, they were created, blowing up buildings. To unite millions of people around the idea of peace is impossible. Hatred and war are needed for unity. Whether it’s Chechnya, Georgia, Transnistria, Syria, Donbass, or all of NATO. The population wants to know where the enemy is. The leader’s task is to find or create this enemy. Putin successfully copes with this.

Religiosity. It is hard to believe that a former member of the Communist Party and a lieutenant colonel of the KGB became religious. However, understanding the darkness of his people, he is doing absolutely right by associating himself with the Russian Orthodox Church and its top officials. The idea of a king as God’s anointed one is close to many Russians, who make up the majority of the active voting electorate. Religion, as it was, according to Marx, is the opium of the people, and it will always remain so. It is difficult and tiring to believe in oneself, in logic, in common sense, and in cause-and-effect relationships. Simple formulas are needed that allow shifting responsibility for one’s mistakes onto someone else. Christ is a better fit for this than anyone else, even better than Putin.

Hypocrisy. The theatrical opposition, led by the unwavering Zhirinovsky, clearly demonstrates that Putin knows how to play a double game and negotiate with opponents. He doesn’t “kill everyone in the toilets” like many dictators do. He pays someone, like Kadyrov, shares the stage and glory with others, like Sobchak and Navalny, and simply kills someone else - in each case, an individual decision is made. Questions of honor, conscience, and human dignity are completely ignored, as a competent leader of the masses should. People don’t care about the character of their leader; they care about their personal comfort. A leader who cares about the comfort of the people, rather than the purity of his conscience, will be respected and reelected.

Self-assurance. Not recognizing one’s own mistakes is a repelling quality in an ordinary person. However, in a prominent leader, it becomes a virtue. The people are unable to understand the reasons behind the mistakes and the lessons learned. A mistake is just a mistake. According to Machiavelli, a leader who makes mistakes and admits them is a weak leader. Putin never criticizes himself or admits mistakes, while continuing to make promises and not fulfill them, and even lie. From a purely human point of view, this outrages us, but on a scale of millions, it is a quality of an effective leader.

Patriotism. Wisdom, determination, energy, and decisiveness are important qualities of a leader. However, patriotism is more important. The people, while sometimes willing to forgive mistakes, will never forgive disloyalty. Constantly strained and often openly hostile relations with other countries and their leaders are a clear message from Putin to his citizens: “I am with you, not with them.” International sanctions and Russia’s isolation on the world stage have little practical impact on the average person, but through the media, they vividly illustrate the president’s patriotism.

The conclusion is clear - Putin is doing everything right, considering the circumstances he is in and the people who choose him. With other people, in another country, under different conditions, perhaps something could be done differently.
