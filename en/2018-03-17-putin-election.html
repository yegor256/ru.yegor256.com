“Что сделал Путин, чтобы завтра победить”

Tomorrow in Russia, there will be presidential elections in which Vladimir Putin will win. This will be his fourth presidential term. In fact, he has been in power for 18 years, starting from the moment of its transfer in January 2000. Almost 150 million people in the territorially largest country in the world will once again entrust this person with the mandate to manage their destiny. It is difficult not to agree that this is a respectable result. How does he do it?

The main factor of Putin’s success is his sober and adequate attitude towards the human material he has to deal with. When Feuchtwanger commented on Stalin’s tasteless and exaggerated adoration of his personality during a conversation with him, Stalin, according to Feuchtwanger, “apologized for his peasants and workers, saying that they were too occupied with other matters and couldn’t develop good taste in themselves.” Apparently, Putin also possesses such sober cynicism, which is evident in his management style towards his people.

Here are the main methods that bring such results:

Autocracy. Russia is Putin, Putin is Russia. It is difficult to say how decisions are made behind the scenes of power, which advisors carry more weight, and how authority is truly distributed among them. For an ordinary citizen, it creates a feeling that the country is governed by one person. It is not important what people’s opinion of this person is, whether it is love or hatred. What matters is that the opinion is focused. This allows for keeping the situation under control.

Cult of Personality. It is difficult to create a cult of personality for a ruler in six years of power, but it can and should be done in almost twenty years. The presence of a cult makes governance easier because it allows one to rely on authority. The average citizen is generally not inclined to think deeply and critically. Complex logical constructions are difficult and tiring for them. They need clear answers to pressing questions from those in power. The logical conclusion, “because it’s Putin,” easily and confidently puts an end to the majority of them.

Openness. Annual press conferences with journalists, open lessons with schoolchildren, visits to factories, interviews with foreign journalists - all this creates an image of a person who is open and accessible. What exactly he says - and he speaks quite well - is not so important. It is important to see him openly communicating with people. This creates a sense of “familiarity” and “kinship”. When we meet a famous actor on the street, we smile and nod, not receiving any reaction in return, which surprises and bewilders us - we are used to considering this person as our close acquaintance. The same effect arises from the public figure of a politician - Putin has long become familiar to millions of Russians.

Aggressiveness. Even before becoming president, in 1999 Putin promised to “wipe out terrorists in the toilets”, which, despite the rudeness of the wording, is pleasant to hear for the average voter. In this formulation, there is a clear formula: we are against them. Who exactly are these “they” that we will “wipe out” doesn’t play a big role. What matters is that there is “us” who will not allow themselves to be offended. Throughout Putin’s years in power, Russia had enough enemies, and if there weren’t enough, they were created by blowing up buildings (read). It is impossible to unite millions of people around the idea of peace. Hatred and war are needed for unity. Whether it’s Chechnya, Georgia, Transnistria, Syria, Donbass, or all of NATO. The population wants to know where the enemy is. The leader’s task is to find or create this enemy. Putin successfully copes with this.

Religiosity. It is hard to believe that a former member of the CPSU and a KGB lieutenant colonel came to believe. However, understanding the darkness of his people, he is acting absolutely correctly by associating himself with the Russian Orthodox Church and its leaders. The idea of the king, as the anointed one of God, is close to many Russians who make up the majority of the actively voting electorate. Religion, as Marx believed, is the opium of the people and will always remain so. It is difficult and exhausting to believe in oneself, in logic, in common sense, and in cause-and-effect relationships. Simpler formulas are needed that allow one to shift responsibility for their mistakes onto someone else. For this purpose, Christ is a perfect fit, just like Putin.

Power. Even Machiavelli believed that the only way to control the masses is through fear. Neither love, nor respect, nor common sense, nor even money can stop the masses when they are out of control. By granting almost unlimited powers to the security structures, in particular FSB, Putin simplifies and crystallizes the image of power for his electorate - it is clear to everyone whom to fear, who holds the power, where it comes from, and to whom to pay homage. The negative impact on the country is less than the positive, considering the type of people Putin deals with.

Mercilessness. Regular arrests and cruel punishments of people from their closest circle create an image of a fair ruler. It doesn’t matter what exactly the next Ulyukaev is guilty of, what matters is how strict and inevitable the punishment is. The people are afraid of the oprichniki, and the oprichniki are afraid of the tsar - this formula has been working for hundreds of years. The people believe that the tsar knows nothing about lawlessness and corruption. The people want to see the tsar punishing the arrogant murderers. The people write letters to the tsar and count on his justice. This format of communication with the authorities is convenient and understandable to the people.

Hypocrisy. The theatrical opposition led by the unwavering Zhirinovsky clearly demonstrates that Putin knows how to play a double game and make deals with his opponents. He doesn’t “kill everyone in the toilets” like many dictators do. He pays someone, like Kadyrov, he shares the stage and glory with someone, like Sobchak and Navalny, and he simply kills someone — in each case, an individual decision is made. Questions of honor, conscience, and human dignity are completely ignored, as a competent leader of the masses should. People don’t care what their leader is like, they care about their personal comfort. A leader who cares about the people’s comfort, rather than the purity of his conscience, will be respected and re-elected.

Self-confidence. Not recognizing one’s own mistakes is a repulsive quality in an ordinary person. However, in a prominent leader, it turns into a virtue. The people are unable to understand the reasons for the mistakes and the conclusions drawn. A mistake is just a mistake. According to Machiavelli, a leader who makes mistakes and even admits to them is a weak leader. Putin never criticizes himself or admits to mistakes, while continuing to make promises and not fulfill them, and even lie. From a purely human point of view, this angers us, but on a scale of millions, it is a quality of an effective leader.

Patriotism. Wisdom, determination, energy, and decisiveness are important qualities of a leader. However, patriotism is more important. The people, even if they are sometimes willing to forgive mistakes, will never forgive disloyalty. Constantly strained, and often openly hostile, relations with other countries and their leaders are a clear message from Putin to his citizens: “I am with you, not with them.” International sanctions and Russia’s isolation on the world stage have little practical impact on the average citizen, but through the media, they vividly illustrate the president’s patriotism.

The conclusion is that Putin is doing everything right, taking into account the circumstances in which he finds himself and the people who choose him. With other people, in another country, under different conditions, perhaps something could be done differently.
