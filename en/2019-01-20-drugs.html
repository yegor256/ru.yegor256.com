“Кого бы нам еще наказать за наркотики?”

I finished watching the series El Chapo (2017-2018) yesterday, which tells the story of the ongoing life of one of the most successful drug lords of modern times named Joaquin Guzman. The first season turned out to be the strongest, the second weaker, and I was only able to digest the third season thanks to the storyline involving Conrado. But right now, I’m not talking about cinema, but about drugs.

Throughout the entire film illustrating the last 20 years of modern history, the corrupt government of Mexico fights against the number one enemy of the state. In the confrontation with Joaquin, the less corrupt but no less treacherous and two-faced DEA (American Drug Enforcement Administration) also actively participates. In the end, our hero, with whom we manage to become friends during the film, is caught, extradited to the USA, and handed over to the hands of a fair American court. Justice prevails, and… American teenagers stop using methamphetamine!

The creators of the film do not tell us anything about this, even in the final credits, where it is solemnly announced that Joaquin has killed between two and three thousand people throughout his professional career. They do not mention it, presumably because the arrest of El Chapo is of little concern to teenagers, and finding where to buy methamphetamine does not hinder them at all. Statistics show that, despite the active fight against drug trafficking, drug consumption is increasing, and mortality from drug overdose is twice as high in the United States compared to any other developed country, such as Europe.

I do not have exact statistics on the situation in Ukraine, but based on the graffiti on the walls of buildings in any major city, one can infer the situation in the market: drugs are in high demand. Regular arrests of drug dealers do not hinder buyers from finding the goods they need… from new drug dealers.

Want to know my opinion? I consider punishing producers to be hypocrisy. After all, many of those who caught El Chapo, extradited him to New York, and will soon be judging and lynching him, are his own clients. They, as representatives of the entire country, openly express only half of the public interests. However, in reality, society wants two things simultaneously: 1) narcotic euphoria and 2) to punish those who sell it.

The second interest is expressed openly and verbally, while the first interest is secret and involves money: the volume of consumption of illegal drugs in the United States alone exceeds $100 billion per year. We vote with our dollars and hryvnias for the production of drugs, while openly persecuting those who produce them. The number of people who use drugs at least once a year is approximately 275 million, which is about 5.6% of the world’s population aged 15 to 64.

Парадокс.

But if we really want El Chapo to grow cotton instead of cocaine and synthesize gouache instead of fentanyl, let’s all together… stop using drugs. There’s no need to shoot, kill, arrest, prosecute, and spend three billion dollars a year on it (DEA budget). It’s enough to simply stop buying what this brave Mexican produces and sells. I’m sure he will quickly redirect his energy to a socially beneficial business.

On the contrary, by actively fighting against sellers like him, we expose our own weakness and stupidity: nothing can stop us from enjoying something sweet before bed. So should we imprison all the confectioners? What do confectioners have to do with it? Are they to blame for us being overweight, lazy, and weak-willed? They only parasitize on our shortcomings, but they are not the root cause. Drug dealers are the confectioners, and we, along with you, are the lazy and overweight ones.

What should we do if the problem is not with the manufacturers, but with us?

How to reduce the demand for drugs? How to convince the population that addiction is evil? Maybe it is necessary to intimidate? Or maybe remove restrictions, and the interest will decrease on its own?

On the first path of intimidation, countries such as Iran, Saudi Arabia, and China, where the death penalty is provided, including for drug possession, are involved (for example). In many other countries, the punishments are less severe but still harsh, including in Ukraine and Russia. Statistics will undoubtedly show that police measures have a positive effect.

Countries in Western Europe are taking the second path. For example, in the Netherlands, or Portugal, the possession of small amounts of drugs is not considered a criminal offense, although it does entail certain measures of social impact. Once again, statistics show that legalization brings positive results.

I don’t believe in either the first or the second way.

It’s not about intimidation or permits, but about our needs. Drugs are needed where needs for joy, happiness, success, self-realization, and everything else that Maslow described in his famous pyramid are not sufficiently satisfied. In one of his latest novels, Vladimir Sorokin vividly depicts the world of the future, in which Telluria becomes the richest country thanks to the invention of a unique drug, the effect of which significantly surpasses all others. The whole world depends on Telluria because it knows how to make people happy and sells “nails made of tellurium” at a very high price.

The film Traffic (2000) effectively illustrates the problem: the fight against drug trafficking turns into a battle with one’s own family for one of its leaders. The true enemy turns out to be not the heroin seller, but its consumer - the main character’s own daughter, who realizes that even if he defeats the producer, he won’t be able to defeat the main buyer - his daughter will always find a way to buy a new dose of “happiness”.

The point is to improve the standard of living of the Ukrainian population and satisfy their needs in education, leisure, art, and everything that replaces drugs. It is necessary to give people something that will bring them as much joy as cocaine. Obviously, it is difficult to do, but it is necessary. This is where state funds and our joint efforts should be invested.

Fighting against drug trafficking and drug addicts is like fighting against confectioners. In the end, we will still be overweight and controlled by Telluria.

Or maybe it’s already being controlled?
