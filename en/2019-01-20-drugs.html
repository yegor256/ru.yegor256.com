“Кого бы нам еще наказать за наркотики?”

Yesterday I finished watching the TV series El Chapo (2017-2018), which tells the story of the still unfinished life of one of the most successful drug lords of modern times named Joaquín Guzmán. The first season turned out to be the strongest, the second one weaker, and I was only able to digest the third season thanks to the storyline of Conrado. But we’re not talking about cinematography here, we’re talking about drugs.

Throughout the entire film illustrating the past 20 years of modern history, the corrupt government of Mexico fights against the number one enemy of the state. In the confrontation with Joaquin, the less corrupt but no less treacherous and two-faced Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) also takes an active part. In the end, our hero, with whom we manage to become friends during the film, is caught, extradited to the USA, and handed over to the hands of a fair American court. Justice prevails, and… American teenagers stop using methamphetamine!

The creators of the film do not tell us anything about this, not even in the final credits, where it is solemnly announced that Joaquin has killed between two and three thousand people throughout his professional career. They do not mention it, presumably because teenagers are not particularly concerned about El Chapo’s arrest, and finding where to buy methamphetamine does not interfere at all. Statistics indicate that, despite the active fight against drug trafficking, drug consumption is increasing, and the mortality rate from drug overdose is twice as high in the United States compared to any other developed country, such as Europe.

I don’t have exact statistics about the situation in Ukraine, but based on the graffiti on the walls of buildings in any major city, one can judge the situation in the market: drugs are in high demand. Regular arrests of drug dealers do not hinder buyers from finding the goods they need… from new drug dealers.

Do you want to know my opinion? I consider punishing the producers as hypocrisy. After all, many of those who caught El Chapo, extradited him to New York, and will soon judge and lynch him, are his own clients. They, as representatives of the entire country, openly express only half of the public interests. However, in reality, society wants two things at the same time: 1) narcotic euphoria and 2) to punish those who sell it.

The second interest is expressed openly and verbally, while the first one is secret and involves money: the volume of consumption of illegal drugs in the United States alone exceeds $100 billion per year. We vote with our dollars and hryvnias for the production of drugs, while openly persecuting those who produce them. The number of people who use drugs at least once a year is approximately 275 million people, which is about 5.6% of the world’s population aged 15 to 64 years.

Парадокс.

If we really want El Chapo to grow cotton instead of cocaine and synthesize gouache instead of fentanyl, let’s all together… stop using drugs. There is no need to shoot, kill, arrest, prosecute, and spend three billion dollars a year on it (DEA budget). It is enough to simply stop buying what this brave Mexican produces and sells. I’m sure he will quickly redirect his energy to a socially beneficial business.

On the contrary, by actively fighting against sellers like him, we acknowledge our own weakness and stupidity: nothing can stop us from indulging ourselves at night. So are we going to imprison all the confectioners? What do confectioners have to do with it? Are they to blame for the fact that we are fat, lazy, and weak-willed? They only parasitize on our shortcomings, but they are not the root cause. Drug dealers are the confectioners, and we are the lazy and overweight ones.

What to do if the problem is not with the manufacturers, but with us?

How to reduce the demand for drugs? How to convince the population that addiction is evil? Maybe it is necessary to intimidate? Or maybe lift the bans, and the interest will decrease on its own?

In the first way of intimidation, countries like Iran, Saudi Arabia, and China, where the death penalty is provided, including for drug possession, are taking steps. In many other countries, the punishments are less severe but still harsh, including in Ukraine and Russia. Statistics will undoubtedly show that police measures have a positive effect.

The second path is followed by the countries of Western Europe. For example, in the Netherlands or Portugal, the possession of soft drugs is not considered a criminal offense, although it entails certain measures of social impact. And again, statistics show that legalization brings positive results.

I don’t believe in the first way or the second way.

It’s not about intimidation or permits, but about our needs. Drugs are needed where needs for joy, happiness, success, self-realization, and everything else that Maslow described in his famous pyramid are not sufficiently satisfied. In one of his recent novels, Vladimir Sorokin vividly portrays the world of the future, in which Telluria becomes the richest country thanks to the invention of a unique drug whose effect surpasses all others. The whole world depends on Telluria because it knows how to make people happy and sells “nails made of tellurium” at a very high price.

The film Traffic (2000) effectively illustrates the problem: the fight against drug trafficking turns into a battle with one’s own family for one of its leaders. The true enemy turns out to be not the heroin dealer, but his consumer - his own daughter, who realizes that even if he defeats the producer, he won’t be able to defeat the main buyer - his daughter will always find a way to buy a new dose of “happiness”.

Olga Bogomolets believes that “today we need not to legalize drugs, but to expand the list of prohibited substances and ensure effective control over their circulation and [to] conduct a systemic fight against drug trafficking organizers”. No, Olga, it’s not about legalization or fighting against drug trafficking — that won’t help the cause.

The matter lies in improving the standard of living of the Ukrainian population and satisfying their needs in education, leisure, art, and everything that replaces drugs. It is necessary to provide people with something that will delight them as much as cocaine does. Obviously, this is difficult to achieve, but it is necessary. It is precisely in this that state funds and our joint efforts should be invested.

But to fight against drug trafficking and drug addicts - it’s like fighting against confectioners. In the end, we will still be fat, and Telluria will control us.

Or maybe it’s already being controlled?
