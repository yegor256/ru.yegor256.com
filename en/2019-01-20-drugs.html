“Кого бы нам еще наказать за наркотики?”

I finished watching the series El Chapo (2017-2018) yesterday, which tells the story of the unfinished life of one of the most successful drug lords of our time named Joaquín Guzmán. The first season turned out to be the strongest, the second weaker, and I was only able to digest the third season thanks to the storyline of Conrado. But we’re not talking about cinema right now, we’re talking about drugs.

Throughout the entire movie, which illustrates the last 20 years of modern history, the corrupt Mexican government fights the number one enemy of the state. In the battle against Joaquín, the less corrupt but no less treacherous and two-faced DEA (U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration) also actively participates. In the end, our hero, with whom we become friends during the movie, gets caught, extradited to the U.S., and handed over to the hands of a fair American court. Justice triumphs, and… American teenagers stop using methamphetamine!

The filmmakers don’t tell us anything about this even in the final credits, where it is solemnly announced that Joaquín has killed between two and three thousand people throughout his professional career. They don’t mention it, probably because the arrest of El Chapo doesn’t concern teenagers much, and finding where to buy methamphetamine doesn’t hinder them at all. The statistics show that despite the active fight against drug trafficking, drug consumption is increasing, and the mortality rate from drug overdoses is twice as high in the U.S. compared to any other developed country, such as Europe.

I don’t have precise statistics on the situation in Ukraine, but judging by the graffiti on the walls of buildings in any major city, we can infer that drugs are in demand in the market. Regular arrests of drug dealers do not prevent buyers from finding the goods they need… from new drug dealers.

Do you want to know my opinion? I consider punishing the producers hypocritical. After all, many of those who caught El Chapo, extradited him to New York, and will soon judge and lynch him are his clients themselves. They, as representatives of the whole country, openly express only half of the public interests. However, in reality, society wants two things simultaneously: 1) narcotic euphoria and 2) to punish those who sell it.

The second interest is openly and verbally expressed, while the first is done secretly and with money: the volume of consumption of illegal drugs only in the U.S. exceeds 100 billion dollars per year. We vote with our dollars and hryvnias for drug production, while openly persecuting those who produce them. The number of people who use drugs at least once a year amounts to approximately 275 million people, which is about 5.6% of the world population aged 15 to 64.

If we really want El Chapo to grow cotton instead of cocaine and synthesize gouache instead of fentanyl, let’s all together… stop using drugs. There is no need to shoot, kill, arrest, judge, and spend three billion dollars a year on it (DEA budget). It is enough to simply stop buying what this brave Mexican produces and sells. I am sure he will quickly redirect his energy to a socially useful business.

On the contrary, by actively fighting against such sellers, we demonstrate our own weakness and stupidity: nothing can stop us from indulging in our nightly pleasures. So, should we imprison all confectioners? What do confectioners have to do with it? Are they to blame for us being fat, lazy, and weak-willed? They only parasitize on our weaknesses, but they are not the root cause. Drug dealers are the confectioners, and the lazy and overweight ones are us.

But what should we do if the problem is not with the producers, but with us?

How can we reduce the demand for drugs? How can we convince the population that addictions are evil? Maybe we need to intimidate? Or maybe lift the bans, and the interest will naturally decrease?

The first path of intimidation is taken by countries like Iran, Saudi Arabia, and China, where the death penalty is imposed, including for drug possession. In many other countries, punishments are less severe but still harsh, including in Ukraine and Russia. Statistics undoubtedly show that police measures have a positive effect.

The second path is taken by Western European countries. For example, in the Netherlands or Portugal, possession of soft drugs is not a criminal offense, although it entails certain measures of social influence. And again, statistics show that legalization brings positive results.

I do not believe in either the first or the second path.

The issue is not about intimidation or permissions, but about our needs. Drugs are needed where the needs for joy, happiness, success, self-realization, and everything else that Maslow described in his famous pyramid are not sufficiently satisfied. In one of his recent novels, Vladimir Sorokin vividly portrays a future world in which Telluria becomes the richest country thanks to the invention of a unique drug whose effect far surpasses all others. The whole world depends on Telluria because it knows how to make people happy and sells “nails made of tellurium” at a high price.

The film “Traffic (2000)” illustrates the problem well: the fight against drug trafficking turns into a fight against one’s own family for one of its leaders. The true enemy turns out to be not the heroin dealer, but his consumer - the daughter of one of the main characters who realizes that defeating the producer will not allow him to defeat the main buyer - his daughter will always find where to buy a new dose of “happiness”.

Olga Bogomolets believes that “today we need not to legalize drugs, but to expand the list of prohibited substances and ensure effective control over their circulation and to conduct a systematic fight against drug business”. No, Olga, it is not about legalization or fighting against the drug business - that won’t solve the problem.

The solution lies in improving the standard of living of the Ukrainian population and satisfying their needs for education, leisure, art, and everything else that replaces drugs. We need to give people something that will delight them as much as cocaine. Obviously, that is difficult to do, but it is necessary. This is where state money and our joint efforts should be invested.

Fighting against drug trafficking and drug addicts is like fighting against confectioners. In the end, we will still be fat, and Telluria will be in control of us.

Or maybe it already is?
