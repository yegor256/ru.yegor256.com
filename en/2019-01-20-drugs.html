“Кого бы нам еще наказать за наркотики?”

Yesterday I finished watching the TV series El Chapo (2017-2018), which tells the story of the ongoing life of one of the most successful drug lords of our time, named Joaquín Guzmán. The first season turned out to be the strongest, the second one was weaker, and I managed to endure the third only because of the storyline with Conrado. But now we are not talking about cinema, but about drugs.

Throughout the entire film illustrating the last 20 years of modern history, the corrupt government of Mexico is fighting the number one enemy of the state. In the confrontation with Joaquin, the less corrupt but no less treacherous and two-faced Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) of the United States also actively participates. In the end, our hero, whom we manage to become friends with during the film, is caught, extradited to the USA, and handed over to the hands of a fair American court. Justice triumphs, and… American teenagers stop using methamphetamine!

The creators of the film do not tell us anything about this, not even in the final credits, where it is solemnly announced that Joaquin has killed between two and three thousand people throughout his professional career. They probably don’t mention it because teenagers are not particularly concerned about the arrest of El Chapo, and finding where to buy methamphetamine does not interfere at all. Statistics show that, despite the active fight against drug trafficking, drug consumption is increasing, and the mortality rate from drug overdose is twice as high in the USA compared to any other developed country, such as Europe.

I don’t have exact statistics about the situation in Ukraine, but based on the graffiti on the walls of houses in any major city, one can infer about the market situation: drugs are in high demand. Regular arrests of drug dealers do not prevent buyers from finding the goods they need… from new drug dealers.

Do you want to know my opinion? I consider punishing the producers as hypocrisy. After all, many of those who caught El Chapo, extradited him to New York, and will soon judge and lynch him, are his clients themselves. As representatives of the entire country, they openly express only half of the public interests. However, in reality, society wants two things at the same time: 1) narcotic euphoria and 2) to punish those who sell it.

The second interest is expressed openly and verbally, while the first is secret and involves money: the volume of consumption of illegal drugs in the United States alone exceeds 100 billion dollars per year. We vote with our dollars and hryvnias for the production of drugs, while openly persecuting those who produce them. The number of people who use drugs at least once a year is approximately 275 million people, which is about 5.6% of the world population aged 15 to 64.

Парадокс.

But if we really want El Chapo to grow cotton instead of cocaine and synthesize gouache instead of fentanyl, let’s all together… stop using drugs. There is no need to shoot, kill, arrest, prosecute, and spend three billion dollars a year on it (DEA budget). It is enough to simply stop buying what this brave Mexican produces and sells. I’m sure he will quickly redirect his energy towards a socially beneficial business.

On the contrary, by actively fighting against sellers like him, we admit our own weakness and foolishness: nothing can stop us from indulging in sweetness at night. So should we imprison all confectioners? What do confectioners have to do with it? Are they to blame for us being fat, lazy, and weak-willed? They only parasitize on our shortcomings, but they are not the root cause. Drug dealers are the confectioners, and we, with you, are the lazy and fat ones.

Что же делать, если проблема не в производителях, а в нас?

How to reduce demand for drugs? How to convince the population that addiction is evil? Maybe it is necessary to intimidate? Or maybe to lift the bans, and the interest will decrease on its own?

In the first path of intimidation, countries like Iran, Saudi Arabia, and China, where the death penalty is imposed, including for drug possession, are taking part. In many other countries, the punishments are less severe but still harsh, including in Ukraine and Russia. Statistics will undoubtedly show that police measures have a positive effect.

The second path is taken by countries in Western Europe. For example, in the Netherlands or Portugal, the possession of soft drugs is not considered a criminal offense, although it does entail certain measures of social impact. And again, statistics show that legalization brings positive results.

Я не верю ни в первый, ни во второй путь.

It’s not about intimidation or permissions, but about our needs. Drugs are needed where needs for joy, happiness, success, self-realization, and everything else that Maslow described in his famous pyramid are not sufficiently satisfied. In one of his recent novels, Vladimir Sorokin vividly depicts a world of the future where Telluria becomes the richest country thanks to the invention of a unique drug whose effect significantly surpasses all others. The whole world depends on Telluria because it knows how to make people happy and sells “nails made of tellurium” at a very high price.

The film Traffic (2000) effectively illustrates the problem: the fight against drug trafficking turns into a battle with one’s own family for one of its leaders. The true enemy turns out to be not the heroin dealer, but his consumer - his own daughter, who realizes that even if he defeats the producer, he will not be able to defeat the main buyer - his daughter will always find a place to buy a new dose of “happiness”.

Olga Bogomolets believes that “today we need not to legalize drugs, but to expand the list of prohibited substances and ensure effective control over their circulation and [carry out] a systematic fight against drug trafficking organizers”. No, Olga, it’s not about legalization or fighting against drug trafficking – that won’t help the cause.

The point is to improve the standard of living of the Ukrainian population and satisfy their needs in education, leisure, art, and everything that will replace drugs. It is necessary to give people something that will delight them as much as cocaine. It is obvious that it is difficult to do, but it is necessary. This is where state funds and our joint efforts should be invested.

Fighting against drug trafficking and drug addicts is like fighting against confectioners. In the end, we will still be overweight and controlled by Telluria.
