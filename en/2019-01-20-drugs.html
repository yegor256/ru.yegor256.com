“Кого бы нам еще наказать за наркотики?”

I finished watching the TV series El Chapo (2017-2018) yesterday, which tells the story of the unfinished life of one of the most successful drug lords of our time named Joaquin Guzman. The first season turned out to be the strongest, the second weaker, and the third I managed to digest only thanks to the storyline of Conrado. But right now, we’re not talking about cinematography, but about drugs.

Throughout the entire film illustrating the last 20 years of modern history, the corrupt government of Mexico fights against the number one enemy of the state. In the confrontation with Joaquin, the less corrupt but no less treacherous and two-faced Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) also actively participates. In the end, our hero, whom we manage to become friends with during the film, is caught, extradited to the United States, and handed over to the hands of a fair American court. Justice triumphs, and… American teenagers stop using methamphetamine!

The creators of the film do not tell us anything about it, not even in the final credits, where it is solemnly announced that Joaquin has killed between two and three thousand people throughout his professional career. They do not say, probably because the arrest of El Chapo does not concern teenagers much, and finding where to buy methamphetamine does not interfere at all. Statistics show that despite the active fight against drug trafficking, drug consumption is increasing, and the mortality rate from drug overdoses is twice as high in the USA compared to any other developed country, such as Europe.

I don’t have accurate statistics on the situation in Ukraine, but based on the graffiti on the walls of buildings in any major city, one can judge the situation in the market: drugs are in high demand. Regular arrests of drug dealers do not interfere at all with buyers finding the goods they need… from new drug dealers.

Do you want to know my opinion? I consider punishing manufacturers as hypocrisy. After all, many of those who caught El Chapo, extradited him to New York, and will soon judge and lynch him, are his clients themselves. As representatives of the whole country, they openly express only half of the public interests. However, in reality, society wants two things at the same time: 1) narcotic euphoria and 2) to punish those who sell it.

Secondly, it expresses its interest openly and verbally, while the first interest is secretive and involves money: the volume of consumption of illegal drugs in the United States exceeds $100 billion annually. We vote with our dollars and hryvnias for the production of drugs, while openly persecuting those who produce them. The number of people who use drugs at least once a year is approximately 275 million, which is roughly 5.6% of the world’s population aged 15 to 64.

Парадокс.

But if we really want El Chapo to grow cotton instead of cocaine and synthesize gouache instead of fentanyl, let’s all together… stop using drugs. There’s no need to shoot, kill, arrest, prosecute, and spend three billion dollars a year on it (DEA budget). It’s enough to simply stop buying what this brave Mexican produces and sells. I’m sure he will quickly redirect his energy to a socially beneficial business.

On the contrary, by actively fighting against sellers like him, we admit our own weakness and stupidity: nothing can stop us from indulging in sweets at night. So should we imprison all confectioners? What do confectioners have to do with it? Are they to blame for the fact that we are overweight, lazy, and lacking in willpower? They are just parasites on our shortcomings, but not their root cause. Drug dealers are the confectioners, and we, together, are the lazy and overweight ones.

Что же делать, если проблема не в производителях, а в нас?

How to reduce demand for drugs? How to convince the population that addiction is evil? Maybe it is necessary to intimidate? Or maybe to lift restrictions, and the interest will decrease on its own?

On the first path of intimidation, countries like Iran, Saudi Arabia, and China, where the death penalty is provided, including for drug possession, are leading the way. In many other countries, the punishments are less severe but still harsh enough, including in Ukraine and Russia. Statistics will undoubtedly show that police measures have a positive effect.

The second path is taken by countries in Western Europe. For example, in the Netherlands, or Portugal, the possession of small amounts of drugs is not a criminal offense, although it does entail certain measures of social influence. And again, statistics show that legalization brings positive results.

Я не верю ни в первый, ни во второй путь.

It’s not about intimidation or permissions, but about our needs. Drugs are necessary where needs for joy, happiness, success, self-realization, and everything else that Maslow described in his famous pyramid are not sufficiently satisfied. In one of his latest novels, Vladimir Sorokin vividly depicts the world of the future, where Telluria becomes the richest country thanks to the invention of a unique drug, the effect of which significantly surpasses all others. The whole world depends on Telluria because it knows how to make people happy and sells “nails made of tellurium” at a very high price.

The movie Traffic (2000) effectively illustrates the problem: the fight against drug trafficking turns into a battle with one’s own family for one of its leaders. The true enemy turns out to be not the heroin seller, but its consumer - the daughter of one of the main characters, who realizes that even if he defeats the producer, he won’t be able to defeat the main buyer - his daughter will always find a way to buy a new portion of “happiness”.

Olga Bogomolets believes that “today we need not to legalize drugs, but to expand the list of prohibited substances and ensure effective control over their circulation and [to] conduct a systematic fight against drug trafficking organizers”. No, Olga, it’s not about legalization or fighting against drug trafficking — it won’t help the cause.

The case is about improving the standard of living of the Ukrainian population and satisfying their needs in education, leisure, art, and everything that will “replace” drugs. It is necessary to give people something that will delight them as much as cocaine. Obviously, this is difficult to do, but it is necessary. This is exactly where state funds and our joint efforts should be invested.

Fighting against drug trafficking and drug addicts is like fighting against confectioners. In the end, we will still be fat, and Telluria will be in control of us.
