Nation, ethnicity, ethnic group, [ethnicity]. These and similar terms refer to groups of people who identify themselves based on certain common characteristics, such as language, customs, ideology, or religion. In any case, as Lev Gumilev noted, the constant unifying message for them is: “we are such-and-such, and all others are different”.

According to Vakhushti Parcvenia, education and the existence of different nations are an objective consequence of the process of socialization. In other words, for a person to survive, it is necessary to feel a sense of belonging to a group, which a person will create even if it does not exist.

Thus, finding signs of distinction from “others” in oneself and one’s environment, and then elevating these signs to the level of national identity — is a constructive process that contributes to the individual’s survival.

However, a natural consequence of the formation of a people and a nation is nationalism - the “measles of humanity,” as Albert Einstein put it. The meaning of this characterization is that it is a childhood disease that humanity, like a child, must overcome in the early stages of its development, when the key to the success of a tribe, city, or state lies in the timely physical destruction or enslavement of the surrounding tribes, cities, and states. In these conditions, nationalism, or even open chauvinism and xenophobia, unite the population and help it survive.

Nationalism is necessary and very useful in the presence of an external threat. For example, Ivan Ilyin identified nationalism with the “instinct of national self-preservation” and believed that this instinct is “a true and justified state.” This is a kind of immunity of a social group, protecting it from destruction or absorption by another larger and more stable group. In simpler terms, it is important for us to cultivate in ourselves and our children a hatred for strangers if we want our own children to survive.

There are different opinions, for example, Andrey Konchalovsky believes that “nationalism, in its constructive forms, is primarily about serving one’s own people, not destroying other peoples in the name of one’s own.” This is a rose-colored view. As shown above, the concept of dividing the population into nations and ethnicities was born exclusively for military purposes: to identify one’s own and develop a sense of hostility towards others. Everywhere that rhetoric of “us versus them” is used, it is about war. When the second part of this pair is removed and one only calls for “serving one’s own people,” as Konchalovsky did, it smells of nothing but hypocrisy.

In other sources, I found even more uncertain figures: ranging from several hundred to several thousand. It’s simple, mainly thanks to technological progress, the boundaries between nations are blurring and disappearing: we no longer know who is who.

The defining trends on the ethnic map of our planet at the moment are consolidation and assimilation. In simpler terms, it is becoming increasingly difficult to determine whether someone is Russian, Ukrainian, Jewish, Tatar, or African. Surprisingly, many scientists even consider the term “race” to have lost its scientific relevance when it comes to humans. In other words, it is becoming more difficult to objectively determine the nationality of a specific inhabitant of our planet, and there is less and less real necessity for it.

We are born in the USSR to a Ukrainian mother and a Belarusian father; we live in Berlin; we speak English in the office, Russian with my wife, German with the children, and Turkish in the supermarket; we believe in Jesus Christ, we don’t celebrate Easter, but we celebrate Soviet Army Day; we wear Chinese jeans; we love Italian cuisine and French comedies. What nationality are we? What nationality will our children be, who will speak Russian with a strong German accent only when asked? None?

It is absolutely obvious that such signs of division by nationality as place of birth, language, cultural traditions, religious beliefs, as well as the shape of the skull and eye shape, lose their relevance. Moreover, the use of these outdated signs, according to Parzvaniya, can only indicate the “narrow-mindedness and backwardness” of the people who use them.

However, the desire to gather in groups and identify oneself through belonging to them has not disappeared. As we have seen above, it is innate and vital. In other words, nationalism, chauvinism, and xenophobia are ineradicable because they are natural. The question is only how those who are not “backward and limited” will identify the “other”.

The nationalism of the future, if civilization continues to develop rather than degrade, will divide people into groups and communities based on such characteristics as professional interests (for example, artists, programmers, writers, designers), life beliefs (for example, vegetarians, childfree, downsizers), sports (for example, bodybuilders, snowboarders, cyclists), music, and others. These groups and communities are the “nations of the future”.

These new nations are already forming if you observe the development of communities, for example on Facebook. Alongside politically charged groups dedicated to hating “vatniks” or “ukrams,” groups of vegetarians or lovers of good cinema are equally, and often significantly, more popular.

The more developed a person is, the more educated and oriented towards self-development, the less significant for them are the outdated categories of national division. At the same time, their desire to identify themselves through belonging to a group and opposing themselves to “others” does not go away. It is realized through the formation of new nations and peoples that have nothing in common with Ukrainians, Russians, Chinese, or Jews. Now they are called “coffee lovers,” “theater actors,” “tourist bloggers,” and “followers of rebirthing.”

Sooner or later, the Ukrainian nation will cease to exist, just like the Georgian, Tatar, Spanish, or Russian nations. The Ukrainian language, literature, music, cuisine, rituals, and traditions will remain, but there will be no “Ukrainian people.” This concept will simply become outdated.
