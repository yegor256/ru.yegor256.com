“Национализм будущего”

People, nation, ethnicity, ethnos. These and similar terms denote groups of people who identify themselves based on various common characteristics, such as language, customs, ideology, or religion. In any case, as Lev Gumilev noted, the unchanging unifying message for them is: “we are such-and-such, and all others are different”.

According to Vakhtang Parcvania, education and the existence of different nations is an objective consequence of the process of socialization. In other words, for a person, as a “social being,” it is necessary for survival to feel a sense of belonging to a group, which a person will create even in its absence, even in an empty space.

Thus, finding signs of distinction from “others” within oneself and one’s environment, and then elevating these signs to the status of national identity — is a constructive process that contributes to the survival of the individual.

However, a natural consequence of the formation of a people and a nation is nationalism - the “measles of humanity,” in the words of Albert Einstein. The meaning of this characterization is that it is a childhood disease that humanity, like a child, must overcome in the early stages of its development, when the key to the success of a tribe, city, or state is the timely physical destruction or enslavement of the surrounding tribes, cities, and states. In these conditions, nationalism, or even open chauvinism and xenophobia, will unite the population and help it survive.

Nationalism is necessary and very useful in the presence of an external threat. For example, Ivan Ilyin identified nationalism with the “instinct of national self-preservation” and believed that this instinct is “a correct and justified state.” This is a kind of immunity of a social group, protecting it from destruction or absorption by another larger and more stable group. In simpler terms, it is important for us to cultivate in ourselves and our children a hatred towards strangers if we want our children to survive.

There are different opinions, for example, Andrey Konchalovsky believes that “nationalism in its constructive forms is, above all, serving one’s own people, and not destroying other nations in the name of one’s own.” This is a rose-colored view. As shown above, the concept of dividing the population into nations and nationalities was born solely for military purposes: to identify one’s own and develop a sense of animosity towards others. Wherever the rhetoric of “us versus them” is used, it is about war. When the second part of this pair is removed and one is called to only “serve one’s own people,” as Konchalovsky did, it smells of nothing but hypocrisy.

Now let’s pay attention to an interesting situation with the estimation of the total number of peoples and nations on the planet. According to various opinions, currently there are anywhere from 800 to 2000 of them in the world. Wikipedia lists only a few hundred. Other sources provide even more indefinite figures: from a few hundred to several thousand. Why isn’t there an accurate list of them, considering that we are talking about groups consisting of tens of thousands of people and more? Where does this inaccuracy come from?

It’s simple, mainly thanks to technological progress, the boundaries between nations blur and disappear: we no longer know who is who.

The defining trends on the ethnic map of our planet at the moment are consolidation and assimilation. In other words, it is becoming increasingly difficult to say who among us is Russian, Ukrainian, Jewish, Tatar, or Black. Surprisingly, many scientists even consider the term “race” in relation to humans to have lost its scientific relevance. In other words, it is becoming more and more difficult to objectively determine the nationality of a specific inhabitant of our planet, and there is less and less real need for it.

We are born in the USSR to a Ukrainian mother and a Belarusian father; we live in Berlin; we speak English at the office, Russian with our wife, German with our children, and Turkish at the supermarket; we believe in Jesus Christ, we don’t celebrate Easter, but we celebrate the Day of the Soviet Army; we wear Chinese jeans; we love Italian cuisine and French comedies. What nationality are we? What nationality will our children be, who will speak Russian with a strong German accent only when asked to do so? None?

It is quite obvious that such signs of division by nationality as place of birth, language, cultural traditions, religious beliefs, as well as cranial shape and eye shape, lose their relevance. Moreover, the use of these outdated signs, according to Parcavian, can only indicate the “narrow-mindedness and backwardness” of the people who use them.

However, the desire to gather in groups and identify oneself through belonging to them has not disappeared anywhere. As we have seen above, it is innate and vital. In other words, nationalism, chauvinism, and xenophobia are incurable because they are natural. The question is only how those who are not “backward and limited” will identify the “other”.

The nationalism of the future, if civilization continues to develop rather than degrade, will divide people into groups and communities based on such characteristics as professional interests (for example, artists, programmers, writers, fashion designers), life beliefs (for example, vegetarians, childfree, downshifters), sports (for example, bodybuilders, snowboarders, cyclists), music, and others. It is these groups and communities that will be the “nations of the future”.

These new nations are already forming if you observe the development of communities, for example on Facebook. Alongside politicized groups dedicated to hatred towards “vatniks” or “ukrams”, groups of vegetarians or aficionados of good cinema are no less, and often significantly more popular.

The more developed a person is, the more educated and oriented towards self-development, the less significant for him are the outdated categories of national division. At the same time, his desire to identify himself through belonging to a group and opposing himself to “strangers” does not disappear. It is realized through the formation of new nations and peoples that have nothing in common with Ukrainians, Russians, Chinese, or Jews. Now they are called “coffee lovers,” “theater actors,” “tourist bloggers,” and “followers of rebirthing.”

Sooner or later, the Ukrainian nation will cease to exist, just like the Georgian, Tatar, Spanish, or Russian. The Ukrainian language, literature, music, cuisine, rituals, and traditions will remain, but there will be no “Ukrainian people.” This concept will simply outlive itself.
