“Справедливость из Пензы”

I am writing this article based on the results of my trip to small Russian cities, namely Penza, Samara, and Novosibirsk. It left a depressing impression, especially after Moscow. Poverty and dirt. At the same time, there is absolutely no feeling that these cities are inhabited by stupid and lazy people deserving such a life. Rather, on the contrary, it’s like “they are not lazy and could live differently”. What is the matter? Why do people in the third Russian city barely make ends meet, working two shifts, while there is dirt and potholes on their streets? How are they different from Muscovites or, for example, residents of Zurich?

Ответ очевиден: воруют! Однако, не все так просто.

In both Penza and Zurich, very similar people live, most of them have eight working hours per day to create goods and services. Each Swiss produces approximately $65,000 of these goods and services per year, while the average Russian is slightly less, only $29,000. Twice as less. Have you been to Zurich? And to Penza? The Swiss autobahn and the road from Penza airport to its center are not “twice as much”, these are two big differences.

Apparently, the issue is not laziness and incompetence, but rather “theft”. But who? Under the cover of night, thieves persistently break into Penza’s storerooms, steal all the valuables created by hardworking people, and disappear in an unknown direction. Unlucky residents of Penza wake up in the morning, discover broken locks, sigh, return to lathe machines and sewing machines, and continue working. And so it repeats year after year.

No, it’s not the same thieves. The robbers of Penza do not need the night to take a large part of the GDP produced by its residents. They act during the day, openly, without much restraint or fear of anything or anyone. Because they are the residents of Penza. More precisely, its best representatives.

The cause of dirt and poverty is not theft, despite the high GDP level, but rather an imbalanced distribution of earned resources. $65,000 earned by a Swiss person in a year will mostly be spent on themselves, their family, and road repairs in front of their house. Some portion will go to officials, capitalists, and other idlers who do not create the product themselves but merely own the means of its creation - capital and land. However, this portion will be significantly smaller than what an average Russian gives to their owners.

At the very beginning of perestroika, a film called Jack Vosmerkin - “the American” was released. Its protagonist tried to build a business in young Soviet Russia. On his plot of land, he planted tobacco, his family helped it grow, and he decided to start an advertising campaign by giving away free cigars to his fellow villagers as a “trial”. The whole village happily smoked the free promotional product, but they refused to buy the product outright, mainly citing that “neighbors don’t do that to each other”. They were vehemently outraged by Jack’s desire to profit from his fellow unfortunates - fellow poor people.

Then the most interesting thing happened. Jack, having no means of existence and being rejected by the market, traded his tobacco for pennies to the local bully Skorokhodov, who ground it into snuff and began selling it to the same poor people who immediately lined up. No more protests were heard.

Why is that? What is the difference between Jack, who grew tobacco, and Skorokhodov, who only resold it? Why were people willing to receive only free gifts from Jack, while there was no doubt that Skorokhodov needed to be paid?

The difference between them is huge - they come from different social classes. Jack is just as poor as they are. Skorokhodov is their owner. In the minds of the heroes of this comedy, despite the revolution and profound social changes of that time, the formula of social “justice” has forever remained: we work, the owner becomes rich. And if any American tries to question this dogma, even to our advantage, the whole village will stand up in defense. Skorokhodov didn’t have to do anything. The entire village defended his interests as the owner of capital.

Something similar happens in modern Russia, Ukraine, and many other countries, where people are accustomed to wealthy and all-powerful owners who “rightfully” own the majority of all the products and services that these people create. In other words, the entire city of Penza works for a small number of people who are called thieves in the opposition press, but whose interests the whole city will rush to defend if their right to own the city is called into question.

It’s not about stealing. No one is stealing anything, although technically the actions of officials can almost always be classified as such. The matter lies in the natural and historically inherited desire of the entire people for social inequality and material injustice.

Can this formula be changed to bring our consciousness closer to the consciousness of an average Swiss person, for whom a wealthier fellow villager is not a misunderstanding or a threat - that is an interesting question. However, a much more interesting question is whether it is necessary.
