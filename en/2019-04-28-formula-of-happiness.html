“Справедливость из Пензы”

I am writing this article based on the results of my trip to small Russian cities, namely: Penza, Samara, and Novosibirsk. It left a depressing impression, especially after Moscow. Poverty and dirt. At the same time, there is absolutely no feeling that they are inhabited by stupid and lazy people deserving such a life. Rather the opposite, “they seem not to be idlers and could live differently”. So what’s the matter? Why do people in the third Russian city struggle to make ends meet, working two shifts, while their streets are full of dirt and potholes? How do they differ from Muscovites or, let’s say, residents of Zurich?

Ответ очевиден: воруют! Однако, не все так просто.

In both Penza and Zurich, very similar people live, most of them have eight working hours a day to create goods and services. Each Swiss produces approximately $65,000 worth of these goods and services per year, while the average Russian is slightly less - only $29,000. Twice as less. Have you been to Zurich? How about Penza? The Swiss autobahn and the road from Penza airport to its center are not “twice as different”, it is two big differences.

Apparently, the issue is not about idleness and negligence, but about “stealing”. But who? Under the cover of night, burglars persistently break into Penza’s storerooms, steal all the valuable items created by hardworking people, and disappear in an unknown direction. Unfortunate residents of Penza wake up in the morning, discover broken locks, sigh, return to their lathes and sewing machines, and continue working. And so it repeats from year to year.

No, they don’t steal that way, not those ones. The robbers of Penza don’t need the night to take a large part of the GDP produced by its residents. They act during the day, openly, not restraining or fearing much. Because they are the residents of Penza. More precisely, its best representatives.

The reason for the dirt and poverty is not theft, considering the high GDP, but rather the unbalanced distribution of earned resources. $65,000 earned by a Swiss person in a year will mostly be spent on themselves, their family, and road repairs in front of their house. Some portion will go to officials, capitalists, and other idlers who do not create the product themselves but only possess the means for its creation - capital and land. However, this portion will be significantly smaller than what an average Russian gives to their owners.

At the very beginning of Perestroika, a film called “Jack Vosmerkin - ‘the American’” was released, the protagonist of which tried to build a business in young Soviet Russia. On his plot of land, he planted tobacco, grew it with the help of his family, and decided to start an advertising campaign by giving away free cigars to his fellow villagers as a “trial”. The entire village gladly smoked the free promotional goods, but flatly refused to buy the product, mainly citing that “neighbors don’t behave like that”. They were categorically outraged by Jack’s desire to profit from his fellow unfortunate ones, who were just as poor.

Then the most interesting thing happened. Jack, having no means of existence and being rejected by the entire market, traded his tobacco for worthless items with the local bully Skorokhodov, who ground it into snuff and started selling it to the same poor people who immediately lined up. No more protests were heard.

Why is that? What is the difference between Jack, who grew tobacco, and Skorokhodov, who simply resold it? Why were people only willing to receive free gifts from Jack, while no one doubted that Skorokhodov needed to be paid?

The difference between them is huge - they come from different social classes. Jack is just as poor as they are. Skorokhodov is their owner. In the minds of the characters in this comedy, despite the revolution and profound social changes of that time, the formula of social “justice” has forever remained: we work, the owner gets rich. And if any American tries to question this dogma, even if it is for our benefit, we will all stand up to defend it. Skorokhodov didn’t need to do anything. The whole village defended his interests as the owner of capital.

Something similar is happening in modern Russia, Ukraine, and many other countries, where people are accustomed to wealthy and powerful owners who “rightfully” own the majority of all the products and services that these people create. In other words, the entire city of Penza works for a small number of people who are called thieves in the opposition press, but whose interests the whole city will rush to defend if their right to own the city is ever called into question.

It’s not about stealing. Nobody steals anything, although technically the actions of officials can often be classified as such. The matter lies in the natural and historically inherited desire of the entire nation for social inequality and material injustice.

Is it possible to modify this formula and bring our consciousness closer to the consciousness of an average Swiss person, for whom thanks to it, a wealthier fellow villager is not a misunderstanding or a threat - an interesting question. However, a much more interesting question is whether it is necessary?
