“Справедливость из Пензы”

I am writing this article based on the results of my trip to small Russian cities, namely: Penza, Samara, and Novosibirsk. It left a depressing impression, especially after Moscow. Poverty and dirt. At the same time, there is absolutely no feeling that they are populated by stupid and lazy people who deserve such a life. Rather the opposite, “they seem not to be idle and could live differently.” What is the matter? Why do people in the Russian third city, working two shifts, barely make ends meet, while their streets are dirty and full of potholes? How do they differ from Muscovites or, let’s say, residents of Zurich?

The answer is obvious: they steal! However, it’s not that simple.

In both Penza and Zurich, very similar people live, most of them have eight working hours a day to create goods and services. Each Swiss person produces approximately $65,000 worth of these goods and services per year, while the average Russian produces slightly less, only $29,000. Twice as little. Have you been to Zurich? And to Penza? The Swiss autobahn and the road from the Penza airport to its center are not “twice as much”, they are two big differences.

Apparently, the issue is not about idleness and sloppiness, but about “stealing”. But who? Under the cover of night, burglars persistently break into the Penza storage rooms, steal all the values created by hardworking people and disappear in an unknown direction. Unlucky residents of Penza wake up in the morning, discovering broken locks, sigh, return to their lathes and sewing machines, and continue working. And so it repeats year after year.

No, they don’t steal that way. The robbers of Penza don’t need the night to take a large part of the GDP produced by its residents. They act during the day, openly, without much restraint or fear of anyone. Because they are the residents of Penza. Or rather, its best representatives.

The reason for the dirt and poverty is not theft, given such a high GDP level, but rather the unbalanced distribution of earned resources. $65,000 earned by a Swiss person in a year will mainly be spent on themselves, their family, and road repairs in front of their house. Some portion will go to officials, capitalists, and other idlers who do not create the product themselves but only own the means for its creation - capital and land. However, this portion will be significantly smaller than what an average Russian gives to their owners.

At the very beginning of perestroika, a film called Jack Vosmorkin - “American” was released, featuring a protagonist who tried to build a business in young Soviet Russia. On his plot of land, he planted tobacco, grew it with the help of his family, and decided to start an advertising campaign by giving out free cigarettes to his fellow villagers “for a trial.” The whole village gladly smoked the free promotional product, but refused to buy the product outright, mainly citing that “neighbors don’t do that.” They were categorically outraged by Jack’s desire to make money off his fellow unfortunate people.

Then the most interesting thing happened. Jack, having no means of existence and being rejected by the entire market, traded his tobacco for worthless items to the local strongman Skorokhodov, who ground it into snuff and started selling it to the same poor people who immediately lined up. No more protests were heard.

Why is that? What is the difference between Jack, who grew tobacco, and Skorokhodov, who only resold it? Why were people willing to receive only free gifts from Jack, while there was no doubt that Skorokhodov needed to be paid?

The difference between them is huge - they come from different social classes. Jack is as poor as they are. Skorokhodov is their owner. In the minds of the heroes of this comedy, despite the revolution and profound social changes of that time, the formula of social “justice” has forever remained: we work, the owner gets richer. And if any American tries to question this dogma, even in our favor, we will all stand up to defend it. Skorokhodov didn’t have to do anything. The whole village defended his interests as the owner of capital.

Something similar happens in modern Russia, Ukraine, and many other countries, where people are accustomed to wealthy and all-powerful owners who “rightfully” possess the majority of all the products and services that these people create. In other words, the entire city of Penza works for a small number of individuals who are called thieves in the opposition press, but whose interests the whole city will rush to defend if their right to ownership of the city is ever questioned.

It’s not about stealing. Nobody is stealing anything, although technically the actions of officials can often be classified as such. The issue lies in the natural and historically inherited desire of the entire population for social inequality and material injustice.

Can this formula be modified to bring our consciousness closer to the consciousness of an average Swiss, for whom a fellow villager getting rich thanks to it is not a misunderstanding or a threat - that’s an interesting question. However, a much more interesting question sounds like this - is it necessary?

Maybe let Jack return to his America, and we are more accustomed to working on Skorokhodov’s.
