“Почему я не выступлю на ITEM”

I will tell you the whole story, and you decide whose side you are on. I often speak at IT conferences, and a lot. Usually, I don’t care who organizes them, whose money is involved, and how much they earn from them. What matters to me is who comes to listen to me and whether they are satisfied with what I can tell them. But this time it turned out to be more complicated, and I refused to speak at two conferences in my hometown.

A few weeks ago, I was invited to speak at the IT conference Run-IT in Dnipro. After looking at their schedule, I asked which specific session and time slot they wanted to assign me. Upon receiving the answer, I inquired about the opening speaker for the conference. Opening the conference is always a prestigious mission and those who are unquestionably trusted by the entire audience are invited for it. The response was Yanika Merilo (Deputy Mayor of Dnipro).

I asked what valuable contribution the deputy mayor had made to the IT industry to open a conference where programmers would be speaking. The response was, and I quote, “We were strongly recommended to do so”. I shrugged my shoulders and refused to speak.

Then I became curious if the same thing would happen at another Dnieper conference ITEM, where my presentation had already been accepted. I asked the organizers from a distance, “Will there be officials at the conference?” And I received the answer, “No, of course not, only Yanika will speak at the opening.”

I became interested again in what exactly she did for IT? What has she done to deserve the honor of opening the conference, apart from her well-known invitation to “enter the beautiful world of IT”? The answer was, and I quote: “If we don’t befriend the city authorities, we will be banned.” I shrugged again. So, it turns out that’s how things are with IT and the city authorities in Dnipro.

Programmers pay money to come and listen to me and several dozen of my colleagues who create software products, write books, and develop new technologies. And officials from the City Council, led by Filatov, using administrative resources, put pressure on the organizers, forcing them to place their people prominently among the speakers. Thus, people like Merilo, without contributing anything significant to IT, earn themselves a reputation: later she can say that she was listed as one of the main speakers at the country’s most important IT conferences, which means she has a direct connection to the industry.

Undoubtedly, the City Council not only puts pressure on the organizers but also offers something in return. Both conferences explained to me in conversation, I quote: “The City Council helps us negotiate venues for the conference.” By “helping to negotiate,” it is obviously meant that there is also some administrative pressure on the owners of these venues in order to reduce the rental price of the halls. In other words, both ITEM and Run-IT not only do not resist this corruption but also indulge it.

So, apparently, the City Council pays for these two conferences. Not with money, of course, but with those favors that the corrupt authorities usually use to thank their cronies.

In this case, I have a fair question - why should we, as speakers, advertise Gorsvet’s people for free? Why should I spend a full working day to help someone who has no connection to my industry become a respected figure in it, and not get anything in return? Meanwhile, ITEM and Run-IT will earn.

This is, at least, unfair.

I suggested ITEM three possible solutions to this situation:

The first option is correct and honest: refuse the City Council. However, there may be problems with the venue - its cost will increase. Let’s tell them that we are against corruption, and therefore we need to add an additional $10 to the cost of the entrance ticket. We don’t want to dance to the tune of the criminals from the City Council, and the conference will be opened by those who truly have weight in IT. I was ready to help with this. ITEM refused, arguing: “You can play fair when you have nothing to lose.”

Second option: pay me for the work. Since you receive something from the City Council for advertising their people, share it with the speakers. Since this event is held for the purpose of promoting Yanika Merilo, I want her to pay me for my work. ITEM refused.

Option three: remove me from the conference speakers. ITEM agreed.

So, I will not be speaking at ITEM this year, although I was planning to and had prepared an interesting presentation.

In summary, I suggest that all readers of this article, who are mainly, I am sure, people from the IT industry, think about whether it is worth attending the conferences that our criminal government uses for its own purposes. I propose that both speakers and listeners boycott such conferences.

This will be a real response to the corruption that is happening in the country.

Not in words, but in deeds.
