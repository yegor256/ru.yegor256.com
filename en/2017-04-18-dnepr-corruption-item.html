“Почему я не выступлю на ITEM”

I will tell the whole story, and you decide whose side you are on. I often speak at IT conferences and a lot. Usually, I don’t care who organizes them, whose money is involved, and how much they earn from them. What matters to me is who comes to listen to me and whether they are satisfied with what I can tell them. But this time it turned out to be more complicated, and I refused to speak at two conferences in my hometown.

A few weeks ago, I was invited to speak at the Run-IT IT conference in Dnipro. After looking at their schedule, I asked which session and time slot they wanted to put me in. After receiving an answer, I asked who would be speaking at the opening of the conference. Opening the conference is always a prestigious mission, and they invite those who clearly deserve the trust of the entire audience. The answer was Yannika Merilo (Deputy Mayor of Dnipro).

I asked what valuable contribution the Deputy Mayor had made to the IT industry to open the conference where programmers would be speaking. The answer was, I quote: “We were strongly recommended.” I shrugged my shoulders and refused to speak.

Next, I became interested in whether the same thing would happen at another Dnipro conference, ITEM, where my presentation had already been accepted. I asked the organizers from afar: “Will there be government officials at the conference?” And I received the answer: “No, of course not, only Yannika will speak at the opening.”

I asked again, what exactly had she done for IT? What has she done to deserve the honor of opening the conference, other than her well-known invitation to “enter the wonderful world of IT”? The answer was, I quote: “If we don’t befriend the city authorities, we will be banned.” I shrugged my shoulders again. So, this is how things are with IT and the city authorities in Dnipro.

Programmers pay money to come and listen to me and several dozen of my colleagues who create software products, write books, and develop new technologies. And the officials from the City Council, led by Filatov, using administrative resources, put pressure on the organizers, forcing them to place their people prominently among the speakers. Thus, people like Merilo, without contributing anything to IT, earn themselves a name: later she can say that she was listed as one of the main speakers at the country’s most important IT conferences, which means she has a direct connection to the industry.

Undoubtedly, the City Council not only puts pressure on the organizers but also offers something in return. Both conferences explained to me in conversation, I quote: “The City Council helps us negotiate venues for the conference.” By “helping to negotiate,” it is obviously necessary to understand some administrative pressure on the owners of these venues to lower the rental price. That is, both ITEM and Run-IT not only do not resist this corruption but also cater to it.

So, apparently, the City Council pays these two conferences. Not with money, of course, but with those services with which corrupt authorities usually thank their minions.

In that case, I have a fair question - why should we, the speakers, advertise the City Council’s people for free? Why should I spend a full working day helping someone who has nothing to do with my industry become a respected figure in it, and not get anything in return? While ITEM and Run-IT will make money.

This is, at the very least, unfair.

I offered ITEM three options to resolve this situation:

The first option is right and honest: refuse the City Council. However, there will likely be problems with the venue - its cost will increase. I suggested to the organizers - let’s make this situation public. Let’s bring this issue to all the programmers attending the conference. Let’s tell them that we are against corruption, and therefore, we need to add an extra $10 to the ticket price. We don’t want to dance to the tune of the criminals from the City Council, and those who truly matter in IT will open the conference. I was ready to help with this. ITEM refused, arguing: “You can play fair when you have nothing to lose.”

The second option: pay me for my work. Since you receive something from the City Council for advertising their people, share it with the speakers. Since this event is being held for the purpose of promoting Yannika Merilo, I want her to pay me for my work. ITEM refused.

The third option: remove me from the list of speakers at the conference. ITEM agreed.

So, I will not speak at ITEM this year, even though I planned to and prepared an interesting presentation.

To summarize, I suggest that all those reading this article, mostly people from the IT industry, think about whether it is worth attending conferences that our criminal government uses for their purposes. I propose that both speakers and listeners boycott such conferences.

This will be a real response to the corruption that is happening in the country.

Not in words, but in actions.
