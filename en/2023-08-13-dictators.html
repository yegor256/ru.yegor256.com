If we believe historians, first there were empires, emperors, and slavery. Then the empires fell because they became ashamed, and the dark years of medieval feudalism came, which at some point bored all enlightened people, and “under the cries of newly discovered redskins” came the New Time, and then the Renaissance. Slavery was abolished not immediately, but democracy was not immediately introduced. All former slaves were given equal rights by printing laws and constitutions for them. Kings and emperors were either hanged or renamed as “constitutional” monarchies, thereby freeing the individual from the oppression of other individuals, but in vain…

The main achievement of many years of our struggle for freedom is the recognition of the right of every individual to have their own beliefs. What prevented them from having them before the victory of liberalism? Not what, but who. Other individuals hindered the freedom-loving individual, who, unsurprisingly, also had their own convictions. From the king to the local landlord, everyone had their own opinion, which the serf had to take into account. And not just consider, but obey someone else’s will, sometimes under the threat of death. It’s good that all of this is already in the distant past.

Liberal reforms depersonalized power, replacing the owner with parliament, and his good or bad will with laws. In dark times, runaway peasants were returned to the feudal lord, who both executed and showed mercy. In today’s victorious democracy, border violators are caught by the customs control service, and the penitentiary system punishes them. Interestingly, we are satisfied with the power of laws, while the power of a specific person causes rejection. We are much more willing to be slaves to the constitution, the police, and society than to an individual. We have replaced the power of specific authorities with the authority of an anonymous power.

The USA, as the guardian of liberalism, has been attacking its opponents with the same weapon for many decades: “the lack of power alternation speaks of restrictions on freedom!” It doesn’t matter how the dictator behaves - their prolonged presence in power is a threat to the liberal world order. No matter what freedoms they grant to their subjects, no matter how successful the economy is, no matter how just their will is, and no matter what support they enjoy from their people - they are usurpers and medieval tyrants. The freedom-loving people are obligated to overthrow them and establish a power of impersonal laws and corrupt parliaments.

Neither power nor resources can be depersonalized, they cannot belong to everyone at once. A person is not only a social being but also a herd animal.
