I remember when I was four years old. There was a New Year’s morning performance at the kindergarten. Of course, with the fabulous Father Frost and his no less fabulous Snow Maiden. A fairy tale is a fairy tale, but after the holiday, I tried to explain to my parents that Father Frost was actually our janitor Ivan Ilyich. Unfortunately, I didn’t have direct evidence at that time, but there were several indirect clues: the voice, eyes, and shoes of the fairy tale character were very similar to Uncle Vanya’s attributes. At that time, I was very upset by my parents’ unwillingness to listen to my arguments. They persuaded me that Father Frost was real and that the janitor, who for some reason was not at the performance, simply got sick that day.

Many years passed, and I learned that my childhood desire to find an alternative explanation for what was happening, contrary to the generally accepted version, is called a conspiracy theory. Like adult conspiracy theorists, I didn’t have indisputable evidence at the time, and I couldn’t uncover the conspiracy. Ded Moroz (Russian version of Santa Claus) was exposed later when I became a full member of the closed group “adults,” where children are not allowed for their own good. In the end, I learned the main thing - being a conspiracy theorist is dangerous and unprofitable. No one will believe you anyway, and you will waste a lot of nerves and energy.

Later, much more serious stories replaced the harmless Grandfather, which were even harder to believe than in a fairy-tale character, despite the insistence of all official sources on this. For example, what about the story of how two American astronauts were stranded on the Moon in 1969, spent twenty-one hours there, then pressed the “Home” button, took off, docked with the spacecraft that was waiting for them in lunar orbit, turned it around, and rushed to the frozen Earth, anxiously waiting. Do you doubt that it happened exactly like this? You are a conspiracy theorist and a supporter of the moon landing hoax.

Or take for example the unfinished story of how the planet was struck by a previously unknown to science coronavirus, spreading at the speed of the medieval plague and threatening all of us with inevitable and agonizing death… from a runny nose. As evidence: graphs of the virus’s spread, interviews with virologist doctors, and photographs of hospitals under construction. Is that not enough for you? Do you think that the global media is deceiving you, that the numbers are falsified, and that COVID-19 patients do not exist? You are a crazy conspiracy theorist, and taking your doubts seriously and discussing them is a waste of time.

To be fair, it is worth acknowledging that not all conspiracy theories are capable of discovering a janitor’s mustache under the guise of Ded Moroz (Russian Santa Claus). Many of them are unable to even provoke a smile, for example, seriously claiming that the Earth is flat, all clockmakers have conspired, and Australia does not exist. However, now is not the time to discuss them or who is right: Holocaust deniers or those who build memorials for it. I am sure that each of them has their own truth.

In any legend, whether it’s about Ded Moroz, Americans on the Moon, or the epidemic of the Chinese virus, there are interested parties who benefit from the fact that the majority of us continue to believe in the legend as truth, while the minority is left with the demeaning status of conspiracy theorists. If we consider our civilization as a closed system, it is logical to assume that as long as the beneficiary is not overthrown and the legend is alive, this situation is beneficial to everyone. In other words, it will be better for all of us if the Americans’ journey to the Moon is considered a fact and the coronavirus epidemic is seen as a real threat.

For example, once we were sure that the Sun revolves around the Earth, and all other versions were considered heresy and conspiracy theories. We lived happily until the time came and the legend and the truth swapped places. Another example, in the 19th century, Hungarian doctor Ignaz Philipp Semmelweis suggested that the mortality rate among childbirth patients could be reduced if the hospital staff washed their hands before surgery, for which he was ridiculed and fired (antiseptics and bacteria were discovered much later). Conspiracy theorists and insane authors of insane ideas disrupt our ecosystem, and it, in turn, resists. At some point, it gives in, and yesterday’s heresy is recognized as the truth, and yesterday’s truth is seen as innocent delusion. Until such a revolution occurs, the system remains in a state of relative rest, and the majority of its members (us and you) have an interest in this.

The viral hysteria, unfolding before our eyes, has if not authors, then a number of interested parties. Mass antivirus psychosis will lead to an increase in hygiene, which in turn will lead to a weakening of immunity, which will lead to higher mortality and population reduction on a global scale, the importance of which I have already written. Thus, a relatively inexpensive campaign successfully contributes to solving the global problem of overpopulation of the planet. Is this beneficial to us? Individually for each of us - of course not. For civilizations as a whole - yes.
