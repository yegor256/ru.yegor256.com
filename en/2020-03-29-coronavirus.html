I remember, I was four years old. There was a New Year’s performance in kindergarten. Of course, with the fabulous Father Frost and his no less fabulous Snow Maiden. Fairy tale is a fairy tale, but at the end of the holiday, I tried to explain to my parents that Father Frost was actually our janitor Ivan Ilyich. Unfortunately, I didn’t have direct evidence back then, but there were several indirect clues: the voice, eyes, and shoes of the fairy-tale character were very similar to the same attributes of Uncle Vanya. At that time, I was very upset by my parents’ unwillingness to listen to my arguments. They were persuading me that Father Frost was real, and the janitor, who for some reason was not at the performance, simply fell ill that day.

Many years passed, and I learned that my childhood desire to find an alternative explanation for what was happening contrary to the generally accepted version is called a conspiracy theory. Like adult conspiracy theorists, I didn’t have undeniable evidence at that time, and I couldn’t expose the conspiracy. Ded Moroz (Russian equivalent of Santa Claus) was exposed later, when I became a full member of the closed group “adults,” where children are not allowed for their own good. In the end, I learned the main thing - being a conspiracy theorist is dangerous and unprofitable. No one will believe you anyway, and you will waste a lot of nerves and energy.

Later, much more serious stories replaced the harmless Grandfather, in which it was even harder to believe than in a fairy-tale character, despite the insistence of all the official sources. For example, there is the story about how two American astronauts were stranded on the Moon in 1969, spent twenty-one hours there, then pressed the “Home” button, took off, docked with the spacecraft waiting for them in lunar orbit, turned it around, and rushed towards the frozen Earth in anxious anticipation. Do you doubt that everything was exactly like that? You are a conspiracy theorist and a supporter of the moon landing conspiracy.

Or take for example the unfinished story about how the planet was struck by a previously unknown to science coronavirus, spreading at the speed of the medieval plague and threatening all of us with inevitable and agonizing death … from a runny nose. As evidence: graphs of the virus spread, interviews with virologist doctors, and photographs of the construction of hospitals. Is this not enough for you? Do you think that the world media is deceiving you, that the numbers are falsified, and that COVID-19 patients do not exist? You are a crazy conspiracy theorist, and taking your doubts seriously and discussing them is a waste of time.

To be fair, it is worth acknowledging that not all conspiracy theories are capable of discovering the caretaker’s mustache under the guise of Ded Moroz. Many of them are unable to even provoke a smile, for example, by seriously claiming that the Earth is flat, that all watchmakers conspired, or that Australia does not exist. However, it is not about them now, nor about who is right: Holocaust deniers or the builders of its memorials. I am sure that each of them has their own truth.

In any legend, whether it’s about Ded Moroz, Americans on the Moon, or an epidemic of the Chinese virus, there are interested parties who benefit from the fact that the majority of us continue to believe in the legend as truth, leaving the minority satisfied with the demeaning status of conspiracy theorists. If we consider our civilization as a closed system, it is logical to assume that as long as the beneficiary is not overthrown and the legend is alive, this situation benefits everyone. In other words, it will be better for all of us if the Americans’ journey to the Moon is considered a fact and the coronavirus epidemic is seen as a real threat.

For example, at one point we were convinced that the Sun revolves around the Earth, and all other versions were considered heresy and conspiracy theories. We lived happily until the time came and the legend and the truth switched places. Another example, in the 19th century, Hungarian doctor Ignaz Philipp Semmelweis suggested that mortality among childbirth patients could be reduced if hospital staff washed their hands before surgery, for which he was ridiculed and fired (antiseptics and bacteria were discovered much later). Conspiracy theorists and mad authors of crazy ideas disrupt our ecosystem, and it, in turn, resists. At some point, it gives in, and yesterday’s heresy is recognized as truth, and yesterday’s truth is seen as innocent misconception. Until this revolution occurs, the system remains in a state of relative peace, and the majority of its members (including us) are interested in this.

The viral hysteria unfolding before our eyes has, if not authors, a number of interested parties. At the same time, its main beneficiary is you and me, obediently wearing masks and washing our hands for 20 seconds. What is our benefit? There are many, but I like this version. Mass antivirus psychosis will lead to enhanced hygiene, which in turn will lead to weakened immunity, which will lead to higher mortality rates and a reduction in population on a global scale, the importance of which I have already written about. Thus, this relatively inexpensive campaign successfully contributes to solving the global problem of overpopulation. Is this beneficial for us? Individually, for each of us - of course not. For civilizations as a whole - yes.

However, it is still beneficial for humanity to have a legend in which hundreds of millions of children believe. Does each of us need a viral epidemic that kills small businesses and slows down the economy? No. However, it seems that we all need it together.
