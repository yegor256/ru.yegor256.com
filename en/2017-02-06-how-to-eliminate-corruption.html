“Как нам победить коррупцию?”

Ukraine is still one of the countries with the highest level of corruption in the world. On one hand, it’s good when you can steal and bribe. On the other hand, it’s sad to realize that Ukrainians continue to be one of the dumbest nations on the planet, unable to organize the work of their officials and police in a way that prevents them from stealing. How can we become smarter? Are there any proven methods?

According to Robert Klitgaard, known for his research on the nature of this phenomenon, “corruption is greed plus opportunity minus control.” This formula implies that in order to eradicate corruption, a) the opportunities for officials need to be reduced, and b) control over them needs to be strengthened. However, reducing greed does not seem possible to me simply due to human nature - we always want more.

So, the first thing is the reduction of the officials’ powers and the ways to do it a lot. For example, in Georgia during Saakashvili’s time, the “one-stop shop” principle was introduced (also applied in other countries) in order to reduce the number of procedures for obtaining state documents, and therefore reduce the number of signatures that they put for a bribe.

The second part of the formula requires increased control over officials, which primarily involves tightening punishments and direct punitive actions. For example, Hong Kong is known for its concept of “presumption of guilt” for officials, where each of them is considered a thief until proven otherwise. The experience of Singapore is also illustrative, where under the leadership of Lee Kuan Yew, anti-corruption legislation was tightened to such an extent that, for example, even the promise of a bribe became considered a bribe. And China is ahead of everyone in this regard - over the past ten years, more than 10,000 officials have been executed for corruption.

Analyzing the situation in Ukraine, several years ago Saakashvili stated that the current state system is one that “does not allow not to steal.” The wording remarkably combines all three components of the formula described above: 1) officials’ salaries are meager, 2) there are many opportunities to steal, and 3) there is little control. Only the lazy and foolish would not steal.

Everything seems logical in this formula, and the solution is obvious for both Ukraine and any other country: 1) increase salaries for officials, 2) reduce their number and simplify the procedures they control, and finally 3) regularly execute violators. However, there is another important aspect that many people pay attention to - people’s attitude towards corruption. The people who, through their contributions, sustain officials and from whose pockets they steal.

Kaha Bendukidze, drawing analogies between cargo cults in Papua New Guinea and the consciousness of contemporary residents of post-Soviet territories, said that “if we want to be mature societies, we need not to receive our rules of life like manna from heaven, but rather to achieve them ourselves,” and then added that “to fight corruption, one must want it strongly.” The main idea is that only we, together, can achieve new rules of life without corruption if we want it and strive for it.

Vitaliy Portnikov, in his analysis of the corruption fight in Ukraine, writes that the success of this fight “may not be related to government measures, repression, or punishments, but to public morality,” referring to the urgent need for changes in our consciousness before any anti-corruption measures can take effect.

In my deep conviction, the first step in this fight should be a change in our attitude towards wealth of officials. Most often it causes envy in us, but it should provoke anger. We must understand that their expensive cars are primarily a sign of our own stupidity. They are stolen from us, they drive on our own streets, and we just watch and do nothing.

Moreover, we need to change our attitude towards officials in general. It is important to understand that they are our service staff, living off our money. We are forced to keep them close in order to organize our lives and, to put it bluntly, take out the trash and clean the roads. The head of the regional tax office or the deputy head of the customs should be much less significant to us than, for example, the head chef or the supermarket director.

The new rules that Kaha Bendukidze spoke about will involve exactly such a transformation of the system: the reverent and timid attitude towards officials will be replaced by a calm and utilitarian one. After this, all other changes will occur naturally, and corruption will turn from a widespread disaster into a localized problem, as in developed countries.
