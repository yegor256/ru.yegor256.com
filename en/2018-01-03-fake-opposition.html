“План "Фиктивная Оппозиция"”

The main task of any government regime is survival. The main potential threat to this is always the people oppressed by this regime. The most active and determined representatives of the people, united around a common goal, are called the opposition. The opposition’s transition to decisive actions results in rallies, demonstrations, strikes, riots, and acts of terrorism. Unsuccessful attempts are classified as a state coup and are harshly punished by the regime. Successful attempts are given the proud name of Revolution and are celebrated. The new regime.

In order to avoid watching the anniversary celebration of the revolution on the television cameras of the detention center, the regime needs to keep the opposition under control. It is necessary to prevent them from taking decisive actions. For this purpose, they can be pursued, punished, frightened, and destroyed. However, the martyr role of opposition revolutionaries only stimulates their enthusiasm and attracts new supporters. Only a regime of strong dictatorship (Lukashenko, Kim Jong-un, Berdymukhamedov) can afford open repressive measures against the opposition.

The weak regime, taking its first steps towards true authoritarianism (Russia, Ukraine), finds it more beneficial to use the method of fictitious opposition, which consists of the following. The most prominent, loud, and audacious figures are chosen from the opposition movement, whose slogans are filled with hatred towards the regime, but completely exclude any constructive ideas (such as “Putin is a thief” or “Poroshenko is the supreme profiteer”). These figures become the spokespersons of the opposition (Navalny, Saakashvili). The regime minimally obstructs them, only enough to not strip them of their status as revolutionary heroes (regularly arresting and releasing them).

Soon, the majority of those dissatisfied with the regime gathers around these leaders. Over time, the percentage of those capable of meaningful and constructive revolutionary actions among the gathered begins to decline. This is mainly due to the intensive influx of enthusiastic admirers of catchy, and often vulgar, slogans. In terms of quality, the composition of the opposition mass descends to the level of an ordinary citizen, completely unable to inflict any significant harm to the regime.

Opposition leaders, being public figures, are entirely dependent on the opinions and desires of their audience. Being hostages of those they have gathered around themselves, they are forced to generate the information product that their opposition electorate demands, namely, public abuse towards those in power. However crude and offensive this opposition may sound, it is completely harmless to the regime, because those who are truly capable of carrying out a coup will not rally behind its slogans. By the time the main opposition leaders triumph, such people simply do not exist among their supporters.

The regime does not hinder the development of the fake opposition, but on the contrary, actively contributes to it. The louder and cruder their slogans sound, the more incapable population they gather around themselves, and the fewer truly dangerous fighters against the regime remain there. At the same time, the real revolution loses its chances of winning over minds because its voice sounds quieter and less convincing against the categorical, vivid, bold, and primitive slogans.

Russia has been following this path for a long time. Now Ukraine is also adopting this approach.
