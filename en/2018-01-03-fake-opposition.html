“План "Фиктивная Оппозиция"”

The main goal of any state regime is survival. The main potential threat to this is always the people who are oppressed by this regime. The most active and decisive representatives of the people, united around a common goal, are called the opposition. The transition of the opposition to decisive actions results in rallies, demonstrations, strikes, riots, and terrorist attacks. Unsuccessful attempts are qualified as a coup d’état and brutally punished by the regime. Successful ones are given the proud name of Revolution and celebrated. The new regime.

In order to not watch the anniversary celebration of the revolution on the television in a detention center, the regime needs to keep the opposition under control. It is necessary to prevent it from taking decisive actions. To do this, it can persecute, punish, intimidate, and destroy it. However, the martyr role of opposition-revolutionaries only stimulates their enthusiasm and attracts new supporters. Only a regime of strong dictatorship (Lukashenko, Kim Jong-un, Berdimuhamedov) can afford open repressive measures against the opposition.

For a weak regime taking its first steps towards true authoritarianism (Russia, Ukraine), it is more advantageous to use the method of fictitious opposition, which consists of the following. The most vibrant, loud, and audacious figures are selected from the opposition movement, whose slogans are filled with hatred towards the regime but completely exclude any constructive ideas (such as “Putin is a thief” or “Poroshenko is the supreme profiteer”). These figures become the mouthpieces of the opposition (Navalny, Saakashvili). The regime only minimally obstructs them, just enough to not deprive them of the status of revolutionary heroes (regularly arresting and releasing them).

Soon, the majority of those dissatisfied with the regime gather around these leaders. Over time, the percentage of those capable of meaningful and constructive revolutionary actions among those gathered begins to decrease. This is mainly due to the intensive influx of enthusiastic admirers of catchy and often vulgar slogans. In terms of quality, the composition of the opposition masses descends to the level of ordinary citizens who are completely incapable of causing any significant harm to the regime.

Opposition leaders, being public figures, are entirely dependent on the opinions and desires of their audience. Being hostages of those they have gathered around them, they are forced to generate the informational product demanded by their opposition electorate, which is mainly public insults directed at those in power. However crude and offensive this opposition may sound, it is completely harmless to the regime because those who are truly capable of carrying out a coup d’état will not follow its slogans. By the time the main opposition leaders triumph, there are simply no such people among their supporters.

The regime does not hinder the development of fictitious opposition, but on the contrary, actively promotes it. The louder and more aggressive their slogans sound, the more incapable population they will gather around them, and the fewer truly dangerous fighters against the regime will remain there. At the same time, the chance for a real revolution to capture minds diminishes because its voice sounds quieter and less convincing against the backdrop of categorical, vibrant, audacious, and primitive slogans.

Russia has long been following this path. Now Ukraine is picking up this method.
