“Мы не рабы, рабы не мы”

In order to be free, one must be a slave to the law - the meaning of this famous statement by Marcus Tullius Cicero is proposed to be revealed to several generations of high school graduates during literature exams. It is expected that by the time they come of age, students should understand that only a society that follows the rules and respects the accepted laws will survive and prosper. According to the school program, the only alternative to the “dictatorship of the law” is chaos, in which society inevitably degrades and collapses.

It sounds logical, but not everything is so straightforward. Let’s think about it, and two interesting facts will help us:

Firstly, in the first century BC in the Roman Empire, slavery was legalized. According to Wikipedia, Marcus Tullius Cicero, being “one of the most prominent and consistent supporters of the preservation of the republican system,” had personal slaves whom he treated as his own property strictly in accordance with the laws he respected so much. Moreover, the slave uprising led by Spartacus was completely suppressed just a few years before Cicero uttered his famous phrase during a defense speech in court.

Secondly, two decades later, as soon as the troops of the Second Triumvirate occupied Rome, Cicero’s name was included in the proscription lists of “enemies of the people,” and he was caught not far from his Greek villa and beheaded. This was done strictly in accordance with the very law that Marcus Tullius respected so much and called us to be slaves to.

In both cases, we see that becoming a slave, even to such a respected “master” as the law, is not the most rational way to dispose of one’s life. Both Spartacus, as a slave, and the worthy Roman Cicero perished at the hands of those who acted within the framework of the law. The only difference is that Spartacus resisted.

The inspiration for writing this article was the recent story of Vladimir Olentsevich, who attempted to kill Anna Turchynova, the wife of Alexander Turchynov, the secretary of the National Security and Defense Council of Ukraine, with a knife. The attempt failed, and Vladimir will spend the next eight years of his life in prison.

What did this person achieve? What did he want to achieve? He is a lawyer who came from Donetsk, where he previously sought justice through legal means. Apparently, he failed, and he took a knife in his hands.

Most of us do not act like this, preferring to remain slaves to the law until the end, like Cicero, who “stretched out his neck towards the sword,” according to Plutarch.

Drunk employees of the Security Service of Ukraine run over people at pedestrian crossings - we observe, disapprove, and pass by. A judge kills people in a traffic accident and continues to work as a judge - we read about it in the news and turn the page. Billions are being stolen from our budget - we finish our coffee, envy the corrupt officials, and go to work.

We do not take a knife in our hands and do not seek revenge on those who, in the name of the law and under its cover, turn us into slaves.

Should Cicero have learned something from Spartacus? Maybe we can learn something from Vladimir Olentsevich as well? Maybe it’s worth being only partially slaves to the law? Or maybe instead of being slaves to the law, we should become slaves to common sense? Or maybe we should forget about slavery altogether and learn to be masters of our own lives, who know how and want to defend their freedom, even from the law?
