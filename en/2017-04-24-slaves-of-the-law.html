“Мы не рабы, рабы не мы”

“To be free, one must be slaves to the law” - the meaning of this well-known statement by Marcus Tullius Cicero is proposed to be revealed not only to generations of high school graduates during literature exams. It is expected that by the time they come of age, students should understand that only a society that follows the rules and respects generally accepted laws will survive and thrive. According to the school program, the only alternative to the “dictatorship of the law” is chaos, in which society inevitably degrades and collapses.

It sounds logical, however, not everything is so unambiguous. Let’s think about it, and two interesting facts will help us:

Firstly, in the first century BC in the Roman Empire, slavery was legalized. According to Wikipedia, Marcus Tullius, being “one of the most prominent and consistent supporters of the preservation of the republican system,” had personal slaves whom he disposed of as his own property strictly in accordance with the laws he respected so much. Moreover, the uprising of the slaves led by Spartacus was completely suppressed just a few years before Cicero delivered his famous phrase during his defense speech in court.

Secondly, two decades later, once the forces of the Second Triumvirate occupied Rome, Cicero’s name was included in the proscription lists of “enemies of the people” and he was caught not far from his Greek villa and beheaded. This was done strictly in accordance with the very law that Marcus Tullius respected so much and urged us to be slaves to.

In both cases, we see that being a slave, even to such a respected “master” as the law, is not the most rational way to dispose of one’s life. Both Spartacus, as a slave, and the worthy Roman Cicero perished at the hands of those who acted within the framework of the law. The only difference is that Spartacus resisted.

What did this person achieve? What did he want to achieve? He is a lawyer who came from Donetsk, where he previously sought justice through legal means. Apparently, he was unsuccessful, so he took a knife in his hands.

Most of us do not act this way, preferring to remain slaves to the law until the end, like Cicero, who “stretched out his neck to meet the sword himself,” according to Plutarch.
