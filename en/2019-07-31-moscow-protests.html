Uprisings, protests, and revolutions can have various nature, but essentially, they can probably be divided into two categories. The first one is rebellion. Bright examples: Krasnodar (1961), Alexandrov (1961), Tambov (1920-1921), and finally Spartacus Uprising. The cause of rebellion is mass dissatisfaction of the population with living conditions, namely hunger and/or violence. Life becomes worse and worse, people cannot bear it anymore, their patience bursts, and they take to the streets without fearing punishment, because they have no strength left.

The second category, to which most mass protest movements can be attributed, is the change of power. One power group fights against another for access to resources and uses mass unrest as a pressure tool. The most vivid examples of recent times are Euromaidan in Kiev in 2014.

Undoubtedly, it is quite difficult to draw a clear line between the rebellion of an oppressed people and the organized transfer of power under the guise of the people’s struggle against tyranny. Of course, during the events on Maidan in Kiev in 2014, there were many who sincerely were dissatisfied with their lives and hoped to change something by overthrowing Yanukovych. Certainly, during the protest on Tverskaya Street in Moscow last Sunday, there were many who sincerely believed that by replacing Putin with Navalny, they would be able to improve their personal well-being. And of course, every rebellion inevitably mobilizes all the processes of power transfer that have matured by its beginning. And naturally, any rebellion mostly brings to power those who have been fighting for it in one way or another even before the mass actions began.
