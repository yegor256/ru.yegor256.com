Rebellions, protests, and revolutions can have very different nature, but essentially they can probably be divided into two categories. The first one is a revolt. Bright examples: Krasnodar (1961), Alexandrov (1961), Tambov (1920-1921), and finally the Spartacus uprising. The cause of a revolt is mass dissatisfaction of the population with living conditions, specifically hunger and/or violence. Life becomes increasingly worse, people can’t bear it anymore, their patience bursts, and they take to the streets, not fearing punishment because they have no strength left.

The second category, to which most mass protest movements can be attributed, is a change of power. One power group fights against another for access to resources and uses mass disorders as a pressure tool. The most vivid examples in recent times are Euromaidan in Kiev in 2014.

Undoubtedly, it is quite difficult to draw a clear line between the rebellion of an oppressed people and the organized transfer of power under the guise of the people’s struggle against tyranny. Of course, at the rally last Sunday on Tverskaya Street in Moscow, there were many who sincerely believed that by replacing Putin with Navalny, they would be able to improve their personal well-being. And, of course, every rebellion inevitably mobilizes all the processes of power transfer that have matured by its beginning.
