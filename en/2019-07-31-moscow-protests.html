It is impossible to organize a mass rally with just one call to fight for “freedom and respect”.

Rebellions, protests, and revolutions can have the most diverse nature, but essentially, they can probably be divided into two categories. The first is a riot. Bright examples include Krasnodar (1961), Alexandrov (1961), Tambov (1920-1921), and finally, the Spartacus uprising. The cause of the riot is the mass dissatisfaction of the population with living conditions, namely hunger and/or violence. Life becomes worse and worse, people can’t bear it, their patience bursts, and they take to the streets without fear of punishment, as they have no strength left.

The second category, to which most mass protest movements can be attributed, is change of power. One power group fights against another for access to resources and uses mass disorders as a pressure tool. The most prominent examples of recent times are Euromaidan in Kiev in 2014.

Undoubtedly, it is quite difficult to draw a clear line between the rebellion of an oppressed people and the organized transfer of power under the guise of the people’s struggle against tyranny. Of course, during the Maidan protests in Kiev in 2014, there were many who sincerely were dissatisfied with their lives and hoped to bring about some change by overthrowing Yanukovych. Of course, during the recent protest on Tverskaya Street in Moscow last Sunday, there were many who sincerely believed that by replacing Putin with Navalny, they would be able to improve their personal well-being. And of course, every rebellion inevitably mobilizes all the processes of power transfer that have matured by its beginning. And naturally, any rebellion primarily brings to power those who, in one way or another, have been fighting for it even before the mass actions began.

However, sometimes the ears of power fighters stick out too much.

It is evident throughout that the protest movements in Russia in recent years should be attributed to the second category - power transfer. There are no objective reasons for dissatisfaction with life among Russians. Undoubtedly, Russia is not the most comfortable country to live in, and I have written about this many times. However, thanks to powerful propaganda and albeit slowly growing economy, there cannot be natural mass protest sentiments among the population. There are abuses, shortcomings, and corruption, but there are not enough conditions for a mass revolt against the existing system.
