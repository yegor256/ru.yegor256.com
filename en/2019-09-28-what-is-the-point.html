“Зачем?”

Recently, one of the readers of my political op-eds passionately asked me why I do this? What am I trying to achieve and why do I write? What am I calling for? Who am I with and against? What actions do I expect from my readers? He was also very interested in the question of where my homeland is: in Moscow or in Kyiv? The answer seemed to surprise and disappoint him.

Just recently, I read a new book by Eduard Limonov called “Azbuka” - a collection of thoughts on various topics, almost unrelated to each other - about politics, Donetsk, Putin, old age, violence, and of course, women. The text is juicy and frank, and the ideas are radical and sound, for example:

The people do not change power. The people are not able to change anything. They are scattered, attached to their home, their children, their conservative and apolitical wife (most women are apolitical, moreover, under an authoritarian regime, life is better for them, it is safer).

However, while I agree with the author almost everywhere, I was surprised by his naive bewilderment.

Do you, Eduard Veniaminovich, with prison and war behind you, not know how the consciousness of the masses works? Our consciousness. You provoke reflection and reasoning, while we await conclusions. You question elementary truths, which makes us uncomfortable. You offer analysis, while we await synthesis. For us, the squareheads, it is very important to know: Do you actually support Putin or not?!

Give us conclusions, directives, precise algorithms. Synthesize clarity for us, and we will follow you. Divide the world in half and tell us whose banners to stand under. However, you refuse to do so, it disgusts you, quoting:

You refuse to be dull with us, and we refuse to support you. However, it was not always like this.

Previous generations witnessed an open war between two ideologies. The newspaper “Pravda” told the truth, while Time magazine lied. And vice versa, depending on who was reading. It doesn’t matter who was actually right. What matters is that objectively the bipolar agenda gathered the intellectual elite around it. Where else could they gather if the world is polarized and it is certainly interesting to talk and think about it?! Limonov’s first books were written from exile, where he ended up after a conflict with the KGB — “we will either imprison you or you leave” — that’s how it was formulated, in his own words.

The vector of social conflict (capitalism versus communism) coincided with the vector of the most progressive thought (freedom or slavery). The best people were either in the Academy of Sciences or in exile. And there were geniuses and heated debates in both places, and the opportunity to find the truth.

However, the ideology of Lenin and Stalin lost. Limonov returned to Russia. He returned to a place where the war had already ended. There were no longer two camps, no dissidents, and no KGB — the fire of the world revolution had subsided. The intellectual elite returned from exile and celebrated victory, while the economy surrendered to the mercy of the winners — they brought us McDonald’s and Casio calculators, in exchange for oil and a hungry and illiterate market of 150 million people. We emerged from the grim interim of the 1990s without a new political agenda. The Soviet Union, from the “evil empire,” turned into a handful of underdeveloped colonies and people who no longer dreamed of fighting the yoke of capital, but became weaker and more cowardly. There was no longer a need to search for the truth — work for Uncle Sam and be happy.

Nevertheless, intellectuals with their eternal protest needed to be directed somewhere. We needed that very bipolarity that we lost by dismantling Iron Felix. As a result, intentionally or accidentally, a liberal movement emerged, for everything good and against everything bad. The “concerned intelligentsia” joined it because there was nothing else to join (certainly not the bloody regime of VVP!).

However, despite the simplified formula “down with Putin!” the world is no longer as simple as it was in Limonov’s youth. It is no longer divided into Uncle Sam and the proletariat, and therefore, by simply removing Putin and Co. from power, it is unlikely that all the problems can be solved as they were once, by rejecting all the achievements of October at once. I am convinced that liberals, unlike their thousands of subscribers, understand this, but they cannot do anything about it. In order for people to follow them, they need to shout loudly and clearly: “Down with…” and then a specific name.

It turns out to be really loud, but there is little common sense in this cry. Therefore, only those who are not accustomed to seeking meaning and … bored passers-by gather under these banners. As a result, three camps are formed instead of two, as before: 1) the authorities, 2) liberals, and 3) the thinking minority. There are no banners over the third camp, and no one is arrested there, and its numbers are constantly decreasing. It is there that one can find people like Limonov — the dying veterans of the Great War.

Once, in one of the restaurants in Moscow, I was asked to fill out a form to receive a permanent visitor card. The last question on the simple questionnaire was a sarcastic one, “Whose Crimea?” with a free field for filling in. It took me a few minutes to come up with a concise answer that was different from the primitive “ours” or “not ours.”

The world has become more complex, simple formulas like “ours vs. yours” or “freedom vs. slavery” are no longer capable of describing the reality that surrounds us. Anyone who wants to present the situation in the format of “for” or “against” is not just a fool. They are harmful. Whether they are liberals or Kremlin propagandists.

Why am I writing this blog? To instill in you, my readers, a love for questionnaires with free fields. If we can fill in the free fields, then there will be real freedom.
