“Зачем?”

Recently, one of the readers of my political essays passionately asked me why I do this? What am I trying to achieve and why do I write? What am I calling for? Who am I for and with whom? What actions do I push readers towards and what actions do I expect from them? He was also very interested in the question of where my homeland truly is: in Moscow or in Kiev? The answer, it seemed, surprised and disappointed him.

Recently, I read a new book by Eduard Limonov called “Azbuka” - a collection of thoughts on various topics, almost unrelated to each other - about politics, Donbass, Putin, old age, violence, and of course, women. The text is juicy and candid, and the ideas are radical and sound, for example:

We are constantly being pushed into the shit of revolution, claiming that the number of victims of revolution speaks in favor of evolution. But has anyone bothered to count the number of victims of evolution? All those who died from alcoholism, suicide, depression? How many children were not born out of the despair and hopelessness of the previous generation? Now they have forced us to live according to evolution. I am familiar with thousands of people who were unable to fulfill their talents. And they are rotting away on their feet.

Или вот это:

The people do not change power. The people are not capable of changing anything. They are scattered, attached to their home, to their children, to their conservative and apolitical wife (most women are apolitical, and moreover, under an authoritarian regime, life is better for them, it is safer).

Однако, при том, что я согласен с автором почти везде, меня удивило его наивное недоумение:

Square-headed liberals chatter that I supposedly support Putin. But I have more disagreements with VVP than all of their stupid and deceitful liberal clique.

Do you, Eduard Veniaminovich, with prison and war behind your back, not know how the mass consciousness works? Our consciousness. You provoke reflections and reasoning, while we wait for conclusions. You question elementary truths, which makes us uncomfortable. You offer analysis, while we await synthesis. For us, squareheads, it is very important to know: Do you actually support Putin or not?!

Give us conclusions, directives, precise algorithms. Synthesize clarity for us, and we will follow you. Divide the world in half and tell us under whose banner we should stand. However, you refuse to do so, it disgusts you, quoting:

Вы хотите, чтобы все было “за” или “против”? Обратитесь к КПРФ. Это они такие тупые.

Вы отказываетесь тупеть вместе с нами, а мы отказываемся Вас поддерживать. Однако, так было не всегда.

Previous generations witnessed an open war between two ideologies. The newspaper “Pravda” told the truth, while Time magazine lied. However, it depended on who was reading. It doesn’t matter who was actually right. What matters is that objectively the bipolar agenda gathered the intellectual elite around it. Where else could they gather if the world is polarized? It is certainly interesting to talk and think about it! The first books by Limonov were written from emigration, where he ended up after a conflict with the KGB - “either we’ll imprison you or you leave” - that’s how he formulated it, in his own words.

The vector of social conflict (capitalism versus communism) coincided with the vector of the most progressive thought (freedom or slavery). The best people were either in the academy of sciences or in exile. And both places had geniuses, heated debates, and the opportunity to find the truth.

However, the ideology of Lenin and Stalin lost. Limonov returned to Russia. He returned to a place where the war had already ended. There were no longer two camps, no dissidents, and no KGB - the fire of the world revolution had faded. The intellectual elite returned from exile and celebrated victory, while the economy surrendered to the mercy of the victors - they brought us McDonald’s and Casio calculators, in exchange for oil and a 150-million hungry and illiterate market. We emerged from the gloomy interlude of the 90s without a new political agenda. The Soviet Union, once an “empire of evil,” turned into a handful of underdeveloped colonies, no longer aspiring to fight against the yoke of capital, and people became weaker and more cowardly. There was no longer a need to search for the truth - work for Uncle Sam and be happy.

Nevertheless, intellectuals with their eternal protest needed to be directed somewhere. We needed that very bipolarity that we lost by dismantling Iron Felix. As a result, intentionally or accidentally, the liberal movement emerged, for everything good and against everything bad. The “concerned intelligentsia” joined it, because there was nothing else to join (not the bloody regime of VVP!).

However, despite the simplified formula “down with Putin!”, the world is no longer as simple as in Limonov’s youth. It is no longer divided into Uncle Sam and the proletariat, and therefore, only by removing Putin and Co. from power, it is unlikely that all the problems can be solved as they once were, by instantly rejecting all the achievements of the October Revolution. I am convinced that liberals, unlike their thousands of followers, understand this, but they cannot do anything about it. In order for others to follow them, they need to shout loudly and clearly: “Down with…” and then a very specific name.

It turns out to be really loud, but there is little common sense in this cry. That is why only those who are not used to looking for meaning gather under the banners, as well as bored passers-by. As a result, three camps are formed instead of two, as before: 1) the authorities, 2) liberals, and 3) the thinking minority. The third camp has no banners and nobody gets arrested there, and its numbers are constantly decreasing. It is exactly there where you can find people like Limonov - the dying veterans of the Great War.

Once, in one of the restaurants in Moscow, I was asked to fill out a form in order to receive a permanent visitor card. The last item of the simple questionnaire was a sarcastic question “Whose Crimea?” with a blank field for filling in. It took me a few minutes to come up with a concise response, different from the primitive “ours” or “not ours”.

The world has become more complex, simple formulas like “ours vs. yours” or “freedom vs. slavery” are no longer able to describe the reality surrounding us. Anyone who wants to present the situation in terms of “for” or “against” is not just a fool. They are harmful individuals. Whether they are liberals or Kremlin propagandists.

Why am I writing this blog? To instill in you, my readers, a love for forms with open fields. If we can fill in open fields, there will be true freedom.
