“Калиброванная демократия”

Democracy is the worst form of government. Power belongs to the crowd, which is incapable of strategic thinking and consists mostly of ignorant people who make impulsive decisions. This is what Aristotle believed in the 2nd century BC. He considered a more effective system of government to be one in which power belongs to those who have more authority and are therefore able to make more considered and knowledgeable decisions.

Contrary to the thoughts of the Greek philosopher, democracy is currently the functioning form of government in most countries in the world. Even in those countries where monarchy is preserved, such as Great Britain or Spain, the actual leadership of the country is carried out by the cabinet of ministers, and laws are passed by an elected parliament. On paper, it is the people who, through their representatives, govern all countries in the world, including Cuba, North Korea, and China.

For example, Article 71 of the Constitution of Ukraine states that “elections to state authorities and local self-government bodies are free and conducted on the basis of universal, equal, and direct suffrage by secret ballot.”

Aristotle was exactly against this equality, and much later, Vilfredo Pareto, Gaetano Mosca, and many other supporters of elitism argued that people, by their nature, are very unequal both physically and intellectually. And, what is more important, there are significantly more weak and foolish individuals in any society than strong and intelligent ones. Thus, by endowing everyone with equal rights and giving a “green light” to decisions made by a simple majority, we inevitably create conditions for the degradation of the entire community. The most rational and strategically balanced decisions will be in the minority along with their authors, simply due to the inherent inequality of human abilities. To put it bluntly, fools will always shout louder than the wise.

To solve the problem, it is logical to do one of the following: a) eliminate fools altogether, b) deprive them of the right to vote, or c) make them believe they have the right to vote when in fact they do not. The Nazis tried to go down the first path but were stopped in time. The second path leads to massive revolutions, as seen in the example of the year 1917. Currently, all developed countries, led by the United States, are following the third path — creating an illusion of democracy where every citizen has equal voting rights, but the actual governance of the country is carried out regardless of the majority’s choice.

In other words, modern democracy is a qualitative spectacle performed by a minority for the majority in order to calm the latter and instill in them confidence in universal equality. Notice again, all this is done primarily in the interests of the majority.

Maybe there is a fourth way?

Есть.

And what if equal voting rights are replaced by unequal ones? What if each citizen will not have just one vote that they “drop” into the ballot box every few years, but several, depending on their value to society, contribution to its development, and authority? Suppose an economics professor will have 50 votes, while a hairdresser-stylist will have only two.

Of course, the first question that immediately arises is: who and how will decide how many votes each person has and why does a professor have more votes than a hairdresser? To address this, it would be possible to develop a transparent and relatively objective system for evaluating the contribution of each citizen to the social and economic life of the country. For example, the following indicators could be used to calculate an electoral rating:

  • Вылаченные налоги

  • Published scientific works

  • Дети и внуки

  • Convictions and offenses

  • Age and health condition

  • Subscribers on Instagram (don’t laugh!)

And so on. The list can go on. Each indicator can be easily verified and published in a decentralized data storage system, for example, based on Blockchain. Thus, citizens’ voices will be “calibrated” based on their authority in matters of state governance. It is even possible to assume the possibility of regular qualification testing for every citizen who wants to increase their rating. The higher the expected rating, the more professional and complex the questions will be, specifically in the fields of economy, law, or state history. By using decentralized data storage and processing technologies, it is possible to completely eliminate bias in evaluation and any form of corruption.

Such “calibrated” democracy will solve two problems at once. Firstly, it will bring to power those who rightfully deserve it and whose opinion we all should really rely on. Secondly, it will explain to everyone else what exactly they lack in order to also become those whose opinion the whole country listens to. At the same time, this will be done openly, objectively, and fairly, minimizing the eternal conflict between the rich and the poor, the smart and the foolish, the bottom and the top of any society.

Most likely, this model will not lead to a change of power. However, it will make the electoral formula significantly more understandable and fair. After all, it is these qualities that modern democracy lacks.
