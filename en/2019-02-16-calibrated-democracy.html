“Калиброванная демократия”

Democracy is the worst form of government. Power belongs to the crowd, which is incapable of strategic thinking and consists mostly of ignorant people making impulsive decisions. This is what Aristotle believed in the 2nd century BC. He considered a more effective system of government to be one in which power belongs to those who have demonstrably more authority to possess it, and therefore make more thoughtful and knowledgeable decisions.

Contrary to the thoughts of the Greek philosopher, democracy is currently the prevailing form of government in most countries around the world. Even in places where monarchy is maintained, such as in the United Kingdom or Spain, the actual governance of the country is carried out by the cabinet of ministers, and laws are passed by a parliament elected by the people. On paper, it is precisely the people, through their representatives, who govern all countries in the world, including Cuba, North Korea, and China.

For example, Article 71 of the Constitution of Ukraine states that “elections to state authorities and local self-government bodies are free and conducted on the basis of universal, equal, and direct suffrage by secret ballot.”

Aristotle was specifically opposed to this equality, and much later Vilfredo Pareto, Gaetano Mosca, and many other supporters of elitism argued that people are inherently unequal in many ways, both physically and intellectually. And, more importantly, there are significantly more weak and foolish individuals in any society than there are strong and intelligent ones. Thus, by endowing everyone with equal rights and giving “green light” to decisions made by the simple majority, we inevitably create conditions for the degradation of the entire community. The most rational and strategically balanced decisions will be in the minority along with their authors—simply due to the inherent inequality of human abilities. Roughly speaking, fools will always drown out the wise.

To solve the problem, it is logical to either a) eliminate fools altogether, b) deprive them of voting rights, or c) make them believe they have voting rights when in fact they do not. The Nazis attempted to take the first path, but were stopped in time. The second path leads to large-scale revolutions, as illustrated by the events of 1917. Currently, all developed countries, led by the United States, are following the third path—creating an illusion of democracy where every citizen has equal voting rights, but the actual governance of the country is carried out regardless of the majority’s choice.

In other words, modern democracy is a high-quality spectacle, staged by a minority for the majority, in order to calm the latter and instill in them confidence in universal equality. Note once again that all of this is done primarily in the interests of the majority.

Может быть есть четвертый путь?

Есть.

And what if equal voting rights were replaced with unequal ones? What if every citizen would have not just one vote, which they “cast” into the ballot box every few years, but several, depending on their value to society, their contribution to its development and authority. Let’s say an economics professor would have 50 votes, while a hairstylist would have only two.

Of course, the first question that immediately arises is: who and how will decide who has how many votes and why does a professor have more votes than a hairdresser? To answer this, it would be possible to develop a transparent and relatively objective system for assessing the contribution of each citizen to the social and economic life of the country. For example, the following indicators could be used to calculate the electoral rating:

Вылаченные налоги

Опубликованные научные труды

Дети и внуки

Судимости и правонарушения

Возраст и состояние здоровья

Подписчики в Instagram (не смейтесь!)

And so on. The list can go on. Each indicator can be easily verified and published in a decentralized data storage system, for example, based on Blockchain. Thus, the voices of citizens will be “calibrated” depending on their authority in matters of state governance. It is even possible to assume the possibility of regular qualification testing of each citizen who wants to increase their rating. The higher the expected rating, the more professional and complex the questions will be, specifically in the field of economy, law, or state history. By using decentralized data storage and processing technologies, it is possible to completely eliminate bias in assessment and any corruption.

Such “calibrated” democracy will solve two problems at once. Firstly, it will bring to power those who rightfully deserve it and whose opinion is truly worth relying on for all of us. Secondly, it will explain to everyone else what exactly they are lacking in order to also become those whose opinion the whole country listens to. At the same time, this will be done openly, objectively, and fairly, minimizing the eternal conflict between the rich and the poor, the smart and the foolish, the bottom and the top of any society.

Most likely, this model will not lead to a change in the people in power. However, it will make the electoral formula significantly more understandable and fair. After all, it is precisely these qualities that modern democracy lacks.
