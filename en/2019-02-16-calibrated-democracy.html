“Калиброванная демократия”

Democracy is the worst form of government. Power belongs to the crowd, which is unable to think strategically and is mostly made up of ignorant people who make impulsive decisions. This is what Aristotle believed in the 2nd century BC. He considered a more effective system of government to be one in which power is held by those who are known to have more authority, and therefore make more informed and competent decisions.

Contrary to the thoughts of the Greek philosopher, democracy is currently the prevailing form of government in most countries around the world. Even in countries where monarchy is preserved, such as the United Kingdom or Spain, the actual governance of the country is carried out by the cabinet of ministers, and laws are passed by an elected parliament. On paper, it is the people who govern all countries in the world, including Cuba, North Korea, and China, through their representatives.

For example, Article 71 of the Constitution of Ukraine states that “elections to the bodies of state power and local self-government are free and are carried out on the basis of universal, equal, and direct suffrage by secret ballot.”

Aristotle himself opposed this equality, as did later figures such as Vilfredo Pareto, Gaetano Mosca, and many other supporters of elitism, who believed that people are inherently unequal in many respects, both physically and intellectually. And, more importantly, there are significantly more weak and foolish people in any society than strong and intelligent ones. Thus, by endowing everyone with equal rights and giving a “green light” to decisions made by a simple majority, we inevitably create conditions for the degradation of the entire community. The most rational and strategically balanced decisions will be in the minority along with their authors, simply due to the inherent inequality of human abilities. In simple terms, fools will always outshout the wise.

To solve this problem, it is logical to either a) eliminate fools altogether, b) deprive them of the right to vote, or c) make them believe they have the right to vote, while in fact they do not. The Nazis tried to go down the first path, but they were stopped in time. The second path leads to massive revolutions, like the one in 1917. The third path is currently being pursued by all developed countries, led by the United States. It creates an illusion of democracy, where every citizen has equal voting rights, but the actual governance of the country is carried out regardless of the majority’s choice.

In other words, modern democracy is a quality spectacle staged by the minority for the majority, in order to appease the latter and instill in them a sense of universal equality. Notice once again, all of this is done primarily in the interests of the majority.

Perhaps there is a fourth path?

What if equal voting rights were replaced with unequal ones? What if each citizen had not just one vote that they “cast” in the ballot box every few years, but several votes depending on their value to society, contribution to its development, and authority? Let’s say an economics professor would have 50 votes, while a hair stylist would have only two.

Of course, the first question that arises immediately is: who and how would decide how many votes each person has and why does the professor have more votes than the hair stylist? To address this, it would be possible to develop a sufficiently transparent and somewhat objective system for evaluating the contribution of each citizen to the social and economic life of the country. For example, the following indicators could be suitable for calculating the electoral rating:

  • “Published scientific works”

  • Дети и внуки

  • “Criminal records and offenses”

  • Age and health status

  • Instagram subscribers (don’t laugh!)

And so on. The list can go on. Each indicator can be easily verified and published in a decentralized data storage system, for example, based on Blockchain. Thus, citizens’ voices will be “calibrated” depending on their authority in matters of state governance. It is even possible to assume the possibility of regular qualification testing for every citizen who wants to increase their rating. The higher the expected rating, the more professional and complex the questions will be, specifically in the areas of economy, law, or state history. By using decentralized data storage and processing technologies, it is possible to completely eliminate bias in evaluation and any form of corruption.

Such a “calibrated” democracy will solve two problems at once. Firstly, it will bring to power those who deserve it and whose opinions we should truly rely on. Secondly, it will explain to everyone else what exactly they are lacking in order to also become those whose opinions the whole country listens to. At the same time, this will be done openly, objectively, and fairly, minimizing the eternal conflict between the rich and the poor, the intelligent and the foolish, the bottom and the top of any society.

Most likely, this model will not lead to a change in the people in power. However, it will make the electoral formula much more understandable and fair. After all, these are the qualities that modern democracy lacks.
