“Четыре века якобы свободы”

For the past approximately 400 years, since Locke, we have been living under the flag of freedom, equality, and brotherhood. It is now difficult to imagine a reasonable person seriously denying the importance of personal freedom, the value of human life, the virtue of social equality, the preciousness of peace, friendship, and love. These concepts seem to be the foundation of our morality. They have always been with us and will remain forever. However, I propose to delve deeper and understand what freedom really is and whether it is for our benefit.

In his Two Treatises of Government, the English educator and philosopher John Locke formulated: “freedom is to follow my own will in all cases where the law does not prohibit it.” Armed with this obviously attractive formula and inspired by early humanists, liberal revolutionaries of all stripes have been changing the social order of European countries for all these years, and along with it, the consciousness of the inhabitants. As Professor Valery Kuvakin from MGU believes, “the Constitution of the Russian Federation is an expression of victorious liberalism-capitalism in terms of source and content,” and we are liberals with you!

Vladimir Putin at a meeting with schoolchildren emphasized: “freedom ends where the freedom of another person begins,” reiterating the idea of Mikhail Bakunin, one of the first theorists of anarchism and populism.

Либерализм — излишняя терпимость, вредная снисходительность, попустительство. Словарь

Thus, freedom is possible as long as two “free” individuals do not conflict with each other. “Is censorship permissible on the internet?” - school students asked the president. “Of course not,” - the president answered… and added that - “if it is calls for terrorism, then of course it is permissible.” Then a quote from Bakunin followed. In simpler terms, as long as your actions do not interfere with others, we are ready to give you freedom, but as soon as they contradict our expectations, your freedom will be restricted.

The key words here are “conflict” and “contradiction”. Where they are absent, there is freedom, and where they exist, freedom cannot be.

Who will control and punish those who encroach on the freedom of each of us? Roskomnadzor? Morality police? Public court? “Conscience!” - a prepared libertarian will tell you. Each of us must have a moral principle, as Immanuel Kant bequeathed, which will not allow us to encroach on someone else’s freedom. At first, of course, while we are building the society of the future, not everyone will have it. Therefore, we will still need a court and police, acting according to the motives of John Stuart Mill’s essay, recommending the use of violence against a person “only when he harms others”. Then, of course, sooner or later, people will learn to live without conflicts and contradictions, and will stop harming each other.

Is it really good when society consists of those who avoid conflicts, trying not to contradict the interests of others? Will we be able to progress constantly looking back at those whom our progress hinders? And in general, is progress possible without conflict, according to the opinion of the liberal Voltaire — the “basic law of nature”? Can a good teacher be tolerant of the mistakes of underachievers? Can a scientist create something new while fearing to offend colleagues? Can a new product be released while fearing to harm competitors? It sounds absurd.

However, it is precisely this that liberals (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism) lead us to under the slogans of freedom. It is precisely into a society without conflicts - national, domestic, economic, cultural, and political - into a society of freedom!

Certainly, none of this ultimately works out. In a society that has been raised for centuries in the spirit of “everyone can do whatever they want as long as they don’t interfere with their neighbor”, people are inherently disgusted by the idea of violence against them. They are born, as it seems to them, free, and therefore despise both the state and the authorities, inevitably introducing violence into their lives. As a result, three times as many police officers are needed for such citizens than if violence had been an integral part of their existence from early childhood, along with its derivatives: discipline, obedience, order, and control.

Violence in liberal societies is not just excessive, but also hideously hypocritical. Bright slogans about freedom and equality conceal “exploitation and alienation,” as correctly noted by Professor Boris Kashnikov. The cowardly “do no harm to your neighbor” raises weak, indifferent, tolerant, and compliant individuals — ones that are easy to exploit.

Everything would be different if a person did not fear causing harm to their neighbor, disturbing their “freedom,” and considered conflict as the norm - they would then calmly accept disciplined social order, respect subordination, consider any kind of work as the highest virtue, and of course, would not allow alienation and exploitation. This would be a person of a completely different quality: firm, uncompromising, irreconcilable, and consistent. They would consider obedience as the highest good. Of course, not to anyone, but to the “proletariat - the armed vanguard of all the exploited and working people,” as Lenin wrote.

It would be a different society and different people.

Those who for four centuries in a row instill the ideas of freedom in us are our enemies. We should not strive for freedom, but for order and discipline, as natural forms of natural and reasonable violence. We should not be afraid of violence, but respect and understand it. It is a part of our nature, and we need to face this fact head-on. Otherwise, others will do it for us, and we will remain slaves to our own tolerance.
