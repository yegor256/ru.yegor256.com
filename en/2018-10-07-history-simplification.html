“Ветер истории”

Joseph Stalin, according to Alexander Golovanov, once said: “I know that when I am gone, not a single wave of mud will be poured on my head. But I am confident that the winds of history will blow it all away.” The winds of history are us. And the wave of mud is our parents. This is how he saw the future in the difficult year of 1943. However, everything turned out to be the opposite: we have much more mud than he expected from his closest followers, and the winds of history are not yet heard.

A few days ago, I was talking to an old acquaintance of mine. He is an American who has been in Ukraine for some time. Having studied our history, apparently from books and blogs, he told me that Stalin killed 30 million Ukrainians, and Putin took Crimea away from Ukraine. In connection with this, he was wondering how it is possible that we tolerate all of this? Why don’t we take up arms and fight for our freedom? As an American with inherent love for freedom, he finds it incomprehensible.

I asked him how exactly Stalin managed to kill so many people if he had never been to Ukraine. I even joked, asking if he did it with the help of some super-accurate long-range rifle. After that, the conversation took a completely different turn, and I was accused of not sharing the grief and sadness for the innocent victims, and therefore there was nothing to talk to me about.

Я разделяю. И горе и печаль.

However, I ask myself, is it really necessary to simplify it so categorically in order to draw the right conclusions from negative historical experience? Is it necessary, in order to avoid repeating genocide, to declare one of its leaders a messenger of Satan, and all the victims - great martyrs? And won’t such simplification lead to the opposite result, namely to new senseless victims and new unjustified violence?

Убивал ли Сталин украинцев? Аннексировал ли Путин Крым? Сжигал ли Гитлер евреев? И взрывал ли Бен Ладен башни в Нью-Йорке?

Я не знаю. Есть разные мнения на этот счет.

However, I know for sure that the more convinced we are that we know for sure who the villain is and who the savior is, the higher our chances are of finding a new anti-hero. And very soon. The more actively we search for the guilty parties and the more eagerly we shift the blame onto them for what happened, the less responsibility we leave on ourselves.

Remaining unable to take responsibility, we inevitably become hostages to someone else’s will. And most likely, this will be directed against us. We are awaiting new genocides, famines, repressions, and wars if we do not stop simplifying history and demonizing its individual characters.

As Sergei Dovlatov said: “We constantly curse Comrade Stalin, and, of course, rightfully so. And yet I want to ask — who wrote four million denunciations?”

It was not Stalin who killed Ukrainians, it was the whole country, including the efforts of Ukrainians themselves. It was not Putin who annexed Crimea, but the people living in Crimea and Russia. It was not Hitler who killed Jews, but German, Polish, Italian, and Ukrainian soldiers and volunteers, among others.

Иными словами, это делали мы с вами. И наши предки.

For a better future, we must not be afraid to soberly look into the past and acknowledge our personal guilt in what has happened. We must strive not to simplify what has happened, but to see all the facets of past tragedies. We must try to become the very wind of history that the Commander-in-Chief relied on. Currently, we look more like a pile of mud.
