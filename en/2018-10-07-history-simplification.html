“Ветер истории”

Joseph Stalin, according to Alexander Golovanov’s words, once said: “I know that when I am gone, not a single bucket of dirt will be poured on my head. But I am confident that the winds of history will sweep it all away.” The winds of history are us. And the bucket of dirt is our parents. This is how he envisioned the future from the difficult year of 1943. However, everything turned out the opposite: we have much more dirt than he expected from his closest followers, and the winds of history have yet to be heard.

A few days ago, I was talking to my old acquaintance. He is an American who happened to be in Ukraine for some time. Having studied our history, apparently from books and blogs, he told me that Stalin killed 30 million Ukrainians, and Putin took Crimea away from Ukraine. In connection with this, he wondered why we tolerate all this? Why don’t we take up arms and fight for our freedom? As an American with inherent love for freedom, he doesn’t understand it.

I asked him how exactly Stalin managed to kill so many people if he had never been to Ukraine. I even joked whether he did it with some super-accurate long-range rifle. After that, the conversation took a completely different turn, and I was accused of not sharing the grief and sadness for the innocent killed, and therefore, there was nothing to talk to me about.

I feel the pain. And the sadness.

However, I ask myself, is it really necessary to so categorically simplify it in order to draw the right conclusions from negative historical experience? Is it necessary to declare one of its leaders as a messenger of Satan in order to avoid repeating genocide, and to consider all the victims as great martyrs? And won’t such simplification lead to the opposite result, namely to new senseless sacrifices and new unjustified violence?

Did Stalin kill Ukrainians? Did Putin annex Crimea? Did Hitler burn Jews? And did Bin Laden explode the towers in New York?

I don’t know. There are different opinions on this matter.

However, I know for sure that the more convinced we are that we know for sure who the villain is and who the savior is, the higher our chances of finding a new anti-hero. And very soon. The more actively we search for the guilty and the more willingly we blame them for what happened, the less responsibility we leave on ourselves.

Remaining unable to take responsibility, we inevitably become hostages to another’s will. And, most likely, this will be directed against us. We can expect new genocides, famines, repressions, and wars if we do not stop simplifying history and demonizing its individual characters.

As Sergey Dovlatov said: “We endlessly curse Comrade Stalin, and, of course, for a reason. Yet I want to ask — who wrote four million denunciations?”

It was not Stalin who killed Ukrainians, it was the entire country, including the efforts of Ukrainians themselves. It was not Putin who annexed Crimea, but the people living in Crimea and Russia. It was not Hitler who killed Jews, but German, Polish, Italian, and Ukrainian soldiers and volunteers, among others.

In other words, we did this together. And our ancestors.

For a better future, one should not be afraid to soberly look at the past and acknowledge their personal guilt in what has happened. Instead of seeking simplification of what happened, one should strive to see all aspects of past tragedies. One should try to become the very wind of history that the commander-in-chief relied on, while for now, we look more like a mudslide.
