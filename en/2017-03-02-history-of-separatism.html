This marked the beginning of the American colonies’ war for independence from Britain, with Colonel George Washington becoming its hero and later their first president. Almost two and a half centuries later, in March 2014, several thousand people stormed the Donetsk administration building and raised the Russian flag over it, which sparked the war in Donbass, now known as the war with Russia (according to Poroshenko) or the struggle for independence (according to Zakharchenko). What do these two events have in common?

There have been, are, and will be many wars and military conflicts on the planet. They differ in scale (62 participating states and over 60 million deaths in the Second World War compared to, for example, the Anglo-Zanzibar War, which lasted 38 minutes and claimed the lives of about 570 people), content (for example, a war can be liberation, civil, religious, or even have the nature of rebellion, uprising, or revolution), duration, intensity, and God knows what else. However, according to Massimo Morelli’s opinion, a professor of political science and economics at Columbia University, most conflicts are caused by problems of non-compliance with obligations.

If we are talking about wars for independence, then this unfulfilled obligation, apparently, is an unwritten but so obvious obligation not to come “into someone else’s garden with your own rules”. Thus, the uprisings of the Roman provinces hundreds of years ago in Gaul, Macedonia, or Italy, in their nature, were little different from the struggle of the Native Americans against the Americans in the 18th century or the Chechens against the Russian Federation in the 20th. In all cases, the owners of the “garden” were dissatisfied with someone else’s “rules”.

Apparently, this is human nature: we don’t like being controlled from the outside, even if it is done well. We are willing to tolerate it as long as there is a clear benefit (for example, states in the USA), or as long as we are being held by force (for example, republics in the USSR).

The factor of advantage is increasingly losing its relevance for various reasons, while the power component in recent history always leads to casualties and is almost never able to maintain control over the territory.
