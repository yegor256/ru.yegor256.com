“245 лет сепаратизма, от Вашингтона до Захарченко”

In June 1772, in Narragansett Bay (now Rhode Island, USA), a small group of smugglers attacked a government military ship, wounded the captain, disarmed the crew, looted the ship, and set it on fire. This marked the beginning of the American colonies’ war for independence from Britain, in which Colonel George Washington emerged as a hero and later became their first president. Almost two and a half centuries later, in March 2014, several thousand people stormed the Donetsk administration building and raised the Russian flag over it, which marked the beginning of the war in Donbass, now known as the war with Russia (according to Poroshenko’s version) or the struggle for independence (according to Zakharchenko’s version). What do these two events have in common?

There have been, are, and will be numerous wars and military conflicts on the planet. They differ in scale (62 participating states and over 60 million deaths in World War II, for example, compared to the Anglo-Zanzibar War, which lasted 38 minutes and claimed the lives of about 570 people), content (for example, a war can be liberation, civil, religious, or even rebellious, uprising, or revolutionary in nature), duration, intensity, and who knows what else. However, according to Massimo Morelli, a professor of political science and economics at Columbia University, most conflicts arise from problems of “unfulfilled commitments.”

If we are talking about wars for independence, this unfulfilled commitment seems to be an unwritten but obvious obligation not to “bring one’s own rules into someone else’s garden.” Thus, the Roman provincial uprisings centuries ago in Gaul, Macedonia, or Italy had little difference in nature from the Indian wars against Americans in the 18th century or the Chechen conflict with the Russian Federation in the 20th century. In all cases, the owners of the “garden” were dissatisfied with someone else’s “rules.”

Apparently, this is the nature of human beings: we do not like when we are controlled from the outside, even if it is done well. We are willing to endure as long as there is clear benefit (for example, the states in the USA) or until we are kept by force (for example, the republics in the USSR).

The factor of benefit is increasingly losing its relevance due to various reasons, while the use of force in recent history always leads to casualties and almost never manages to maintain control over the territory. From the most recent and still not fully healed, this is, of course, Kosovo, South Ossetia, or Transnistria. Counterexamples, in which territories are somehow maintained, can be Chechnya or Palestine.

Supporting the claim that in most cases, once on the path of separatism, the territory inevitably comes out from under the control of the “oppressors,” is the simple fact that in 1900 there were just over fifty countries on the planet. For example, India, Canada, Poland, Finland, Ukraine, or Australia did not exist at all, they were all part of other states or their colonies. Just over a hundred years have passed, and currently, there are 193 states in the United Nations.

Thus, regardless of our attitude towards it, separatism can be called a stable trend of modern times in changing the world map. There are objectively no reasons for its decline. The recent events in Great Britain and California are vivid confirmations of this.

And now let’s ask ourselves: in which group of territories will Donbas ultimately end up? Will Ukraine retain it by force and await the fate of Chechnya or Palestine, or will a new state like Kosovo finally be formed, with borders being established and weapons being laid down?
